# Projectibility Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer role:** Projectibility specialist
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections constitute a homeostatic property cluster sustained by diachronic mechanisms (semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation), and that the category's projectibility is distinctively pragmatic rather than truth-conditional.

---

## 2. Projectibility Assessment

### Does the outline frame the contribution as "here are mechanisms" or "the category is projectible and here's why"?

**Mixed, leaning toward "here are mechanisms."** The introduction states the contribution in three parts: (a) interjections as HPC case study, (b) pragmatic projectibility, (c) diachronic mechanisms as glue. The ordering itself is telling: the case study comes first, projectibility second, mechanisms third. In the body, the mechanisms section (Section 4) is 3--4 pages and labelled "the JHP centrepiece," while projectibility (Section 5) gets 1.5--2 pages and is labelled "the distinctive contribution." These labels contradict each other. If projectibility is truly the distinctive contribution, it should not be half the length of the mechanisms section. As currently structured, a reader will experience the paper as: "here is a cluster of properties; here are the mechanisms that hold them together; oh, and also the category is projectible in an interesting way." The rhetorical weight falls on mechanisms.

### Are mechanisms presented as *explaining* the projectibility, or as *being* the point?

**As being the point.** Section 4 is self-contained. It presents bleaching paths, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation, dynamic stability, and Dingemanse's liminal signs. Nothing in the section description connects these mechanisms forward to projectibility. The section closes with the Dingemanse point about liminality being functional, not with a bridge to "and this is why the category supports inductive generalisation." The mechanisms are presented as interesting in themselves. This is the central structural problem.

### Does the conclusion cash out in what the category lets you predict, explain, or inductively generalise?

**Partially.** The conclusion reads: "Interjections aren't marginal or anomalous. They're a homeostatic property cluster sustained by identifiable diachronic mechanisms and yielding primarily pragmatic projectibility." This is the right slogan, but it remains at the level of slogan. There is no specific prediction cashed out in the conclusion. What exactly does the pragmatic-projectibility claim let you *do* that you couldn't do before? The conclusion should contain at least one concrete example of an inference licensed by the category -- something of the form: "If a speaker encounters a novel form X and categorises it as an interjection, they can predict that X will be prosodically isolated, syntactically non-integrated, and used for emotive or interpersonal rather than propositional purposes." That kind of concrete cashing-out is absent.

### Boyd's slogan: "a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose." Is the third clause missing or decorative?

**It is present but decorative.** Section 5 lists what interjection-categorisation lets you infer (prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, emotive force, non-referentiality). But these are the same properties already presented in Section 3 as the property cluster. This creates a circularity problem: the "projectibility" section reads as "if you know something is an interjection, you can infer that it has the properties of interjections." That is trivially true of any category and is not Boyd's point.

Boyd's point is that projectibility is *for a purpose* -- it supports inductive inference in some domain of inquiry or practice. The "purpose" for interjection projectibility needs to be specified. Candidates from the Wikipedia article include:

- **Interactional prediction:** If X is an interjection, a conversational analyst can predict its distribution in turn-taking (backchanneling, sequence-initial position, response tokens). Stivers (2019) is already in the source list and directly supports this.
- **Diachronic prediction:** If X is an interjection, a historical linguist can predict that its etymology will show bleaching from a content word, and that its phonological form may have diverged from the source (Gehweiler's four-stage model).
- **Lexicographic prediction:** If X is an interjection, a lexicographer can predict that it will resist standard part-of-speech classification, will lack inflectional paradigms, and will require pragmatic rather than denotational glossing.
- **Acquisition/processing prediction:** If X is an interjection, a psycholinguist can predict that it will be prosodically salient, acquired early, and processed differently from propositional content.

None of these purposes appear in the outline. The section says projectibility is "primarily pragmatic," but this needs to be cashed out as specific inferential purposes, not just a domain label.

### The outline places projectibility as the "climax" of the HPC section. Is this placement sufficient?

**No. The placement creates two problems.** First, projectibility comes after 8--9 pages of property-cluster and mechanism discussion. By the time the reader reaches it, the rhetorical climax has already passed (the mechanisms section is the most detailed and is explicitly labelled "the JHP centrepiece"). Second, 1.5--2 pages is insufficient to develop the "primarily pragmatic" claim, compare it with morphosyntactic projectibility for other categories, and argue that HPC kinds can be "pragmatic kinds." That is three distinct argumentative moves in under two pages.

**Recommendation:** Projectibility should be introduced in the HPC theory section (Section 2) as the *reason HPC matters* -- not just as a concept to be defined, but as the criterion of success for the rest of the paper. The reader should know from page 3 that the paper succeeds or fails on whether interjections are shown to be projectible for a specific purpose. Then Section 5 becomes the payoff of a promise made early, not a late addition.

Alternatively, projectibility could be woven through Sections 3 and 4 rather than siloed. Each property-cluster feature and each mechanism could be evaluated not just for "does it co-occur?" but for "does it support inductive generalisation?" This would make projectibility the organising question of the paper rather than its penultimate section.

### Is the "primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional" claim adequately developed?

**No.** The outline states this claim but does not develop it. Specific questions that need answering:

1. **What does "pragmatic projectibility" mean formally?** Is it projectibility about how forms will be *used* (speech-act type, sequential position, prosodic realisation) rather than about what they will *denote*? The outline needs to define this.

2. **What specific predictions does interjection-categorisation license?** The outline lists four inferences (prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, emotive force, non-referentiality), but these are descriptive properties, not predictions. A prediction has the form: "If X is categorised as an interjection, then in context C, outcome O is expected." For example: "If *bruh* is categorised as an interjection, we predict it will appear turn-initially, carry its own intonation contour, resist embedding under *that*-complementation, and express speaker stance rather than propositional content." The Wikipedia article provides ample material for such predictions (the backchanneling function, the greeting function, the filler-boundary diagnostics from O'Connell & Kowal).

3. **How does pragmatic projectibility differ from semantic projectibility?** The outline says "compare with projectibility for nouns (referential, inflects for number, takes determiners) or verbs." But noun projectibility includes both morphosyntactic predictions (takes determiners, inflects) and semantic ones (referential). Is pragmatic projectibility a *different kind* of projectibility, or just projectibility in a different domain? This distinction matters for the paper's theoretical contribution.

4. **Is "pragmatic kind" a new theoretical category?** The outline claims "HPC kinds can be 'pragmatic kinds.'" This is a significant theoretical claim that needs argumentation, not just assertion. What makes a kind "pragmatic"? Are there other pragmatic kinds? Does this connect to the distinction between content and procedural meaning (Traugott & Dasher, already cited in the mechanisms section)?

## 3. Strengths

- **The empirical base is unusually strong.** The Wikipedia Good Article provides a comprehensive, well-sourced survey of interjection properties across all levels of linguistic analysis. This means the property-cluster section can be empirically rich without requiring new data collection.

- **The diachronic focus is well-matched to the venue.** Gehweiler (2008) was published in JHP, and the bleaching-path analysis provides exactly the kind of mechanism-tracing that historical pragmatics values. The *nay*-to-*yeah* shift and GloWbE variation data add corpus-based substance.

- **The "marginal categories as strongest HPC evidence" argument is genuinely novel.** Most HPC applications in linguistics pick central categories (countability, definiteness). Arguing that the category everyone dismisses is actually the best test case is a clever inversion that could reframe the field.

---

## 4. Weaknesses

- **Projectibility is structurally subordinate to mechanisms.** Despite being labelled "the distinctive contribution," it receives half the page space of mechanisms and comes late in the paper. The outline needs to restructure so that projectibility is the organising question from Section 2 onward, not a climactic reveal in Section 5. **Action:** Either (a) double the length of Section 5 and add concrete predictions, or (b) integrate projectibility into Sections 3 and 4 so that each property and mechanism is evaluated for its contribution to inductive inference, or (c) reframe Section 2 so that projectibility is stated as the success criterion and everything after is evaluated against it.

- **The projectibility section is circular as currently described.** It says: "If X is an interjection, you can infer prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, emotive force, non-referentiality." But these are the cluster properties from Section 3. Projectibility must go beyond restating the cluster. It must specify *for whom* and *for what purpose* these inferences matter. The Wikipedia article suggests at least three concrete purposes (interactional analysis, historical reconstruction, lexicographic practice) that the outline does not exploit. **Action:** Specify at least two concrete inferential purposes with worked examples showing how categorising a form as an interjection generates non-trivial predictions.

- **The "pragmatic kind" claim is asserted but not argued.** Saying "not all kinds project in the same domain" is a significant extension of HPC theory. It needs at least a paragraph of argumentation: What does it mean for a kind to be "pragmatic"? Does this connect to procedural vs. conceptual meaning? Are there degrees of pragmatic vs. truth-conditional projectibility, or is it a binary? Without this, the paper's most distinctive theoretical contribution remains a promissory note. **Action:** Add a subsection or extended paragraph in Section 5 that defines "pragmatic kind," situates it relative to existing typologies of meaning, and explains why interjections are the paradigm case.

---

## 5. Key question I would ask at Q&A

"You argue that interjection projectibility is primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional. Can you give me a concrete example of a prediction that this categorisation licenses which would be *surprising* -- that is, not simply a restatement of the cluster properties you've already listed? For instance, if I encounter a novel form and tentatively categorise it as an interjection, what non-obvious inference does that categorisation support?"

---

## 6. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit.**

The outline has a strong empirical base, a good venue fit, and a genuinely novel inversion (marginal = strongest test case). But it currently reads as a mechanisms paper with a projectibility coda, not a projectibility paper with mechanisms as supporting evidence. The "primarily pragmatic" claim is the paper's most distinctive contribution, but it is underdeveloped, placed late, and risks circularity. Restructuring to make projectibility the organising question -- not the climactic reveal -- would transform this from a good HPC case study into a paper that advances HPC theory itself.
