## 1. How "Stabilizers" Are Defined (vs. Boyd's "Homeostatic Mechanisms")

Reynolds draws a terminological distinction between **mechanism** and **stabilizer**, attributing the functional-role emphasis to Illari & Williamson (2012):

> "'Mechanism' emphasizes causal structure, 'stabilizer' emphasizes the functional role — what keeps the cluster clustered. A stabilizer is a mechanism insofar as it has causal depth, but the term foregrounds maintenance rather than mere causation." (line 29)

The **definition of "mechanism"** is given explicitly:

> "a mechanism posit earns its keep by specifying component processes whose interactions produce the target phenomenon. If you can't say what sub-processes combine to yield the clustering and what would happen if one were disrupted, you don't yet have a mechanism — you have a label with causal ambitions." (line 27)

The **perturbation test** for stabilizer status:

> "Something counts as a stabilizer if removing it would change the clustering — if the category would fragment, drift, or dissolve." (line 29)

Crucially, stabilizers maintain **not just clustering of form properties, but the coupling between form and value** — between a unit's distributional signature and its contribution when deployed. A mere correlate (e.g., orthographic length of a word that happens to be a noun) is explicitly excluded.

The key move beyond Boyd: Reynolds shifts from Boyd's relatively abstract "homeostatic mechanisms" to a framework that demands **causal decomposability** and **perturbation sensitivity**. The stabilizer concept also connects to what Reynolds calls "meta-Occam" — the inventory of stabilizers is small; the categories they maintain are many.

## 2. Recruitment Mechanisms vs. Maintenance Mechanisms

This distinction is not drawn with those exact labels in chapter 6, but the chapter implicitly distinguishes:

- **Recruitment/origination:** How a form enters the system. For quotatives, this is the diachronic pathway — `be like` originating in first-person internal-state reports (Ferrara & Bell 1995), `tte` as a reduction of `to itte`, etc. The chapter notes: "Grammaticalization gives the etiological story of how a form like *be like* developed — how meanings bleach, how forms reduce, how pragmatic inferences harden into grammatical meaning." (line 603)

- **Maintenance/stabilization:** What keeps the resulting cluster coherent *now*. "The framework asks the constitutive question: what keeps the resulting cluster coherent now? The pathways are historical; the stability is mechanistic. Both are needed. Diachrony without synchronic maintenance leaves present-day coherence unexplained; maintenance without diachrony leaves the trajectory unexplained." (line 603)

The five quotative stabilizers described are all **maintenance** mechanisms:
1. Processing economy (light forms win under production pressure)
2. Expressive fit (filling a functional niche older forms leave empty)
3. Acquisition and cohort effects (forms acquired in dense peer networks persist)
4. Social indexing (forms do identity work — marking solidarity, casualness)
5. Transmission dynamics (how local preference becomes population change)

The chapter does address how some mechanisms play dual roles. For example, processing economy both **recruits** forms (light forms are preferentially adopted) and **maintains** them (repeated processing makes future processing easier, creating a feedback loop).

## 3. Synchronic Feedback Loops vs. Diachronic Pathways

This is a major theme. The chapter explicitly distinguishes timescales using a **2x2 typology** (Figure "mechanism-typology", lines 58-105):

| | Individual | Community |
|---|---|---|
| **Fast (ms-minutes)** | Processing economy, Predictive facilitation | Accommodation, Alignment in dialogue |
| **Slow (months-generations)** | Acquisition, Entrenchment | Transmission dynamics, Social indexing |

**Synchronic feedback loops** are discussed extensively:

> "Predictability encourages uptake; uptake feeds entrenchment; entrenchment makes the form more predictable. Meanwhile, social indexing strengthens both the processing advantage and the identity signal." (line 258)

> "light forms spread because they're easy; spreading makes them easier still" (line 184)

The **reciprocal maintenance** between category and mechanism is treated as central:

> "Categories shape what gets entrenched, and entrenchment shapes which categories survive." (line 372)

The chapter includes a causal-loop diagram (Figure "causal-loop", lines 374-427) showing bidirectional arrows between the quotative category and all four stabilizing mechanisms, with reinforcing feedback loops marked.

**Diachronic pathways** are handled through the quotative case study:
- Butters (1982) first attestation → Ferrara & Bell (1995) first-person/internal-dialogue hypothesis → Tagliamonte & D'Arcy (2004, 2007) apparent-time and real-time data showing S-curve diffusion
- Three-phase trajectory: innovation (youth-marked) → spread → consolidation (neutralized)
- A figure (lines 194-256) schematizes the lag between frequency diffusion and social-meaning consolidation

The chapter explicitly addresses the relationship: "For readers familiar with grammaticalization theory: yes, diachrony remains essential... The pathways are historical; the stability is mechanistic. Both are needed." (line 603)

## 4. How Slater's Critique Is Handled (Mechanisms vs. Stable Clustering)

Slater's name does not appear in this chapter. However, the chapter addresses what appears to be the same conceptual challenge — the question of whether mechanisms are doing real explanatory work or just redescribing stable correlations.

The chapter's response is multi-pronged:

**(a) Perturbation tests:** The "What if a mechanism were absent?" section (lines 264-303) provides counterfactual reasoning showing that no single mechanism suffices. Each mechanism's removal produces distinct predicted consequences, compiled in a perturbation grid (Table "perturbation-grid"). This addresses the concern that mechanisms are just labels for stable clustering by showing they make *different* predictions when disrupted.

**(b) Operational tests for mechanism reality** (section 6, lines 513-538): Six ordered tests:
1. Intervention and ablation (strongest)
2. Cross-construction transfer
3. Predictive constraints under licensing conditions
4. Base-rate checks
5. Reciprocal maintenance signatures
6. Distributional and attestation coherence (weakest)

Each test includes a "what could mislead us" caveat.

**(c) The "particle" contrast case** (line 429): The wastebasket category "particle" is shown to fail the maintenance audit — no common acquisition pathway, no processing signature, no transmission dynamic. "The label exists; the mechanism-maintained category doesn't." This demonstrates that the framework can distinguish genuine HPC kinds from mere labels, which is the core of the Slater-type challenge.

**(d) Interventionist realism** as a core commitment (line 570): "Kinds are real to the extent that tracking or manipulating them changes expectations. This is stronger than description: it says that category distinctions track causal structure, not just impose order on data."

**(e) Anti-foundationalist response to regress** (lines 436-442): When mechanisms can themselves be analyzed as stabilized profiles, the chapter offers two responses:
- Interventionist: stop when you reach mechanisms whose disruption would change the clustering in predictable ways
- Anti-foundationalist: explanatory chains don't need bedrock; what matters is mutual support and empirical fruitfulness

## 5. Specific Linguistic Examples of Stabilizers/Mechanisms

### Case Study 1: Quotative markers (primary case)

**English `be like`:**
- First attested 1982 (Butters); spread from first-person internal dialogue to general quotative
- Tagliamonte & D'Arcy (2004): speakers aged 18-27 used it for 13% of quotative tokens in 1995; speakers aged 17-19 used it for 63% by 2003
- Panel data (2007): same speakers carried their inventory from 13% to 31% as they aged into their thirties — real-time change, not age-grading
- Five stabilizers braided together: processing economy, expressive fit, acquisition/cohort effects, social indexing, transmission dynamics

**German `und ich so`:**
- Golato (2000): documented in video-recorded conversations, same enactment function as English `be like`
- Attested in youth speech by late 1990s (Androutsopoulos 1998)
- More register-restricted than English counterpart

**Japanese `tte`** (reduced from `to itte`): phonologically light quotative particle
**Turkish `diye`** (converb of `demek`): expanded into colloquial discourse contexts
**Russian `tipa`** (taxonomic noun "type"): Kolyaseva 2018

Cross-linguistic convergence explained as convergent maintenance under similar functional pressures, not media-driven borrowing (Buchstaller 2014).

### Case Study 2: Independent relative `whose` (secondary case)

- Hankamer & Postal (1973) claimed English lacks independent relative `whose`
- Reynolds (2024) documents attestations spanning seven centuries
- Extremely rare (~once per 100 million words) but attested in edited sources
- Three stabilizers: filler-gap processing (shared with all relatives/interrogatives/clefts), information-structural licensing (contrastive parallelism, deictic anchoring, structural integration), discourse ecology (academic/journalistic prose provides licensing contexts)
- Deliberately chosen to contrast with quotatives: cognitive mechanisms dominate rather than social indexing

### Other examples mentioned:

- **Nouns:** distributional profile (determiners, number inflection, subject/object position, entity reference, adjective modification) — none strictly necessary (`cattle` lacks singular, names resist determiners, `information` doesn't pluralize)
- **`fast`:** dual-access adjective/adverb — mechanism-first account treats it as entrenched in both clusters with token-level activation shaped by syntactic frame
- **Polish aspect:** 90% of verbs strongly prefer one aspect (deep in a single basin); remaining verbs occupy shallower regions
- **`particle`:** wastebasket category that fails the maintenance audit — no shared mechanism
- **Irregular morphology:** `went` resists levelling to `goed` because high frequency keeps the form accessible (Bybee 2006)
- **Additional mechanisms mentioned but not given full case studies:** analogy, standardization, indexicality, error and repair, creativity

## 6. The Relationship Between Stabilizers and Projectibility

This is treated as the central payoff of the chapter, with a dedicated section (lines 541-554) and pervasive throughout.

**Key claim:** "The stabilizing story explains why projectibility comes in degrees." (line 545)

Three degrees:

1. **Strongly projectible:** Category with a deep basin — maintained by multiple aligned stabilizers all pulling in the same direction. "Generalizations about nouns are therefore more likely to extend reliably to new nouns." (line 547)

2. **Weakly projectible at the margins:** Category whose basin overlaps with others, or whose stabilizers pull in different directions. "Predictions work for typical cases but become less reliable at the edges. The degree of projectibility tracks the degree of mechanistic support." (line 549)

3. **No projectibility:** "a label with no shared stabilizing mechanisms behind it" — predictions trained on one subtype don't generalize to others (the `particle` case).

**The operational content:** "how strongly do predictions transfer across novel instances, contexts, and populations?" (line 553)

**Quotative-specific projectibility:** "This functional specialization illustrates projectibility: within this niche, predictions about content type, register, and speaker characteristics are remarkably reliable." (line 175)

**Mechanisms produce projectibility, not just persistence:** "Mechanisms help explain why categories hold together strongly enough to support projection." (line 620) The chapter ends by distinguishing stability from reality: "A stable fiction is still a fiction; a well-maintained convention is still a convention. What distinguishes a genuine kind from a persistent label?" — the answer being projectibility, developed in the next chapter.

## 7. Other Notable Features

### Theoretical commitments (section "What this commits us to")

Three **core** commitments:
1. **Process ontology** — categories are dynamically sustained patterns, "standing waves, not carved statues" (line 568)
2. **Interventionist realism** — kinds are real to the extent that tracking or manipulating them changes expectations
3. **Reciprocal maintenance** — mechanisms and categories co-construct each other

Four **derived** commitments:
4. Population-distributed competence (not idealized speakers)
5. Variation as signal (not noise)
6. Cross-level coherence
7. Operational testability

### The "refactoring" posture

> "The sociolinguists already had the patterns. The cognitivists already had the mechanisms. The typologists already had the parallels. What was missing was a way of treating those results as parts of one explanatory structure." (line 601)

### Mereological decomposition of mechanisms

Transmission is decomposed into: production → signal → perception → memory update → social interaction. "When we say 'transmission maintains the category,' we're compressing a multi-part process into a single term." (line 356)

### Recursive application

Some mechanisms can themselves be studied as stabilized profiles: processing economy bundles reduced forms, high frequency, and resistance to analogical pressure; social indexing bundles youth association, in-group marking, and register restriction. (line 364)

### Neural evidence

Fedorenko et al. (2024): language network is dissociable from Multiple Demand network. This matters because it "undercuts any simple attempt to treat linguistic category maintenance as a by-product of general reasoning pressure. At minimum, the stabilizing pressures look language-specific." (line 109)

### Definiteness thread

The chapter ends with a "definitenessthread" environment predicting that definiteness should be stabilized at more than one scale, yielding "two coupled HPCs rather than one" — setting up a later chapter.
