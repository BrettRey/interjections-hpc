# Review (Round 2): English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer role:** Philosopher of science specialising in natural kinds theory, HPC theory, and scientific realism.
**Venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Round:** 2 (revised outline)

## 1. Round 1 concerns addressed?

### Concern 1: Mechanisms look like recruitment pathways, not maintenance mechanisms.

**Addressed.** This was my most serious objection, and the revision tackles it head-on. Section 4 is now split into 4.1 (recruitment pathways, explicitly labelled as diachronic entry mechanisms) and 4.2 (synchronic feedback loops, explicitly labelled as maintenance). Four named feedback loops are provided:

1. Prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration (bidirectional reinforcement)
2. Non-referentiality cascading to loss of argument structure and then syntactic independence (one-directional but self-sustaining)
3. Onomatopoeia feeding phonological atypicality feeding prosodic isolation (recruitment-as-maintenance)
4. Social indexing preserving forms via identity work

The first two are genuine feedback loops in something close to Boyd's sense. The third cleverly straddles the recruitment/maintenance distinction: the mechanism that brings new items in also maintains the cluster's phonological signature. The fourth (social indexing) is plausible but would need more elaboration in the paper itself to show that it sustains property co-occurrence rather than merely preserving individual lexical items.

The perturbation test is applied with specific predictions (neutralise prosodic isolation: the form reclassifies as verb/adverb; restore referential content: the form re-enters the noun category). This is exactly the kind of operationalisation I asked for. The *pop* example is effective.

**Verdict: Substantially addressed.** The theoretical architecture is now sound. Minor concern remains about feedback loop 4 (social indexing), which may be doing double duty as a stabiliser of the cluster and as a preserver of individual items.

### Concern 2: Slater's critique not engaged.

**Partially addressed.** The revision does not mention Slater by name in the outline body, but the structural changes render the concern less urgent. The paper now has genuine synchronic feedback loops (not just recruitment pathways), so it no longer needs to retreat to Slater's weaker position. The "stabiliser" terminology from Reynolds (2026, Ch 6) may implicitly engage with the mechanisms-vs-no-mechanisms debate, but I cannot verify this without reading Reynolds (2026).

However, the paper still lists Slater (2015) and Magnus, Onishi & Serpico (2021) as key sources. A reader familiar with the HPC literature will expect the paper to position itself relative to these views. Does the author endorse Boyd's mechanism requirement? The revised outline seems to: it does the work of identifying genuine mechanisms, which suggests a Boydian commitment. But this should be explicit, even if brief. One sentence in Section 2.1 would suffice: "We follow Boyd in requiring that the cluster be sustained by identifiable mechanisms, not merely stable" (or, alternatively, "We adopt the stabiliser framework of Reynolds 2026, which requires that proposed mechanisms pass a perturbation test").

**Verdict: Partially addressed.** The structural problem is solved (genuine mechanisms are now identified), but the dialectical positioning relative to Slater remains implicit rather than explicit.

### Concern 3: Projectibility risks circularity.

**Addressed.** The revision breaks circularity convincingly via three moves:

(a) **Partial-observation inference** (Section 5.1): the *bruh* example is effective. Observing prosodic isolation and stance meaning in a novel form licenses prediction of non-inflection, resistance to syntactic integration, resistance to restrictive modification, and bleaching etymology. These are non-trivial predictions that go beyond restating the observed properties. The predictions succeed because the maintaining mechanisms couple the properties. This is precisely the structure I asked for.

(b) **Field-relative projectibility** (Section 5.2): three fields (interactional, diachronic, lexicographic) get different inferential payoffs from the same category label. This is a strong operationalisation that shows projectibility is not a single monolithic claim but a family of domain-specific inferences.

(c) **Category vs. function** (Section 5.3): the distinction between the function label "supplement" and the category label "interjection" is well drawn. The category label adds non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, and diachronic trajectory predictions. The function label provides none of these. This answers the CGEL-internal question cleanly.

**Verdict: Fully addressed.** The circularity worry is resolved. The *bruh* example should survive into the paper.

### Concern 4: Only one bleaching path analysed.

**Addressed.** Section 4.1 now analyses two trajectories: religious noun to interjection (Gehweiler 2008) and verb to interjection (*damn*, CGEL analysis). The entry mechanisms differ (phonological modification vs. syntactic deactivation), which demonstrates multiple pathways into the same cluster. The addition of *come on* and *look* as imperative-to-interjection paths further enriches the picture. This is what HPC predicts: multiple entry points converging on the same attractor basin.

The four-stage model for Path 1 now includes my suggestion that each stage makes the next more probable (constrained trajectory), and the author asks whether Gehweiler's corpus data can identify a phase transition (P3). This is a productive research question for the paper.

**Verdict: Fully addressed.**

### Concern 5: HPC vs. prototype comparison undeveloped.

**Addressed.** Section 2.2 now provides a substantive comparison. Prototype theory captures gradience but not mechanisms. HPC generates mechanism-derived predictions. Four specific predictions (P1 asymmetric fraying, P3 phase transition, P5 conditioning recovers structure, P10 hub-node asymmetry) are stated in Section 6.1 with named defeat conditions. The perturbation test is offered as a discriminator: HPC makes the claim falsifiable; prototype theory offers no analogous test.

This is a genuine improvement. The defeat conditions are particularly welcome. The paper is now committed to specific empirical claims that could fail, which is the mark of a serious theoretical contribution.

**Verdict: Fully addressed.**

### Additional suggestion 1: Foreground dynamic stability.

**Addressed.** Dynamic stability now has its own subsection (4.3) rather than being buried. The *nay* to *yeah* shift, regional variation, and free coinage are presented as evidence that the category is an attractor. The connection to Dingemanse (2020) is made more carefully: HPC provides the framework for understanding the "landscape of possible resources" in terms of property-space attractor basins maintained by specific mechanisms.

### Additional suggestion 2: Reframe Gehweiler's stages as mechanism trajectory.

**Addressed.** Section 4.1 now explicitly frames the four stages as progressively installing cluster properties (non-referentiality at stage 2, phonological divergence at stage 3, full prosodic independence at stage 4) and describes the trajectory as constrained, not random.

## 2. One-sentence summary

The revised outline argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster, maintained by synchronic feedback loops (prosodic-syntactic coupling, non-referentiality cascades, onomatopoeia-driven phonological atypicality, social indexing) and diachronic recruitment pathways (semantic bleaching, pragmatic conventionalisation), and that the category's distinctive contribution to HPC theory is its primarily pragmatic -- rather than truth-conditional -- projectibility.

## 3. Strengths

- **The architecture now has the right shape.** The organising question (what does categorising something as an interjection let you predict?) is introduced in Section 1, operationalised in Section 2, threaded through Section 3 (each property evaluated for inferential contribution), and paid off in Section 5. This is not just a rhetorical improvement; it changes the logical structure. The paper is no longer "here is a cluster, here are some mechanisms, and by the way this is projectible." It is: "here is a question about inference; the cluster and mechanisms are the means by which we answer it." The criterion of success stated in Section 2 ("the paper succeeds if it shows that categorising a novel form as an interjection licences non-trivial inferences for specific purposes") is admirably clear and holds the paper to a specific standard.

- **The perturbation test does real work.** The specific perturbation predictions (neutralise prosodic isolation and the form reclassifies; restore referential content and the form re-enters the noun category) are testable and well-motivated. The *pop* example is effective because it is a real-world case where the perturbation actually occurred: *pop* was reclassified by lexicographers precisely because it was syntactically integrated. This is not a thought experiment; it is an observed mechanism failure leading to observed recategorisation. If the paper delivers on this in full, it will be one of the clearest applications of the perturbation test in the HPC-in-linguistics literature.

- **The "thin-but-genuine HPC" framing is strategically wise.** Rather than claiming that interjections are a robust natural kind on a par with nouns or verbs, the paper positions them as a thin-but-genuine HPC, comparable to adjectives (citing Reynolds 2026, Ch 11). This is honest about the mechanism density while maintaining the core theoretical claim. It also creates space for a typology of word classes by mechanism density, which is a productive research programme.

## 4. Weaknesses

- **Feedback loop 2 (non-referentiality cascade) may not be genuinely homeostatic.** The outline describes this as "a one-directional cascade" (non-referentiality causes loss of argument structure causes syntactic independence) and then notes that "once argument structure is lost, there is no pressure to restore it." This is correct, but it describes a ratchet (an irreversible state transition), not a feedback loop. A ratchet explains why items do not leave the cluster; it does not explain why the properties continue to co-occur in a mutually reinforcing way. Boyd's homeostasis requires active maintenance, not merely the absence of pressure to change. The paper should be transparent about which of its stabilisers are genuine feedback loops (loop 1: prosodic-syntactic coupling) and which are ratchets (loop 2: the non-referentiality cascade). Both contribute to cluster stability, but they contribute differently, and being explicit about the distinction would strengthen the theoretical precision. The Reynolds (2026) "stabiliser" terminology may already accommodate this distinction (I have not read the source), but the paper should make it visible to the reader.

- **The four framework predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10) are stated but not tested.** The outline presents these predictions with defeat conditions, which is commendable. But the paper appears to state them as future research directions rather than delivering results. For a journal article (as opposed to a programmatic chapter), at least one prediction should be tested with actual data. P5 (conditioning recovers structure) seems the most tractable: the outline already mentions GloWbE data and regional variation. Can the paper show that conditioning on region decomposes the apparent gradience at the interjection boundary? If so, that would transform the HPC-vs-prototype comparison from a promissory note into an empirical result. If the corpus data does not exist or is inconclusive, the paper should say so explicitly rather than leaving all four predictions as exercises for the reader.

- **The Slater/Magnus positioning remains implicit.** As noted above: the paper does the work of identifying genuine mechanisms, which implicitly endorses Boyd's mechanism requirement. But a reader coming from the philosophy-of-science side will want to see this commitment stated and briefly defended. The paper cites Slater (2015) and Magnus, Onishi & Serpico (2021) as key sources; it should say in one or two sentences why it follows Boyd rather than Slater, or alternatively why the "stabiliser" framework of Reynolds (2026) renders the dispute moot. This is a minor point, but it is the difference between a paper that is philosophically informed and one that is philosophically rigorous.

---

## 5. Key question

"You distinguish four synchronic feedback loops, but you acknowledge that loop 2 (the non-referentiality cascade) is one-directional, not bidirectional. In Boyd's framework, homeostasis implies active countervailing forces that restore the system to equilibrium when perturbed -- not merely the absence of pressure to change. Is a ratchet sufficient for homeostasis? If so, are you weakening Boyd's requirement, and how does your position then differ from Slater's stable-property-cluster view, which dispenses with mechanisms altogether?"

---

## 6. Verdict

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The revision has addressed all five Round 1 concerns, most of them fully and convincingly. The architecture now has the right logical structure (projectibility as organising question, not afterthought). The mechanisms section distinguishes recruitment from maintenance and provides genuine synchronic feedback loops with a perturbation test. The circularity in projectibility is broken via partial-observation inference, field-relative projectibility, and the category-vs-function distinction. The HPC-vs-prototype comparison is substantive and falsifiable.

Three minor issues remain: (1) the non-referentiality cascade is a ratchet, not a feedback loop, and the paper should be transparent about this; (2) at least one of the four framework predictions should be tested with data rather than left as a promissory note; (3) the paper should explicitly state its position in the Boyd-vs-Slater debate. None of these require structural changes. They can be addressed in drafting. This outline is ready to become a paper.
