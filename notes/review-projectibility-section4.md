# Projectibility Review: Section 4 (Stabilisers: recruitment and maintenance)

**Reviewer role:** Projectibility specialist
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Scope:** Section 4 only (lines 257--440 of main.tex), read against Section 3 and the Round 2 outline review

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 4 presents mechanisms as *explanations for why the cluster holds together* rather than as ends in themselves, and it connects forward to projectibility at several key points, but it does so unevenly -- the recruitment subsections (4.1) are more diachronically absorbed than projectibility-oriented, while the maintenance and perturbation subsections (4.2, 4.4) do the projectibility-linking work well.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The opening paragraph (lines 258--264) frames the section correctly.** It states that a cluster "isn't yet an explanation," distinguishes recruitment from maintenance, and invokes Boyd's requirement that pathways be historical and stability be mechanistic. This framing positions the mechanisms as answering a question raised by Section 3 (why do these properties co-occur?), not as free-standing diachronic narrative. The frame is right.

- **The perturbation test (4.4, lines 393--414) is the section's strongest projectibility moment.** It shows that removing a stabiliser produces *specific, distinct* category shifts (*pop* reclassified as adverb when prosodic isolation is neutralised; *God* reverting to noun when referential content is restored). This is not mechanism description; it is mechanism-derived prediction. If the stabilisers are genuine, then neutralising prosodic isolation should produce adverb-like behaviour, not noun-like behaviour. The asymmetry of the consequences is the signature of genuine mechanisms, and the section says so explicitly (lines 411--414). This is exactly the kind of evidence that makes Section 5's projectibility argument credible.

- **The "convergent pathways" subsection (4.1.3, lines 335--352) explicitly connects to the HPC framework.** The sentence "the convergence is what HPC predicts: different mechanisms can recruit items into the same kind, producing the same cluster of co-occurring properties through different routes" (lines 340--342) is the right kind of forward-linking. It says: the fact that noun-to-interjection and verb-to-interjection pathways converge on the same property profile is itself a prediction of HPC, not merely a historical observation. This earns its place.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **Path 1 (4.1.1, lines 271--313) runs 43 lines of diachronic detail with only one sentence connecting the trajectory to projectibility.** The connection is the aside about P3 (phase transition in genesis, lines 298--305), which links the constrained trajectory to a testable HPC prediction. But the rest of the subsection -- the four-stage model, the Invited Inferencing vs. reanalysis distinction, the seventeenth/eighteenth-century clustering of phonological modifications -- reads as JHP-oriented diachronic narrative. The danger flagged in the Round 2 review has partially materialised: this is the subsection most likely to read as "the JHP centrepiece" that overshadows projectibility. **Actionable fix:** Add a closing sentence to 4.1.1 that explicitly connects the trajectory to the inferential payoff. Something like: "The constrained trajectory matters because it predicts that partial bleaching (stage 2) should be detectable via the *absence* of downstream properties (phonological divergence, full prosodic independence) -- a prediction that goes beyond historical description." This makes the mechanism a generator of predictions, not just a narrative.

- **Path 2 (4.1.2, lines 315--333) has no forward connection to projectibility at all.** It describes syntactic deactivation as a second entry mechanism and contrasts *damn* (partial deactivation) with *come on*/*look* (full deactivation). The contrast is informative, but the subsection never asks: what does this tell us about what we can *infer* from knowing something is an interjection? The degree-of-deactivation correlation (line 333) is the natural hook: if degree of deactivation correlates with degree of cluster integration, then observing a form's position on the deactivation continuum should let you predict its cluster profile. This is projectibility. **Actionable fix:** Add 2--3 sentences at the end of 4.1.2 connecting deactivation degree to inferential consequences. For instance: "This correlation generates a graded prediction: a form that retains complement-taking ability (*damn these mosquitoes!*) should also retain other verb-like properties (e.g., prosodic integration in some constructions), while a fully deactivated form (*come on*) should show the complete interjection profile. The mechanism doesn't just describe entry; it predicts the inferential package available at each stage."

- **The dynamic stability subsection (4.5, lines 416--447) is more descriptive than mechanism-linking.** The COHA data on *nay*/*yeah* turnover and the GloWbE regional variation are good empirical points, but they are presented as *evidence that the cluster persists* rather than as *evidence that the stabilisers generate predictions*. The onomatopoeia paragraph (lines 433--440) comes closest to the right framing ("a new form doesn't need to undergo bleaching or syntactic deactivation to exhibit the full property cluster; it enters directly, pulled into the basin by the stabilisers"), but it still describes an outcome rather than deriving a prediction. **Actionable fix:** Make the prediction explicit. If the stabilisers constitute an attractor basin, then a *newly coined* interjection should immediately exhibit the full cluster profile *without needing to traverse any of the recruitment pathways*. This is a testable prediction (test it against recent coinages like *bruh*, *yeet*, *oof*). Currently the paragraph gestures at this but does not commit to the prediction or name the test cases.

---

## 4. Key question about projectibility handling

**Do the recruitment pathways (4.1) explain why the inferences from Section 3 succeed, or do they merely document how items arrive?**

As currently drafted, 4.1 leans toward the latter. The pathways are described in detail, and the convergence subsection (4.1.3) connects them to HPC's prediction of multiple entry routes. But the connection to projectibility is indirect: you have to infer that constrained trajectories imply predictable property profiles, and that predictable property profiles underwrite inferences. The maintenance subsection (4.2) and perturbation test (4.4) make this connection much more directly. The imbalance matters because a reader skimming Section 4 will encounter 60+ lines of diachronic detail (Paths 1 and 2) before reaching the material that explicitly explains why the inferences work. The risk is that Section 4 reads as "here is the history" rather than "here is why the predictions succeed," especially for a reader who arrives from Section 3's "inferential contribution" paragraphs expecting the mechanisms to cash out those inferential promissory notes.

---

## 5. Verdict

**Needs minor revision.** The section's overall architecture is sound -- recruitment, maintenance, perturbation, dynamic stability -- and the framing paragraph, perturbation test, and convergent-pathways subsection all connect mechanisms to projectibility correctly. But the two recruitment-pathway subsections (4.1.1 and 4.1.2) and the dynamic-stability subsection (4.5) present mechanisms as diachronic narrative without closing the loop to inferential payoff. Three targeted additions (closing sentences connecting each subsection's mechanism to a specific prediction or inferential consequence) would fix this without requiring structural reorganisation. The section does not currently risk overshadowing projectibility at the structural level -- the framing is correct, and Section 5 exists to carry the projectibility argument -- but at the subsection level, the recruitment pathways are more absorbed in their own diachronic stories than the paper's organising question warrants.

### Specific revisions needed

1. **4.1.1 (Path 1):** Add 2--3 closing sentences connecting the constrained trajectory to a specific inferential prediction (e.g., partial bleaching at stage 2 should be detectable via absence of downstream properties).
2. **4.1.2 (Path 2):** Add 2--3 closing sentences connecting degree of syntactic deactivation to the inferential package available at each stage.
3. **4.5 (Dynamic stability):** Make the attractor-basin prediction explicit and name test cases (recent coinages like *bruh*, *yeet*, *oof*).
4. **Optional:** Consider whether the Invited Inferencing vs. reanalysis distinction (lines 307--313) earns its place in a projectibility-oriented paper, or whether it belongs in a footnote. It is interesting diachronic pragmatics, but it does not connect to projectibility and adds 7 lines to the longest subsection.
