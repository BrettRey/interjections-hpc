# Review: Complete Draft -- English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer role:** Philosopher of science specialising in natural kinds theory, HPC theory, and scientific realism.
**Venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Round:** Full-paper review (complete draft)

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster -- maintained by both diachronic recruitment pathways and synchronic stabilisers, with a distinctive form of projectibility that is pragmatic rather than truth-conditional -- and that the field-relative decomposition into three subscripted HPCs (syntactic, semantic, interactional) operationalises Boyd's disciplinary relativism in a way Boyd himself did not develop.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The perturbation test does genuine philosophical work, not merely illustrative work.** The two perturbation cases (lines 592-611) produce distinct, asymmetric consequences: neutralising prosodic isolation leads to adverb-like reclassification (*pop*), while restoring referential content leads to noun-like reclassification (*God*). The distinctness of these consequences is the signature that the proposed stabilisers pick out different causal contributions to the cluster rather than redescribing a single stable pattern. This addresses the core Slater-type challenge (that HPC mechanisms may be epiphenomenal labels rather than causal factors) through empirical demonstration rather than dialectical manoeuvre. The paper could not have made this move with only one perturbation case; having two with different signatures is philosophically decisive. This is among the cleanest applications of perturbation reasoning I have seen in the HPC-in-linguistics literature.

- **The field-relative projectibility architecture (Sections 2.3 and 5.2) is the paper's most original contribution to HPC theory.** Boyd (1999: 150) noted that naturalness is discipline-relative and offered the jade example (natural kind in gemology but not in geology). Reynolds (2025, Ch 7) developed this for the noun/name distinction. This paper goes further: it provides a notation (subscripts) and a mechanism-based explanation for both the overlap and the non-overlap of three distinct HPCs that share a traditional label. The extension differences are concrete, not stipulated: attributive *damn* is interjection_sem but not interjection_syn; *mm-hm* is interjection_int but debatably interjection_syn. And these differences are explained by the mechanism architecture (structural analogy maintains interjection_syn but not interjection_int; conversational routines maintain interjection_int but not interjection_syn). This is a genuine advance over Boyd's programmatic remark.

- **The paper holds itself to an explicit criterion of success (lines 114-117) and meets it.** The criterion -- "the paper succeeds if it shows that categorising a novel form as an interjection licences non-trivial inferences for specific purposes" -- is stated in Section 2 and discharged in Section 5 via the *bruh* example (partial-observation inference), the three-HPC architecture (field-relative projectibility), and the category-vs-function argument (what "interjection" adds beyond "supplement"). Stating and meeting a falsification-style criterion is rare in linguistics papers and philosophically disciplined.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **Boyd vs. Slater is not positioned.** This was flagged in the Round 2 outline review and remains unaddressed in the complete draft. The paper cites Boyd (1999) repeatedly and adopts the stabiliser/perturbation-test apparatus from Reynolds (2025). But it never states where it stands in the Boyd-Slater debate. Slater (2015) argued that stable property clusters suffice for natural-kind status without requiring homeostatic mechanisms; Magnus, Onishi & Serpico (2021) survey the resulting landscape. The paper's outline lists both Slater and Magnus et al. as key sources, but neither appears in the drafted text. This is a significant lacuna for any reader coming from philosophy of science. The paper implicitly endorses Boyd's mechanism requirement (it does the work of identifying mechanisms and applying the perturbation test), but this endorsement should be explicit. Two sentences in Section 2.1 would suffice, e.g.: "We follow Boyd in requiring that the cluster be sustained by identifiable mechanisms, contra Slater (2015), who argues that stable clustering suffices. The perturbation test (below) operationalises this requirement: a proposed kind must be maintained, not merely stable."  Alternatively, the paper could position itself relative to Reynolds's stabiliser framework and explain how that framework addresses the Slater challenge. Either way, the reader needs to know which theoretical commitment is in play.

  **Action required:** Add explicit Boyd-vs-Slater positioning in Section 2.1. Two to four sentences. This is a small addition with large philosophical payoff.

- **The "pragmatic kind" extension (Section 5.4) oscillates between a conservative claim and a strong one without settling.** The core issue, identified in the section-level review, persists in the full draft. Lines 821-828 claim that interjections are a "pragmatic kind" whose "strongest inferences are about how they will be used, not what they will denote." Lines 843-854 then argue that the domain of projectibility follows from the maintaining mechanisms -- pragmatic mechanisms produce pragmatic projectibility, just as morphosyntactic mechanisms produce morphosyntactic projectibility. This is the conservative reading: pragmatic kinds are just kinds whose mechanisms happen to operate in pragmatic domains. Boyd's framework is already domain-general and does not restrict projectibility to truth-conditional inference. If so, the paper is applying HPC to a new domain, which is valuable, but it is not extending the theory itself. Yet line 995-996 claims to "extend HPC theory to a kind whose projectibility is pragmatic rather than truth-conditional," which implies a genuine theoretical extension. The paper needs to choose: is this a new application of existing HPC theory (conservative), or does it require modifying the theory (strong)? If conservative, tone down "extends" language. If strong, explain what standard HPC theory cannot already accommodate about pragmatic projectibility.

  Additionally, the relationship between Section 5.2 (three distinct HPCs) and Section 5.4 (pragmatic kind) remains underspecified. Section 5.2 argues that interjection_syn, interjection_sem, and interjection_int are three distinct HPCs. Section 5.4 claims that "the richest projectibility belongs to interjection_int -- the interactional cluster" (lines 826-827) and then calls the forms at the intersection of all three HPCs "a pragmatic kind." But if interjection_int is a separate HPC from interjection_syn (as 5.2 claims), then the pragmatic richness belongs to interjection_int, not to "interjections" as such. The paper slides between treating interjection as one kind with multiple projectibility domains (5.4) and treating it as three kinds with overlapping extensions (5.2). A reconciling sentence is needed, e.g.: "The traditional label 'interjection' names the intersection of three HPCs, and it is this intersection -- not any single HPC alone -- that constitutes the pragmatic kind."

  **Action required:** (a) Disambiguate conservative vs. strong reading of "pragmatic kind." (b) Add a reconciling sentence between the three-HPC architecture (5.2) and the pragmatic-kind claim (5.4).

- **The framework predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10) in Section 6.1 are stated with defeat conditions but none is rigorously tested.** For a programmatic chapter in a book, promissory predictions are acceptable. For a journal article claiming that HPC adds something prototype theory cannot, at least one prediction should be delivered as an empirical result, not just a research programme. The paper already has the data for a partial test of P1 (asymmetric fraying): the *damn* case shows tight diagnostics (subject, tense) eroding before loose ones (complement frame), and *come on* and *look* show complete erosion. This is stated (lines 884-890) but framed as "suggestive" rather than tested. Similarly, P3 (phase transition) is supported by Gehweiler's data on the clustering of phonological modifications in the 17th-18th centuries (lines 896-904), but the paper hedges with "finer-grained historical data would be needed." P5 (conditioning recovers structure) is explicitly deferred: "hasn't been tested in this paper" (line 911). P10 (hub-node asymmetry) gets "preliminary evidence" from the perturbation test (lines 918-923).

  The pattern is: four predictions, zero fully tested, two with partial evidence, one with suggestive evidence, one entirely deferred. This weakens the HPC-vs-prototype comparison, which is presented as the paper's main empirical leverage. At minimum, the paper should commit more firmly to the evidence it already has for P1 and P3, even if P5 and P10 remain promissory. The hedging is appropriate for a cautious author, but it undercuts the claim that HPC adds empirical predictions that prototype theory cannot generate.

  **Action required:** For P1 and P3, present the existing evidence as genuine (if preliminary) results rather than suggestions. For P5 and P10, the deferral is honest and acceptable, but flag them explicitly as predictions that future corpus work could test. The goal is to show that the predictions are not merely possible but are already partially confirmed.

## 4. Key question

The paper's most important unresolved philosophical tension is between Section 5.2 and Section 5.4. Here is the question:

You argue in Section 5.2 that interjection_syn, interjection_sem, and interjection_int are "not three descriptions of the same category" but "three HPCs with overlapping but non-identical extensions, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes" (lines 756-758). This is a strong claim: there is no single HPC that *is* "the interjection category." But Section 5.4 then calls the forms at the intersection of all three HPCs "a pragmatic kind" (line 828), and the Conclusion treats "interjection" as a single entity that "extends HPC theory" (line 995). Which is it? If three HPCs, the paper's strongest contribution is showing that a single traditional category fragments into multiple natural kinds under HPC analysis -- a genuinely novel result. If one kind with multiple projectibility domains, the contribution is showing that Boyd's disciplinary relativism can be operationalised with subscript notation -- useful but less novel. The answer determines the paper's philosophical weight, and it is currently ambiguous.

## 5. Additional observations

### On the non-referentiality ratchet (Section 4.2.2)

The drafted text (lines 564-570) correctly labels this as "a ratchet rather than a cycle" and argues that it "contributes to cluster stability just the same -- it explains why items that enter the interjection cluster don't drift back toward their source category." This is an improvement over the outline (which called all stabilisers "feedback loops"). The honesty is commendable. But the paper should note that a ratchet is a weaker form of stabilisation than a feedback loop. A feedback loop actively restores the system to equilibrium when perturbed (Boyd's homeostasis in the strict sense); a ratchet prevents backward drift but does not actively maintain forward stability. The distinction matters because it affects the "homeostatic" in HPC: if the primary stabilisers are ratchets rather than loops, the "homeostatic" metaphor is doing less work than it appears to. The prosodic-syntactic coupling (Section 4.2.1) is the paper's strongest genuine feedback loop; the non-referentiality ratchet and social indexing are contributing mechanisms of different causal topology. Being explicit about this hierarchy would strengthen the paper's philosophical precision.

### On the prosodic-syntactic coupling (Section 4.2.1)

The text (lines 547-559) claims bidirectional reinforcement but provides asymmetric evidence. The Gehweiler citation supports the prosody-to-syntax direction; the reverse (syntax licensing prosody) is stated as plausible but unevidenced. The revision has improved this: the text now says "the evidence is stronger in one direction" (line 549) and "primarily prosody-driven, with syntactic reinforcement" (lines 558-559). This is honest. But "primarily prosody-driven" is a different claim from "bidirectional reinforcement." The section heading calls them reinforcing; the text downgrades them. Consider aligning the framing to match the evidence.

### On the Potts/Kaplan engagement (Section 5.2)

The discussion of interjection_sem (lines 722-740) treats Potts's four expressive properties as a cluster sustained by the bleaching mechanism. This is the right move for an HPC reading. But the text does not address a bridging question: Potts's expressives framework is intensional (a semantic type in a multi-dimensional logic), while HPC requires an extensional property cluster. The paper should note, even briefly, that it is treating Potts's type as picking out a cluster whose co-occurrence is explained by mechanism, not by definition. The key evidence for the HPC reading over the definitional reading is that items can have some of Potts's properties without others -- and the paper provides this evidence: *damn* in *the damn car* is perspective-dependent and use-conditional but arguably displaceable in some reported-speech contexts (lines 733-736). This point should be made more explicit as evidence for graded membership in interjection_sem, which is the HPC signature.

## 6. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit.**

The paper is philosophically serious, well-architected, and makes a genuine contribution to HPC theory through the field-relative projectibility apparatus and the perturbation test. It is close to publishable. But three issues prevent acceptance in the current form:

1. **Boyd vs. Slater is not engaged.** For a paper that deploys HPC theory as its central framework, failing to position itself relative to the major internal debate about that framework is a significant omission. This is a small addition (2-4 sentences) with large consequences for philosophical credibility.

2. **The "pragmatic kind" claim is philosophically underdetermined.** The paper oscillates between a conservative reading (new application of existing theory) and a strong reading (genuine theoretical extension) without settling. It also has an unresolved tension between the three-HPC architecture (Section 5.2) and the single-kind framing (Section 5.4 and Conclusion). These are not structural problems; they are ambiguities that paragraph-level revision can resolve.

3. **The framework predictions need more commitment.** The paper states four predictions with defeat conditions (commendable) but treats all four as promissory. At least P1 and P3 can be presented as partially confirmed by the evidence the paper already contains. Moving from "suggestive" to "preliminary but genuine evidence" would transform the HPC-vs-prototype comparison from a programmatic promise into an empirical argument.

None of these issues require restructuring. All three are addressable through targeted paragraph-level revisions. The paper's architecture is sound, its philosophical ambitions are well-placed, and its best contributions (the perturbation test, the subscript notation, the partial-observation inference) are already in publishable form. The revisions are about sharpening precision and owning the philosophical commitments the paper has already implicitly made.
