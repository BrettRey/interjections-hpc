# Hostile Reviewer (R2) -- Section 5 Review

**Reviewer 2** | Journal of Historical Pragmatics | Section-level review
**Section:** 5 ("Projectibility: what the category lets you predict"), lines 501--662

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 5 is the strongest stretch of the paper so far, delivering a non-circular projectibility argument and a genuinely interesting field-relative decomposition, but it oversells the novelty of its predictions and lets the subscript notation do rhetorical work that hasn't been earned by evidence.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The partial-observation inference argument (5.1) is sound and well-executed.** The *bruh* worked example does exactly what I demanded in previous rounds: it starts from two observed properties (prosodic isolation, stance meaning) and derives five predictions about unobserved properties (non-inflection, embedding resistance, modification resistance, turn positioning, etymological trajectory). The predicted properties are genuinely distinct from the observed ones, and the argument correctly traces each inference to a named mechanism from Section 4 (the non-referentiality ratchet, the prosodic-syntactic coupling). This is Boyd's projectibility applied with discipline, not just invoked. Credit where due.

- **The category-vs.-function analysis (5.3) is the most persuasive subsection in the paper.** The question "what does 'interjection' add beyond 'supplement'?" is sharp, and the five-item answer (non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, diachronic trajectory) is concrete. This is where HPC actually does work that neither prototype theory nor simple functional analysis can replicate: it explains why the category label licences a richer inferential package than the function label. The diachronic-trajectory point is especially strong for JHP -- knowing something is a supplement tells you nothing about its history; knowing it's an interjection licenses predictions about bleaching paths and phonological reduction. This is the kind of observation that makes the framework non-decorative.

- **The "pragmatic kind" argument (5.4) is interesting, though underdeveloped.** The observation that different HPC kinds project in different domains (nouns project morphosyntactically; interjections project pragmatically) is a genuine extension of the theory. The parallel with animacy (grammatical projection) and colour (ecological projection) is suggestive. If worked out more fully, this could be the paper's most distinctive theoretical contribution.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The predictions in 5.1 are unevenly non-trivial.** The *bruh* example lists five predictions. Two are genuinely non-obvious: (e) predicting the etymological source from synchronic behaviour is impressive, and (d) predicting turn-initial positioning from prosodic isolation involves a real cross-domain leap. But (a) "it won't inflect" is close to vacuous -- English has no productive morphology for interjections, so this "prediction" is really just saying "it will continue to be a zero-inflecting form in a language where most words outside nouns and verbs don't inflect either." Prediction (b) ("it will resist syntactic integration") is barely distinguishable from restating the observed prosodic isolation -- prosodic isolation *is* syntactic non-integration, viewed from the phonological side. And (c) ("it will resist restrictive modification") follows from non-referentiality by something close to definitional entailment. The paper presents five predictions as if they were equally impressive. They are not. I raised this exact issue in Round 2 ("the paper should be careful to distinguish mechanistically grounded inferences from quasi-definitional ones"), and the full draft has not addressed it. The fix is simple: triage the predictions into tiers. Tier 1: genuinely cross-domain (etymological trajectory, interactional distribution). Tier 2: mechanistically grounded but within-domain (embedding resistance). Tier 3: near-entailments (non-inflection, modification resistance). Present all five, but be honest about which ones are doing heavy lifting.

- **The subscript notation (5.2) adds complexity without sufficient empirical grounding.** Three subscripted HPCs are introduced: interjection_syn, interjection_sem, interjection_int. The claimed payoff is that they have "overlapping but non-identical extensions." But the evidence for non-identical extensions is thin: two examples. (1) *Damn* in *the damn car* is interjection_sem but not interjection_syn -- but this is just the well-known expressive-adjective analysis from Potts (2007), re-labelled. What does the HPC subscript add to Potts's own account? Potts already has the semantic predictions (use-conditionality, perspective dependence, nondisplaceability, descriptive ineffability). The subscript seems to re-package an existing analysis in HPC vocabulary without generating new predictions. (2) *Mm-hm* is interjection_int but debatably interjection_syn -- but the "debate" is gestured at via a single citation (O'Connell & Kowal 2005) without resolution. If the paper cannot show that the subscript notation generates at least one prediction that the existing literature (Potts for semantics, Stivers for interaction, CGEL for syntax) does not already make within its own framework, the notation is parasitic, not productive. The field-relative projectibility idea is genuinely interesting (as I acknowledged in Round 2), but it needs to earn its formalism. My suggestion: take one concrete case where the three subscripts make *conflicting* predictions and show that the conflict is real and illuminating. The *damn* case almost does this (interjection_sem predicts expressive semantics even in syntactically integrated position; interjection_syn predicts supplement function; the two diverge for attributive *damn*). But the paper treats this as a demonstration of non-identical extension rather than extracting the predictive conflict.

- **The "pragmatic kind" claim (5.4) is asserted but not defended against obvious objections.** The paper claims that interjections are a "pragmatic kind" whose projectibility is primarily in the pragmatic domain, parallel to how animacy is a "grammatical kind." But this invites a devastating objection: if the richest projectibility is pragmatic and the syntactic HPC (interjection_syn) is admitted to be "thin," then why not say that interjection is not a syntactic category at all but a pragmatic one? Many functionalists would say exactly this: interjection isn't a word class; it's a discourse function. The paper needs to rebut this objection explicitly. The tools are right there in 5.3 (the category-vs.-function argument), but the connection isn't drawn. If interjection is a pragmatic kind, what stops a reviewer from concluding that it shouldn't appear in a syntax-oriented grammar at all? The paper should address this, because reviewers at JHP will include people who think exactly this.

---

## 4. Key question

**The subscript notation distinguishes interjection_syn, interjection_sem, and interjection_int as three HPCs with overlapping extensions. But existing frameworks already carve this space: CGEL handles the syntax, Potts handles the expressives, Stivers and Dingemanse handle the interactional tokens. What does the HPC umbrella provide that a researcher couldn't get by simply citing those three frameworks and noting that they overlap? In other words: is the subscript notation a genuine theoretical contribution, or is it a notational reformulation of the fact that different subfields study different aspects of the same words?**

---

## 5. Verdict

**Needs revision** -- but the revisions are targeted, not structural.

The section's architecture is right. The partial-observation argument works. The category-vs.-function analysis is strong. The pragmatic-kind observation is novel. But three things need fixing before this section can carry the weight the paper places on it:

1. **Triage the predictions in 5.1.** Distinguish genuinely cross-domain inferences (etymological trajectory, interactional distribution) from near-entailments (non-inflection, modification resistance). Present all of them, but grade them honestly. As it stands, the section implies all five predictions are equally non-trivial, and a careful reader will notice they are not.

2. **Make the subscript notation earn its keep in 5.2.** Either (a) show that the three-HPC decomposition generates at least one prediction that the existing frameworks (CGEL, Potts, Stivers) do not individually generate, or (b) reframe the notation as a unifying analysis rather than a novel predictive tool. Option (a) is stronger. The predictive conflict between interjection_syn and interjection_sem for attributive *damn* is the most promising candidate -- work it out explicitly.

3. **Defend the "pragmatic kind" claim in 5.4 against the functionalist objection.** If interjection's richest projectibility is pragmatic, the paper must explain why it should still appear in grammars as a syntactic category. The answer is presumably that interjection_syn, however thin, is still a genuine HPC with its own stabilisers -- but this needs to be stated and defended, not left implicit.

If these three fixes are made, Section 5 will do what the paper needs it to do: demonstrate that HPC is not decorative vocabulary but a framework that generates inferential payoffs unavailable to prototype theory, functional analysis, or the existing field-specific literatures taken separately. Without them, a sceptical reviewer can still read the section as an elaborate relabelling exercise.
