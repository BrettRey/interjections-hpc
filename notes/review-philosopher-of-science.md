# Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer role:** Philosopher of science specialising in natural kinds theory, HPC theory, and scientific realism.
**Venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections, far from being a marginal or anomalous lexical category, constitute a homeostatic property cluster (HPC) kind sustained by diachronic mechanisms (semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation) and yielding primarily pragmatic -- rather than truth-conditional -- projectibility.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The choice of test case is genuinely interesting for HPC theory.** Interjections have been marginalised or denied category status by major grammars, which means they are precisely the kind of case that should be hardest for HPC to handle. If HPC works here, it demonstrates real explanatory power at the margins. This is the right argumentative strategy: go to the hard case, not the easy one. The author understands that the strength of a framework is tested by its most recalcitrant instances, not its most cooperative ones.

- **The property cluster is well-documented and honestly hedged.** The outline and Wikipedia source present co-occurring properties across phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics, and the author is careful to note that each property holds statistically, not necessarily ("commonly," "typically," "usually," "generally"). This is faithful to Boyd's original formulation: the properties are reliably co-instantiated, not exceptionlessly so. The systematic attention to boundary cases (interjections vs. nouns, verbs, adverbs, fillers, routine formulae) further demonstrates that the author grasps the HPC prediction that boundaries should be graded, not sharp.

- **The "pragmatic kinds" extension is a genuine theoretical contribution.** The claim that interjection projectibility is primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional is novel within the HPC-in-linguistics literature. If properly developed, this observation enriches the theory: it suggests that the domain of projectibility varies across kinds, which is an insight that could apply beyond linguistics (e.g., to social kinds, aesthetic kinds, or other categories where the inferential payoff is not in the domain of natural-scientific prediction).

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The mechanisms may not be homeostatic in Boyd's sense.** This is the most serious concern. Boyd's "homeostatic mechanisms" are processes that actively sustain the co-occurrence of properties in a cluster -- they explain why the properties hang together, not merely how individual items enter the category. The outline lists semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, and pragmatic conventionalisation. But as currently framed, these look like *recruitment pathways* (how nouns or verbs become interjections) rather than *maintenance mechanisms* (why, once something is an interjection, it tends to exhibit the full cluster of properties). Consider: semantic bleaching explains how *Jesus* enters the interjection category, but what keeps *Jesus* prosodically isolated, non-inflecting, and emotive once it gets there? The paper needs a clear account of the feedback loops or constraints that sustain co-occurrence. The prosodic-isolation-reinforcing-syntactic-independence point (Section 4, bullet 2) gestures at a genuine feedback loop, but it is the only one that does. The others are diachronic entry mechanisms, not synchronic maintenance mechanisms. Boyd's account requires the latter. The paper should either (a) develop genuine synchronic feedback loops (e.g., non-referentiality entails loss of argument structure, which entails syntactic non-integration, which reinforces prosodic isolation -- a mutual-reinforcement chain), or (b) explicitly argue that diachronic entry mechanisms are sufficient for HPC kindhood, which would require engaging with the literature on whether mechanisms are necessary at all (see next point).

- **Slater's critique is not addressed.** The outline lists Slater (2015) as a key source but does not indicate how the paper engages with his argument. Slater argues that homeostatic mechanisms are not necessary for natural kindhood -- what matters is the stable property cluster itself, not the causal story behind it. This is directly relevant. If the author's "mechanisms" turn out to be recruitment pathways rather than maintenance mechanisms (as I suspect), Slater's position could actually rescue the argument: the cluster is stable enough to ground induction regardless of whether we can identify Boyd-style homeostatic mechanisms. But the author needs to address this explicitly. Ignoring Slater while citing him is a gap that any reviewer familiar with the HPC literature will notice. Additionally, Magnus (2012) offers a further alternative (the "Stable Property Cluster" view) that would be relevant here. The outline lists Magnus, Onishi & Serpico (2021) -- the paper should clarify which strand of the mechanisms-vs-no-mechanisms debate it endorses.

- **Projectibility needs sharper articulation.** The outline says categorising something as an interjection lets you infer pragmatic properties: prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, emotive force, non-referentiality. But these are just the properties of the cluster itself. Projectibility in Boyd's sense is about *inductive inference beyond the observed instances* -- it is the capacity of the kind to support lawlike generalisations and predictions about new, unobserved members. The paper needs to show that encountering a novel item and recognising it as an interjection (e.g., a newly coined *oof* or a borrowed *yaar*) lets you infer properties you have not yet observed in that particular item. The current formulation risks circularity: "if X has cluster properties, you can infer it has cluster properties." The fix is to emphasise that partial observation of some cluster properties licenses inference to others -- that is, if a novel item is prosodically isolated and emotive, you can predict it will be non-inflecting and syntactically supplemental. This is what makes the cluster projectible. The paper should make this inferential structure explicit.

---

## 4. Key question I would ask at Q&A

"You identify semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, and pragmatic conventionalisation as the homeostatic mechanisms sustaining the interjection cluster. But these seem to explain how items *enter* the category, not why the properties *co-occur* in stable fashion once an item is there. Can you articulate the feedback loop? Specifically: what prevents an item from acquiring some cluster properties but not others? What makes the cluster 'sticky'? And if you cannot identify such a feedback loop, are you committed to Boyd's mechanism requirement, or would you accept Slater's weaker position that stable clustering suffices for kindhood without homeostatic mechanisms?"

---

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.**

The test case is well chosen, the empirical material is rich and honestly presented, and the "pragmatic kinds" extension is a genuine contribution. But the paper's central theoretical claim -- that interjections are an HPC kind -- depends on correctly identifying homeostatic mechanisms, and the current mechanisms look more like diachronic recruitment pathways than synchronic maintenance processes. The paper must either develop genuine feedback loops or explicitly engage with Slater's critique and argue that mechanisms are not necessary. The projectibility section also needs sharpening to avoid circularity. These are addressable problems, not fatal ones. A revised version that tackles them would be a strong contribution to both the HPC literature and to historical pragmatics.

---

## Additional notes for the author

1. The dynamic stability point (Section 4, bullet 4) -- where individual members enter and leave but the cluster persists -- is actually the strongest evidence for homeostasis, and it is currently buried. Consider foregrounding it. The *nay*-to-*yeah* shift and the free coinage via onomatopoeia demonstrate that the category is an attractor in something like a dynamical-systems sense: perturbations (loss of old members, arrival of new ones) do not destroy the cluster. This is closer to what Boyd means by "homeostatic."

2. The comparison with prototype theory (Section 6) is welcome but should be sharper. Prototype theory captures gradience but not mechanisms. HPC captures both. But if your mechanisms turn out to be weak, the advantage over prototype theory narrows. Make sure the mechanisms do real explanatory work that prototypes cannot.

3. Dingemanse's "liminal signs" framing is suggestive, but be cautious about claiming that HPC provides the "formal framework" for it. HPC is a metaphysical theory about natural kinds; Dingemanse is doing interactional linguistics. The connection is real but indirect. Overstating it will invite pushback from both camps.

4. The Gehweiler (2008) material on bleaching paths is excellent and well-suited to JHP. Consider whether the four-stage model (proper name > exclamatory use > phonological modification > primary interjection) can be reframed as a *mechanism trajectory* that progressively installs more cluster properties. This would help bridge the gap between recruitment pathways and maintenance mechanisms: the trajectory is not random but constrained, with each stage making the next more probable. That constraint is arguably homeostatic.
