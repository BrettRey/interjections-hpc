# Review: "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster"

**Reviewer 2** | Journal of Historical Pragmatics

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections, long marginalised or denied category status, constitute a homeostatic property cluster (HPC) kind sustained by diachronic mechanisms (semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation), with a distinctive contribution being that the projectibility of interjection membership is primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The empirical base is genuinely excellent.** The Wikipedia Good Article provides a well-sourced, multi-level (phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics) survey of interjection properties, and the outline makes intelligent use of it. The boundary-case material (interjections vs. nouns, verbs, adverbs, fillers, routine formulae) is the best part of the paper. The Ameka/Wilkins debate on routine formulae, the Meinard analysis of syntactic isolation, and the O'Connell & Kowal criteria for distinguishing fillers are handled with care. This is not a hand-waving treatment.

- **The diachronic centrepiece is well chosen for JHP.** Gehweiler's four-stage bleaching model, the COHA data on *nay*-to-*yeah* shift, and the GloWbE variation data give the paper a genuine historical-pragmatic spine. The observation that multiple diachronic pathways feed into the same property cluster (gradual inferencing vs. abrupt reanalysis) is interesting and specific. This section has the potential to do real work if it is developed with sufficient detail on corpus evidence.

- **The "pragmatic kinds" extension to HPC theory is at least a novel claim.** The idea that some HPC kinds project morphosyntactically while interjections project pragmatically is a genuine theoretical proposal that I have not seen elsewhere in the HPC-in-linguistics literature. Whether it succeeds is another matter (see below), but it is not mere redescription. Credit where due.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The paper never demonstrates that HPC does explanatory or predictive work that prototype theory does not.** The outline repeatedly claims that HPC "predicts" graded boundaries, fuzzy membership, and exceptional items. But prototype theory, developed specifically for lexical categories (Rosch 1975; Taylor 2003), predicts exactly the same phenomena: graded membership via distance from the prototype, fuzzy boundaries via overlapping similarity spaces, better-and-worse exemplars via family resemblance. The outline's Section 6 asserts that "prototype theory gets the gradience but not the mechanisms." This is a promissory note, not an argument. Prototype theory has always accommodated causal/functional explanations for why certain features cluster (Lakoff 1987, ch. 4 on "motivated" radial categories). The author needs to show a *specific analytical result* that HPC delivers and prototype theory cannot. As it stands, I can take every empirical claim in the outline, replace "HPC kind" with "prototype category," and lose nothing.

  **Actionable fix:** Construct an explicit comparison. Take one boundary case (say, routine formulae) and show step by step what HPC analysis yields that a prototype analysis misses. If you cannot do this, the framework is decorative.

- **"Boundary disputes as HPC predictions" is post hoc accommodation, not prediction.** The outline claims (Section 6) that graded boundaries with nouns, verbs, adverbs, fillers, and formulae are "exactly what property clusters produce." But HPC theory also "predicts" clear boundaries if the mechanisms are strong enough, and it "predicts" no category at all if the mechanisms are too weak. A theory that is compatible with clear boundaries, fuzzy boundaries, and no boundaries does not *predict* any of them. To genuinely predict, you would need to specify the mechanism strengths ex ante and derive the boundary gradient from them. The paper proposes no such quantitative or even ordinal model. Compare this with actual predictive work in HPC philosophy of biology, where mechanism strengths are independently measurable (e.g., gene flow rates, selection pressures). What is the analogue for interjections? How strong is "prosodic isolation" relative to "semantic bleaching"? Until the paper can rank, measure, or even order the mechanisms' contributions, "prediction" is too strong a word; "compatibility" is more honest.

  **Actionable fix:** Either (a) provide an explicit mechanism-strength model (even qualitative: "bleaching is the primary pathway, prosodic isolation is reinforcing"), with evidence, and derive boundary predictions from it; or (b) downgrade "predicts" to "accommodates" throughout and argue that accommodation is theoretically valuable. Option (b) is more honest but weakens the paper considerably.

- **Projectibility as "primarily pragmatic" is underdeveloped and possibly circular.** The outline's Section 5 claims that categorising something as an interjection lets you infer pragmatic properties (prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, emotive force, non-referentiality). But three of these four are the very properties used to *define* the category in Section 3. This is not projectibility; it is circularity. Projectibility in Boyd's sense is the ability to infer *new, previously unobserved* properties on the basis of category membership. If I encounter a novel interjection (say, a nonce coinage), what does the HPC analysis let me predict about it that I would not predict simply by noting that it occurs in supplement function with prosodic isolation? The outline needs to identify inferences that go beyond the defining cluster, or the projectibility claim collapses into "members of the category have the properties of the category."

  **Actionable fix:** Identify at least two projectible inferences that are *not* part of the defining property cluster. For example: does categorising *X* as an interjection predict anything about its diachronic trajectory (will it bleach further? will it develop routine-formula uses?), its sociolinguistic distribution (regional variation patterns), or its psycholinguistic processing (reaction times, ERP signatures)? These would be genuinely projectible. The four items currently listed are not.

---

## 4. Key question (Q&A)

Here is the question I would ask at the conference, and I would want a direct answer:

**"You claim that HPC theory predicts the graded boundaries we observe between interjections and adjacent categories. I can construct a parallel argument for prototype theory, family resemblance, or even a bare statistical-clustering approach, and make the same 'prediction.' So my question is concrete: Give me one empirical observation about English interjections that your HPC analysis predicts (or even retrodicts with greater specificity) that these alternative frameworks do not. Not a restatement in HPC vocabulary of something we already knew, but a novel inference. If HPC theory is doing genuine theoretical work and not merely providing a philosophical vocabulary for restating gradient category membership, there should be at least one such case."**

---

## 5. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit.**

The empirical material is strong, the diachronic focus is well suited to JHP, and the "pragmatic kinds" idea is potentially interesting. But the paper currently fails to demonstrate that HPC theory does analytical work that simpler, well-established frameworks (prototype theory, gradient category analysis) cannot do. The projectibility argument is circular as stated. The "prediction" claim is post hoc. These are not fatal problems, but they require substantial theoretical revision, not just polishing. Address the three actionable fixes above, and this could be a genuinely valuable paper. Without them, it is a competent survey of interjection properties dressed in an ill-fitting philosophical suit.
