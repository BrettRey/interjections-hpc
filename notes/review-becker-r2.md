# Round 2 Review: Social Positioning of the Restructured Paper

**Reviewer persona:** Howard Becker, *Writing for Social Scientists*
**Paper:** "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster"
**Target venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics

---

## 1. Round 1 Concerns Addressed?

### Gift before demand: Yes, and it works.

The restructuring is real. Section 2 is now "The property cluster" -- five subsections of phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics, all with interjection data, before HPC theory appears. HPC theory has been pushed to Section 3. The reader encounters *uh-oh*, *tut-tut*, *damn*, *gee*, *bruh* before encountering Boyd, Slater, or perturbation tests. This is the reversal I asked for.

The effect is substantial. By the time the reader reaches Section 3 ("HPC theory and why projectibility matters"), they have already seen the hedged universals (Wilkins's "commonly," "typically," "usually," "generally"), the co-occurrence without necessity, and the *damn* cluster-breaking example. The framework arrives as an answer to a question the data has already raised: why do these properties hang together when no single one is necessary? That is exactly the right order. The demand follows the gift.

The "Inferential contribution" paragraphs at the end of each subsection in Section 2 are a smart structural choice. They do double duty: they advance the empirical description and they pre-load the projectibility argument that Section 5 develops. The reader absorbs the idea that properties license inferences before they learn the word "projectibility." This is pedagogically effective.

### Adversarial tone: Mostly fixed.

The verbs are better. "Treated" (line 33, for Jespersen) replaces "denied." "Included the category but called it" (line 36, for Quirk) replaces "conceded." "Listed interjection as one of nine lexical categories" (line 37, for CGEL) and "dropped it from the textbook" (line 41, for the Students') are factual rather than evaluative.

But one attack survives and it's the sharpest one. Line 42-43: "because 'there really isn't anything interesting for a grammar to say' about interjections. A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut." That last sentence is still a gotcha. It scores a point against Huddleston & Pullum rather than acknowledging the reasonable textbook rationale (space constraints, pedagogical priorities). This is the kind of line that makes the author feel clever and the reader feel manipulated. It should be cut or reframed. The factual contrast between the two books is interesting; the editorial commentary ("thin enough for the textbook to cut") is the problem.

## 2. Tribal Dynamics

The restructured paper reads more like an insider's contribution to JHP than the original did, but it isn't fully there yet.

**What works tribally:**

The Gehweiler material (Section 4.1.1) is still the strongest tribal credential and it now appears in better company. The recruitment pathways section (4.1) is good historical pragmatics: documented trajectories, staged change, mechanism identification. The Traugott & Dasher framing in 4.1.3 ("Convergent pathways") is well judged -- it uses vocabulary the JHP audience already owns. The COHA/GloWbE corpus material in Section 4.4 ("Dynamic stability") is tribal currency. The Ameka-Wilkins resolution (Section 6.3) remains the paper's most generous moment.

**What still feels foreign:**

The subscript notation (interjection_syn, interjection_sem, interjection_int) in Section 3.3 is still the most socially marked element. It has moved from the pre-data position to a post-data position, which helps, but it still announces "I am from a different discipline and I have brought my notation." The notation earns its keep analytically in Section 5.2 (the attributive *damn* convergence-divergence pattern), and that payoff now comes only about 400 lines after the notation is introduced rather than 600. Better. But a JHP reader may still experience Section 3.3 as a formalism they didn't ask for.

The numbered predictions with defeat conditions (Section 6.1: P1, P3, P5, P10) remain hypothesis-testing rhetoric from experimental science. JHP publishes close historical and corpus work, not prediction-and-falsification frameworks. This material is intellectually strong but socially risky. I am not saying to remove it -- the predictions are the paper's distinctive contribution to HPC theory -- but the framing could be softened. "Four predictions are testable" is less confrontational than the current presentation, which reads like a research proposal.

**The "criterion of success" paragraph** (lines 321-324) is still in the paper and still performative. "This paper succeeds if..." tells the audience the author will judge themselves by a criterion the audience may not share. It worked as self-discipline in drafting. In the published paper, it feels like a contract the reader never signed. The argument is strong enough to succeed on its own terms without this meta-declaration.

## 3. Remaining Problems

### Problem 1: The introduction still front-loads philosophy.

The opening three paragraphs are well judged: Bullokar, Jespersen, Quirk, CGEL, the empirical picture. This is good. But paragraph 4 (lines 59-65) introduces Boyd, HPC theory, and the paper's threefold contribution before the reader has seen any data. The paragraph is only six lines, and it's well written, but it asks the reader to accept the relevance of "homeostatic property cluster theory (Boyd 1999)" on faith. In the restructured version, Section 2 delivers the data that motivates the framework, so the introduction doesn't need to sell the framework -- it just needs to promise it. Consider: "This paper argues that the properties of interjections are not random co-occurrences but a cluster maintained by identifiable mechanisms, with consequences for what the category lets you predict. The framework is developed in Section 3; the data that motivates it comes first." That's less informative than the current paragraph but more inviting.

### Problem 2: Section 2.6 ("Co-occurrence, not necessity") does theoretical work it shouldn't yet.

This subsection (lines 249-276) summarizes the cluster and then explains it in HPC terms: "This is what an HPC predicts." But Section 3 hasn't happened yet. The reader hasn't been told what an HPC is. The subsection is doing two things at once: closing the empirical survey and opening the theoretical argument. The *damn* cluster-breaking example is excellent and belongs here, but the HPC framing ("a list of co-occurring properties can't explain this pattern; a cluster maintained by mechanisms can") is doing theory before the theory section. Either move the HPC framing into Section 3 (where it would be a strong opening: "Section 2 documented a cluster. Here is how to understand it...") or keep it here but signal more clearly that this is a preview: "The next two sections develop this distinction."

Actually, re-reading, lines 274-276 do signal this: "The next two sections develop the theoretical framework (Section 3) and the mechanisms themselves (Section 4)." So the forward reference is present. The problem is smaller than I initially thought. But the phrase "This is what an HPC predicts" (line 258) still uses framework vocabulary before the framework has been introduced. A reader who doesn't know HPC will stumble. Consider: "This co-occurrence pattern -- statistically reliable but not definitionally required -- is what the framework in Section 3 is designed to explain."

### Problem 3: The conclusion's first sentence is still an attack.

Line 976: "English interjections aren't marginal or anomalous." This is a direct contradiction of Quirk et al. and it's positioned for maximum impact as the paper's final-section opener. The rest of the conclusion is good -- balanced, forward-looking, honest about untested predictions. But opening with a flat negation of a canonical characterisation feels like the paper's last word is a correction rather than a contribution. Consider leading with the positive claim: "English interjections are a thin-but-genuine HPC" (which is the next sentence). The negation can follow as a consequence, not a declaration.

---

## 4. Advice

The restructuring has changed the paper's social dynamics. The gift now precedes the demand. The reader encounters *uh-oh*, *damn*, *gee*, *bruh*, the Gehweiler trajectory, the corpus turnover, and the *damn* cluster-breaking example before they encounter Boyd, Slater, or perturbation tests. This is the right order and it makes the philosophy of science feel earned rather than imposed. What remains is finishing work. The introduction's fourth paragraph still front-loads HPC before the data. Section 2.6 uses HPC vocabulary before the framework section. The conclusion opens with a negation rather than a contribution. The "criterion of success" paragraph is still performative. These are all fixable in an afternoon, and fixing them would complete the social repositioning. The paper's strongest passages -- the Gehweiler trajectory, the *damn* cluster-breaking analysis, the Ameka-Wilkins resolution, the subscript payoff for attributive *damn* -- are already doing the work of showing the JHP reader something they couldn't see before. The remaining problems are all cases where the paper tells instead of shows, or corrects instead of contributes. The argument is strong enough to let the data do the persuading. Trust it.
