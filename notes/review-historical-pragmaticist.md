# Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer expertise:** Historical pragmatics, grammaticalization, semantic change, diachronic pragmatics
**Target journal:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics (John Benjamins)

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections, far from being the marginal and anomalous category dismissed by major grammars, constitute a homeostatic property cluster (HPC) sustained by identifiable diachronic mechanisms (semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation) and yielding primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional projectibility.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The diachronic framing is genuinely well-suited to JHP.** Section 4 positions Gehweiler's (2008) four-stage bleaching model alongside Traugott & Dasher's subjectification trajectory, which grounds the HPC mechanisms in established grammaticalization theory rather than inventing new apparatus. The insight that bleaching paths *are* homeostatic mechanisms (they continually replenish the cluster as individual members enter and leave) is a real contribution. The nay-to-yeah diachronic data and the regional variation evidence (lah, yaar, haba) together make a convincing case for "dynamic stability," which is the most distinctive claim in the paper from a historical-pragmatic perspective.

- **The neglect narrative in Section 1 is well-constructed and provides genuine scholarly motivation.** Moving from Bullokar (1586) through Jespersen (1924), Quirk et al. (1985), and Huddleston & Pullum (2005) establishes a clear trajectory of declining attention. This is not merely rhetorical; it motivates the theoretical intervention. The Wikipedia article supplies ample primary-source quotations (Bullokar's definition, Murray's definition, Quirk et al.'s "marginal and anomalous") that can be deployed effectively.

- **The projectibility argument (Section 5) is the paper's most original theoretical contribution.** The claim that interjections project pragmatically rather than truth-conditionally extends HPC theory in a non-trivial way: it suggests that not all natural kinds project in the same domain. This is genuinely novel within the HPC-in-linguistics programme and gives the paper a distinctive thesis beyond "here is another HPC case study."

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The diachronic mechanisms section (Section 4) relies too heavily on a single extended case study.** Gehweiler (2008) on *gee!* does essential work: it provides the four-stage bleaching model, the prosodic-isolation-reinforcing-independence claim, and the invited-inferencing-vs.-abrupt-reanalysis distinction. But for a paper in JHP, the diachronic centrepiece needs more empirical depth. The outline mentions *nay*-to-*yeah* (COHA data) and regional variants (GloWbE), but these are presented as evidence of "dynamic stability," not as independent mechanism analyses. The paper needs at least one or two additional bleaching trajectories analysed in comparable detail. Candidates from the Wikipedia article: the *God* > *gosh* / *golly* path, the *damn* trajectory (verb > interjection, with the complement-retention evidence from CGEL), or the *hell* > *heck* euphemistic pathway. Without these, a JHP reviewer will reasonably object that the mechanism analysis is underspecified and overgeneralised from a single case.

- **The engagement with grammaticalization theory is gestural rather than substantive.** Section 4 invokes Traugott & Dasher's subjectification scale (truth-conditional > non-truth-conditional, content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective) but does not work through how specific interjections move along this cline. For JHP, this needs to be demonstrated, not merely asserted. Which interjections show the full Invited Inferencing trajectory? At what stage does a bleaching noun become "intersubjective"? Is the Traugott & Dasher framework actually the right one here, or does bleaching to interjection status involve a more abrupt categorical shift than gradual invited inferencing predicts? Gehweiler (2008) herself distinguishes gradual (speaker-based) from abrupt (hearer-based) reanalysis, and the outline notes this, but does not resolve the tension. A JHP reviewer will want to know whether the paper is claiming that interjection formation is a species of grammaticalization (in Traugott's sense) or something distinct.

- **The boundary-dispute material (Sections 3 and 6) risks becoming a catalogue rather than an argument.** The Wikipedia article provides excellent boundary cases (interjections vs. nouns, verbs, adverbs, fillers, routine formulae), and the outline correctly notes that HPC predicts graded boundaries. But the current structure risks presenting five parallel boundary discussions without a unifying analytical point beyond "boundaries are graded." For JHP readers, the interesting question is diachronic: *how* do items cross these boundaries, and does the direction of crossing tell us something about the mechanisms? The Ameka-vs.-Wilkins debate on routine formulae, for instance, could be reframed as a question about whether formulae are *entering* or *leaving* the interjection cluster diachronically (conventionalisation vs. specialisation). Without this reframing, Section 3 reads as synchronic description, which undercuts the paper's positioning for a historical pragmatics journal.

---

## 4. Key question I would ask at Q&A

You claim that diachronic mechanisms (bleaching, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation) are the "homeostatic glue" that sustains the interjection cluster. But Gehweiler's (2008) bleaching model describes how items *enter* the cluster. What mechanisms keep items *in* the cluster once they are there? HPC theory requires not just recruitment mechanisms but maintenance mechanisms. Is pragmatic conventionalisation the maintenance mechanism? If so, how do you account for items that *leave* the cluster (e.g., *nay* declining in interjection use) -- does the cluster have mechanisms of expulsion, or is departure simply a matter of frequency attrition? And if departure is mere attrition rather than a mechanism-driven process, does that weaken the "homeostatic" part of the HPC claim?

---

## 5. Verdict: **Revise and Resubmit**

**Justification:** The paper has a genuine contribution to make (pragmatic projectibility, interjections as HPC test case) and is well-targeted at JHP via Gehweiler and the diachronic framing, but the mechanism analysis needs more empirical depth (additional bleaching paths beyond *gee!*), the engagement with grammaticalization theory (Traugott & Dasher, Heine & Kuteva) needs to move from invocation to demonstration, and the boundary material needs diachronic reframing to justify publication in a historical pragmatics venue rather than a general linguistics journal.

---

## Additional notes for the author

1. **Gehweiler (2008) appeared in JHP.** This is a significant advantage: you are building directly on a paper published in the target journal. Make this connection explicit and frame your paper as extending Gehweiler's single-case analysis to a general theoretical claim. This positions the paper as a natural sequel rather than an unrelated submission.

2. **Dingemanse (2020) is an asset but needs careful handling.** The "liminal signs" framing is attractive, but Dingemanse's theoretical commitments (interactional linguistics, usage-based) differ from HPC's realist ontology. You need to address whether HPC and Dingemanse's interactional account are compatible or in tension. A brief paragraph acknowledging this would pre-empt a reviewer objection.

3. **The COHA and GloWbE data should be presented quantitatively.** The outline mentions corpus data but does not specify what kind of analysis. For JHP, even simple frequency-over-time plots (nay vs. yeah in COHA) would strengthen the diachronic claims considerably. If you have access to the data, include figures.

4. **Consider engaging with Heine & Kuteva (2002) on grammaticalization paths.** Their *World Lexicon of Grammaticalization* catalogues cross-linguistic source-target patterns. If interjection formation shows up as a recurrent grammaticalization path (NOUN > INTERJECTION, VERB > INTERJECTION), this would strengthen the HPC claim that the cluster is sustained by regular mechanisms rather than idiosyncratic historical accidents.

5. **The paper's length (estimated 13--15 pages from the outline) is appropriate for JHP.**
