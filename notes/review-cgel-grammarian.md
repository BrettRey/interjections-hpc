# CGEL Grammarian Review: "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster"

**Reviewer expertise:** English descriptive grammar (CGEL tradition), lexical categories, the category-function distinction, supplement function, peripheral word classes.

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections, far from being a marginal or defective lexical category, constitute a homeostatic property cluster (HPC) sustained by identifiable diachronic mechanisms (semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation, pragmatic conventionalisation) and yielding primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional projectibility.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The category-function distinction is correctly maintained throughout.** The outline consistently treats interjection as a lexical category and supplement as a syntactic function, which is exactly the CGEL analysis (H&P 2002: 1350ff). The statement in Section 3 that interjections occupy "supplement function" and the Section on syntax that notes "the supplement function may be realized by other units" (relative clauses, NPs, AdjPs, etc.) shows a clear grasp of the many-to-many mapping between categories and functions. This is the right analysis and it is rare to see it stated this cleanly outside CGEL itself.

- **The boundary-case strategy is well suited to HPC argumentation.** Each property in Section 3 is paired with a boundary case (adverbs also lack inflection, bleached nouns retain vestiges of nominal morphology, verb-derived interjections take NP complements). This is not mere throat-clearing; it is exactly the kind of evidence that HPC theory is designed to handle and that classical necessary-and-sufficient-condition approaches fail on. The Wikipedia source material provides unusually thorough documentation of these boundary disputes, and the outline deploys them strategically.

- **The diachronic mechanism section (Section 4) is the right centrepiece for JHP.** Gehweiler's (2008) four-stage bleaching model provides a concrete, testable mechanism trajectory, and the nay-to-yeah diachronic data from COHA gives the paper empirical grounding that a purely synchronic account would lack. Linking Traugott & Dasher's subjectification scale to the HPC mechanism story is a genuinely productive theoretical move.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The syntactic analysis of interjection phrases needs more precision and more CGEL engagement.** The outline mentions "interjection phrases" (*oh boy*, *holy cow*, *damn these mosquitoes*) but does not clearly distinguish between the different syntactic structures these represent. In CGEL's analysis, *damn these mosquitoes* involves an interjection heading a phrase with an NP complement (H&P 2002: 1361fn), but *oh boy* is a different structure: is *boy* a vocative (NP in a distinct function), or is *oh* a modifier of a nominal interjection, or is the whole thing a fixed collocation? The Wikipedia article lumps these as "conventionalized pairs" following Bloomfield and Ameka, but a paper claiming syntactic precision needs to sort out the internal structure. Are *holy cow* and *hot damn* modifier + head structures (with an AdjP modifying an interjection)? If so, what licenses an adjective to modify an interjection? This is syntactically interesting and the outline currently waves at it. The paper should either analyse these structures properly or explicitly acknowledge the gap as a problem for future work.

- **Projectibility needs sharper syntactic content.** The projectibility section (Section 5) lists four inferences: prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration (supplement function), emotive force, non-referentiality. But three of these are pragmatic/semantic, and only one (supplement function) is syntactic. For a paper that claims to extend HPC theory, the syntactic projectibility of interjections deserves more attention. What *syntactic* inferences does the category label license? That the item cannot head a clause? That it cannot function as subject, object, or predicative complement? That it resists coordination with items of other categories? These negative distributional facts are precisely what makes "interjection" a useful category label in syntax, and they are not mentioned. The comparison with nouns ("referential, inflects for number, takes determiners") is fine but asymmetric: those are positive facts. The paper should state the corresponding positive and negative syntactic facts for interjections with equal precision.

- **The treatment of fillers (Section 3 / Wikipedia source) underplays a genuine categorial challenge.** O'Connell & Kowal's (2005) distributional criteria for distinguishing fillers from interjections (pauses, citation-introduction, emphasis) are phonological and pragmatic, not syntactic. The paper should note that from a CGEL perspective, the question is whether *um*, *uh*, *well* (filler uses) occupy supplement function or some other function, and whether they belong to the interjection category or constitute a distinct category. CGEL itself is not fully explicit on this point (H&P 2002 treats discourse markers and fillers only briefly). The outline should acknowledge that this is an open question in the framework and explain how HPC handles it: are fillers peripheral members of the interjection cluster (sharing some but not all properties), or are they a neighbouring but distinct cluster? Either answer is interesting, but the outline currently just reports the debate without taking a position.

---

## 4. Key question for Q&A

You argue that interjections yield "primarily pragmatic" projectibility, which extends HPC theory by showing that not all kinds project in the same domain. But is this really a feature of the *category* or of the *function*? Supplements of any category (NPs, AdjPs, relative clauses in supplement function) share the property of prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration. So what is distinctively projected by the category label "interjection" as opposed to the function label "supplement"? Put differently: if I know something is a supplement, I can already infer prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration. What does knowing it is an *interjection* add beyond what knowing it is a *supplement* already gives me? This question strikes at the heart of the paper's theoretical contribution, and the answer had better be crisp.

---

## 5. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit.**

The core argument is sound, the empirical base is strong, and the diachronic mechanism story is well suited to JHP. But the syntactic analysis needs tightening in three specific places (interjection phrase structure, syntactic projectibility, filler categorisation), and the projectibility section must clearly distinguish what the category label adds beyond what the function label already provides. These are addressable revisions, not structural problems.
