# McCloskey Review: Rhetoric and Positioning

**Reviewer persona:** Deirdre McCloskey, author of *The Rhetoric of Economics*
**Reviewed:** `main.tex` (English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster)
**Target venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics (John Benjamins)
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. Insider or Outsider?

The paper reads as an insider. More precisely: it reads as a linguist who has done philosophy of science homework and is using it surgically, not as a philosopher who has wandered into a linguistics department and begun rearranging the furniture.

The tell is the opening. You don't begin with Boyd (1999) or with any manifesto about how linguistics needs philosophy. You begin with Bullokar in 1586, then walk through Jespersen, Quirk et al., and Huddleston & Pullum. That's a disciplinary genealogy. It says: *I know the field's own narrative about this category, and I'm going to work within it.* The philosophy enters in paragraph five, after you've already established the empirical puzzle on linguistics' own terms. This is correct positioning. The alternative -- leading with HPC theory and then "applying" it to interjections -- would read as an outsider imposing a framework.

The CGEL apparatus throughout (supplement function, IntjP, the category/function distinction) reinforces this. You're not translating linguistics into philosophy; you're using philosophical tools to resolve a problem the discipline generated and then abandoned. The subscript notation (interjection_syn, interjection_sem, interjection_int) is the most "imported" move, and even there, the subscripts are labelled with the discipline's own subfield names, not with philosophical jargon.

**Verdict:** Insider making a move. The framework is imported, but the motivating question, the data, and the disciplinary references are all native.

## 2. The Subversion

### Where it succeeds

The central subversion is this: you take the thing the discipline is most embarrassed by -- a category it can't define cleanly -- and argue that the embarrassment is *evidence for your framework*. "The category that embarrassed classical theory is the strongest evidence for HPC, not the weakest" (line 77). This is a strong rhetorical move because it doesn't ask the discipline to feel bad about its failures. It says: you were right that something interesting was going on; you just had the wrong tools.

The Huddleston & Pullum anecdote is the paper's best moment of subversion:

> "listed interjection as one of nine lexical categories in their comprehensive grammar... but dropped it from the textbook because 'there really isn't anything interesting for a grammar to say' about interjections. A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut." (lines 37--44)

That last sentence is devastating, and it earns its devastation by being precise. You're not attacking Huddleston & Pullum; you're pointing out a contradiction in their own practice. This is how you subvert from the inside: you hold the discipline to its own standards.

The perturbation test (Section 4.3) subverts the "is it a category or isn't it?" framing by making the question empirical rather than definitional. Instead of arguing about whether interjections *really are* a word class, you ask what happens when you remove a proposed mechanism. The *pop* and *God* examples are well chosen: small, concrete, and they do the work.

The Ameka-Wilkins resolution (Section 6.3) is another successful subversion. You take a genuine 30-year debate and say: "Both analysts were right about the data. The disagreement was about the framework" (lines 1006--1008). This is generous, which is why it works. If you'd said one was right and the other wrong, you'd be adjudicating. Instead, you dissolve the dispute -- which is more subversive, because it implies the discipline needed a different kind of framework to see what was happening.

### Where it fails or underperforms

The comparison with prototype theory (Section 2.2) is necessary but undercooked. You say prototype theory "describes the fact that properties cluster but not why they cluster in the pattern they do" (lines 150--151). That's a real distinction, and it's important. But the section reads like a checklist: prototype theory can't do X, can't do Y, can't do Z. This is the rhetoric of superiority, not of engagement. A prototype theorist reading this will feel strawmanned, because you haven't shown that prototype theorists *care* about mechanism-derived predictions. Many of them would say: "Of course we don't predict order of erosion -- that's not what our framework is for." You need to show that a prototype theorist *should* care, not just that they currently don't.

The "thin-but-genuine HPC" framing (Section 6.2) is honest but rhetorically risky. You compare interjections to adjectives and explicitly say they aren't as robust as nouns or verbs. This is truthful. But "thin" is not a word that sells. A hostile reader hears "thin" and thinks: "So it barely qualifies. Why should I care?" You might consider: *modestly stabilised*, *lightly entrenched*, or even just *genuine HPC with fewer stabilisers* without the word "thin" doing so much structural work. The word appears five times in the paper, and by the conclusion it has accumulated a defensive connotation.

## 3. Crank or Prophet?

### Prophet moments

The criterion of success (lines 135--138):

> "This paper succeeds if it shows that categorizing a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes. If it merely lists co-occurring properties, it has described a cluster, not demonstrated a kind."

This is prophetic because it's honest about what would constitute failure. Cranks don't tell you how to falsify their claims. You do, and you do it early. This builds enormous credibility with a sophisticated reader.

The subscript architecture (Section 2.3, reprised in 5.2) is the paper's most original move. The idea that syntacticians, semanticists, and conversation analysts are tracking *different HPCs* with overlapping extensions is genuinely illuminating. It doesn't just redescribe existing practice; it explains why people in different subfields keep disagreeing about the boundaries. This has the feel of something a reader will remember.

### Crank moments

"The cluster is an attractor, not a historical accident" (line 670). This is overclaiming. You have shown convergence from multiple pathways; you have not shown attractor dynamics in any technical sense. Dynamical-systems language is seductive and almost always imprecise when borrowed by humanists. If you mean "multiple routes converge on the same profile, which suggests the profile is stable," say that. "Attractor" imports a mathematical framework you haven't earned.

Similarly: "a property space with attractor basins maintained by the stabilisers above" (lines 676--677). Same problem. You're borrowing from dynamical systems without doing the dynamical systems. A physicist on the editorial board (unlikely at JHP, but possible at review stage) will notice. More importantly, a historically-minded pragmaticist will notice that you've slipped from careful empirical description into metaphor -- and metaphor from a different discipline.

"Three diachronically distinct processes converge on the same synchronic cluster -- evidence for attractor dynamics, not coincidence" (lines 1021--1022, in the conclusion). You've now said this three times. Repetition doesn't make metaphor into evidence. The convergence is real; the "attractor dynamics" gloss is a promissory note you haven't redeemed.

### Preaching moments

The final paragraph of the conclusion:

> "If both predictions fail, the HPC claim is weakened. If they hold, interjections join the growing list of linguistic categories whose structure is better explained by mechanisms than by definitions." (lines 1047--1049)

This is fine in content but slightly sermonic in tone. "Join the growing list" implies a movement. At JHP, you're writing for historical pragmaticists, not for converts to the HPC programme. Consider ending with the historical pragmatics payoff rather than with the programme's expansion.

## 4. The Audience Question

### Who this will convince

**Historical pragmaticists interested in grammaticalization.** The Gehweiler analysis (Section 4.1) is the paper's strongest section for this audience. The four-stage bleaching trajectory, the Traugott connection, the distinction between Invited Inferencing and reanalysis -- this is their language, their evidence base. The HPC framework adds something they can use (perturbation predictions, named defeat conditions) without asking them to abandon their existing tools.

**Corpus linguists interested in category boundaries.** The GloWbE and COHA data, the prediction about conditioning recovering structure (P5), and the dynamic stability argument are directly relevant to empirical corpus work. These readers will appreciate that you've identified testable predictions rather than just re-describing known patterns.

**Syntacticians who take CGEL seriously.** The category/function distinction (Section 5.3), the IntjP analysis, and the supplement-function discussion speak directly to this audience.

### Who this will alienate

**Prototype theorists.** As noted above, Section 2.2 is dismissive rather than engaging. The four predictions you claim prototype theory can't generate (P1, P3, P5, P10) are real advantages, but the framing reads as "your framework is descriptively adequate but explanatorily empty." That's a serious charge, and you make it in two paragraphs without engaging with any prototype theorist's response. Taylor (2003) is cited but not argued with.

**Conversation analysts.** Interjection_int is the thinnest of the three subscript HPCs. You cite Stivers (2019) and Dingemanse (2020) but don't engage deeply with their frameworks. A CA reader may feel that you've borrowed their terminology (response tokens, liminal signs, backchanneling) without understanding their methodology (sequential analysis, turn construction). The interactional predictions in Section 5.2 are plausible but not grounded in actual sequential data.

**Readers allergic to self-citation.** Reynolds (2025) is cited 18 times in a paper with approximately 30 total citations. At a certain point, this stops looking like "building on prior work" and starts looking like a chapter of the book repackaged for a journal. If the HPC book is the theoretical foundation, a single comprehensive citation early on ("for full development, see Reynolds 2025, especially Chapters 6--7 and 11") might be more effective than 18 distributed citations. This is a real risk at JHP, where reviewers may not know or care about the book.

### Is it calibrated for JHP?

Mostly yes, but with two miscalibrations:

1. **Too much HPC apparatus, not enough historical pragmatics.** The paper's strongest JHP material is Section 4 (recruitment pathways, Gehweiler, Traugott & Dasher). But Section 4 is preceded by two sections of HPC theory (Section 2) and property description (Section 3) that a JHP reader will find slow-going. The historical pragmatics doesn't arrive until page 8 or 9. Consider whether Section 2 can be compressed -- the JHP reader doesn't need the full Boyd exegesis, just enough to understand stabilisers, perturbation, and projectibility.

2. **The conclusion points toward the HPC programme, not toward historical pragmatics.** The final paragraph should land on what this means for studying language change, not on what it means for the HPC project. The JHP reader's takeaway question is: "Does this change how I study interjections historically?" Your answer should be: yes, because the stabiliser framework gives you named predictions about bleaching trajectories, and the perturbation test gives you falsifiable claims about mechanism boundaries. Say that in the conclusion.

## 5. One Paragraph of Advice on Positioning

You have a paper that is 70% right and 30% miscalibrated. The 70% is: an insider linguist using philosophy of science to resolve a genuine embarrassment in the discipline's category system, with concrete predictions, named defeat conditions, and an honest assessment of what's thin and what's thick. The 30% is: too much self-referential apparatus (the book citations), a dismissive treatment of the main alternative (prototype theory), borrowed dynamical-systems language that hasn't been earned ("attractor," "attractor basins"), and a conclusion aimed at the wrong audience. Here is what I'd do. Cut the dynamical-systems metaphors entirely -- "convergent" is a perfectly good word that doesn't import physics. Compress Section 2 to half its current length; the JHP reader needs the punchline, not the derivation. Engage prototype theory charitably or drop it to a footnote -- right now it reads like you're winning an argument nobody at JHP is having. Reduce the self-citations from 18 to 5 or 6, consolidating them into a few signpost references. And rewrite the conclusion to end on a historical-pragmatics question: what does this framework predict about the *next* bleaching path, the *next* category recruited into the interjection cluster? That's the question a JHP reader wants answered, and it's the question your framework is uniquely positioned to answer.
