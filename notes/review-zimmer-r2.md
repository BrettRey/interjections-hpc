**Reviewer persona:** Carl Zimmer (science writer)

**File reviewed:** `main.tex` (restructured paper)
**Focus:** Did the restructure address Round 1 concerns about vividness?

## 1. Round 1 concerns addressed?

The restructure has done real structural work. The paper is better organized than the version I first read. Let me walk through my Round 1 recommendations and what happened.

**"Lead with the organizing question earlier in the Introduction."** Addressed. The question "what does categorizing something as an interjection let you predict?" now appears in the second paragraph of the Introduction (ll. 52--54), before the HPC framework is introduced. And the framing is sharper: "A category that supports no inferences is a filing label. A category that supports reliable inferences across novel instances is a genuine kind." That is a clean statement of stakes. The gear-shift from narrative to theory is still present, but the question now precedes the framework rather than being buried inside it.

**"Add a concrete preview before the HPC slogan."** Partially addressed. The slogan passage (Section 3.1, ll. 295--301) is still abstraction-first: "a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes." But the restructure has moved the property cluster survey (Section 2) ahead of the theory section (Section 3), which means the reader has already seen phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics as concrete interjection properties before encountering the slogan. That sequencing does the work a preview example would have done. The reader arrives at the slogan having already held "uh-oh" and "damn" and "tut-tut" in their hands. It is still not as vivid as it could be, but the structural fix is genuine.

**"Give 'stabiliser' a physical image at first introduction."** Not addressed. The term is still introduced via quotation and negation (ll. 303--308): "something counts as a stabiliser if removing it would change the clustering." The reader still has to wait until Section 4 (Stabilisers: recruitment and maintenance) to see what a stabiliser actually does. The non-referentiality ratchet (Section 4.2.2) remains excellent when it arrives, but the term itself is experientially empty at the point of introduction.

**"Add a one-sentence concrete gloss for each subscript category."** Not addressed. The subscript notation (Section 3.3, ll. 371--397) is still introduced via bullet points dense with technical vocabulary: "use-conditional meaning cluster: independent of at-issue content, nondisplaceable, perspective-dependent, descriptively ineffable." Each bullet reads like a compressed seminar. A single plain-English sentence before the technical detail would help enormously.

**"Thin Section 5.2."** Partially addressed. The section is still the longest and densest in the paper (ll. 698--773), but the restructure has given the reader more preparation by placing the empirical survey first. The reader who arrives at Section 5.2 has already seen the subscript notation, the property cluster, and the mechanisms. The density is the same, but the reader is better equipped to handle it.

**"Make 'pragmatic kind' feel like an arrival."** Not addressed. The passage (Section 5.4, ll. 809--857) is still correct and abstract. The claim that interjections are a "pragmatic kind" -- that their richest predictions are about use, not denotation -- is the paper's distinctive theoretical contribution. It deserves something experiential. What does it look like to encounter a pragmatic kind in the wild? What does a field linguist do differently? The passage tells you what the concept means but doesn't show you what it feels like.

**"Consider the river-delta image."** Not adopted. This is fine -- the image was a suggestion, not a prescription. But the paper still lacks a central physical metaphor. The "ratchet" in Section 4.2.2 is the closest thing, and it works well for that mechanism. The paper as a whole doesn't have an equivalent.

**Overall verdict:** The restructure has solved the biggest structural problem (theory-before-evidence) and sharpened the Introduction. It has not addressed the local-level vividness deficits: the slogan passage, the stabiliser introduction, the subscript bullets, and the pragmatic-kind passage all remain abstract. The paper is better. It is not yet vivid.

## 2. Where vivid (2--3 passages that work)

**The Bullokar-to-Huddleston arc (Introduction, ll. 26--44).** Still the strongest piece of storytelling in the paper. The compression of 440 years into three paragraphs, ending with "A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut" -- that sentence alone would make me keep reading. It has been preserved intact and continues to earn its place.

**The "damn" decoupling (Section 2.6, ll. 264--273).** This is the passage where the reader can hear the difference between two uses of the same word and feel properties peeling away:

> "Damn as an interjection (Damn!) has the full cluster: prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, non-referentiality, emotive force. Damn as an attributive modifier (the damn car) retains emotive force but loses prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration -- it's embedded inside an NP."

The paper is at its best when it puts a single word under two lights and lets the reader see the cluster breaking. This passage does what no amount of theoretical architecture can do on its own: it makes mechanisms visible.

**The "bruh" walkthrough (Section 5.1, ll. 661--690).** Walking through five predictions for a newly conventionalized form, then ranking them by impressiveness ("These predictions aren't equally impressive") -- that is honest science communication. The reader sees the framework doing work rather than being described. The concession about which predictions are "close to entailments" versus which involve "a genuine cross-domain leap" makes the passage more credible, not less. Prediction (d) -- predicting interactional behaviour from a phonological observation -- is the kind of inference that makes a reader sit up.

## 3. Where abstract (2--3 passages that still need grounding)

**The stabiliser definition (Section 3.1, ll. 303--308).**

> "something counts as a stabiliser if removing it would change the clustering -- if the category would fragment, drift, or dissolve."

This is still definition-by-negation with no concrete anchor. The reader encounters the paper's key technical term and is given an if-then conditional about an abstract outcome. By the time the perturbation test makes this concrete (Section 4.3, with "pop" and "God"), the reader has navigated two full subsections of theory. A single sentence at the point of introduction would fix this: "A tent pole is a stabiliser: pull it out and the tent collapses into a different shape. For interjections, prosodic isolation is a tent pole -- remove it and the form stops behaving like an interjection." Something physical. Something before the philosophy.

**The subscript-notation introduction (Section 3.3, ll. 371--397).**

> "Interjection_sem -- the semanticist's category, overlapping with Potts (2007)'s expressives. Use-conditional meaning cluster: independent of at-issue content, nondisplaceable, perspective-dependent, descriptively ineffable (Potts 2007: 166)."

This is the kind of passage that makes non-specialists' eyes glaze. Four Potts-technical properties packed into a single bullet point, each requiring its own explanation. The reader needs a human-scale gloss before the technical detail: "The semanticist's interjection is the one that tells you how the speaker feels without changing what's true or false about the world." Then the four properties. Each property cluster bullet should begin with what it means before explaining what it is called.

**The "pragmatic kind" passage (Section 5.4, ll. 809--857).**

> "Not all kinds project in the same domain. Nouns project morphosyntactically: they take determiners, inflect for number, fill argument slots. For the forms traditionally called 'interjections,' three HPCs overlap, and the richest projectibility belongs to interjection_int -- the interactional cluster."

This is the paper's most important theoretical claim, and it reads like a summary of a textbook chapter rather than a discovery. What would make it land: a scene. A field linguist in a language they don't know hears a form shouted at the start of a turn. They don't know the language's morphology or syntax. But they can already predict that the form won't inflect, won't embed, and will carry stance meaning. That is what it means for a kind to be pragmatic -- its predictions are available to someone who knows the pragmatics before the grammar. That would make "pragmatic kind" feel like an insight rather than a label.

## 4. One concrete image suggestion

The paper has a natural image sitting inside it, undeployed. Section 4.4 (Dynamic stability) describes interjection turnover -- "nay" fading, "yeah" rising, "bruh" appearing -- and says "The items change; the category doesn't." That sentence is the seed of a metaphor the paper never grows.

**The whirlpool.** A whirlpool is made of water, but no particular water. Molecules flow through it constantly. The whirlpool persists not because of its contents but because of the forces that shape it -- the current, the obstacle, the gradient. Remove the obstacle and the whirlpool dissolves. Change the current and it shifts shape.

The interjection cluster is a whirlpool. Individual forms pour through: "nay" flows out, "yeah" flows in, "bruh" appears at the edge. The cluster persists because the same forces -- prosodic isolation, non-referentiality, emotive pressure -- keep shaping new material into the same pattern. Neutralize one force (integrate the form prosodically) and the whirlpool dissolves (the form is reclassified as an adverb). That is the perturbation test, in one image.

This is simpler than the river-delta image I suggested in Round 1. It captures the same ideas (dynamic stability, content turnover, force-maintained shape) but adds the perturbation test (remove the obstacle and the whirlpool dissolves). It could appear in the Introduction, right after "The items change; the category doesn't" (or an equivalent preview of that idea), giving the reader a physical anchor for everything that follows.

## 5. The opening test

Does the Introduction now hook?

**Yes.** The Bullokar opening was always strong, and it has been preserved. The restructure has improved the transition from narrative to theory by placing the organizing question ("what does categorizing something as an interjection let you predict?") earlier and more prominently. The contribution statement (ll. 59--65) is clear: three things the paper does, stated directly.

The remaining weakness is at ll. 46--51, where the paper moves from the historical arc to the empirical situation:

> "The empirical picture, though, hasn't simplified. English interjections span phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics. The category resists clean definition -- no single property is necessary and sufficient -- and its boundaries with nouns, verbs, adverbs, fillers, and routine formulae are graded, not sharp."

This is correct and efficient. But it is a list. After the concreteness of Bullokar, Jespersen, Quirk, and Huddleston & Pullum, the reader gets five subdisciplines and five boundary categories in two sentences. Consider one concrete example of the boundary problem instead of a list: "Is 'hello' an interjection or a greeting formula? Is 'um' a word or a processing artifact? The boundaries are graded, not sharp." That would keep the reader in the world of specific words rather than subdiscipline labels.

But this is a small point. The opening hooks. The question is clear. The stakes are stated. A reader who gets to the end of the Introduction knows what the paper is about and why it matters. That is the test, and the Introduction passes it.

## Summary

**What improved:** The structure (empirical survey before theory), the Introduction's pacing, the organizing question's placement.

**What still needs work:** Local vividness at four key junctures: the stabiliser introduction, the subscript notation bullets, the pragmatic-kind passage, and the transition from historical narrative to empirical overview.

**The biggest single fix available:** Give "stabiliser" and "pragmatic kind" physical images at the point of introduction. The paper's theoretical apparatus is sound. Its weakest points are not logical but experiential -- the reader is told what these concepts mean but never shown what they look like. One image for each would transform the two weakest passages into two of the strongest.

The paper is significantly better than Round 1. Two or three local interventions would make it vivid throughout.
