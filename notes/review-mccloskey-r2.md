# McCloskey Review, Round 2: Restructured Paper

**Reviewer persona:** Deirdre McCloskey, author of *The Rhetoric of Economics*
**Reviewed:** `main.tex` (English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster), post-revision
**Target venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics (John Benjamins)
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. Round 1 Concerns Addressed?

### Dynamical-systems language

**Largely fixed.** In Round 1, I flagged three instances of "attractor" / "attractor basins" / "attractor dynamics" -- language borrowed from physics without the physics to back it. The paper has removed the explicit "attractor basins" and the conclusion's "evidence for attractor dynamics, not coincidence." One instance survives, in Section 4.1.3:

> "the cluster is an attractor that can be reached by different routes" (line 457)

This is the weakest of the three original uses, and in context it's doing less metaphysical work than the earlier two -- it reads more as a synonym for "convergent outcome" than as a claim about dynamical systems. I'd still prefer "convergent target" or simply "the cluster profile that different routes converge on," but I won't die on this hill. The worst offenders are gone.

**Verdict:** Substantially addressed. One residual instance, tolerable.

### Self-citations

**Reduced, but not enough.** Round 1 counted 18 citations to Reynolds (2025). The current draft has 17. That's one fewer, not the consolidation I recommended. The citations remain distributed across eight sections, and the paper still reads as if HPC book Chapters 6, 7, and 11 are co-authoring it. The P-numbered predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10) are each individually cited to the book, generating four citations in Section 3.2 alone.

My Round 1 advice was: consolidate to 5 or 6 signpost references. A single early citation ("for the full framework, see Reynolds 2025, especially Chs. 6--7 and 11; the numbered predictions tested below are developed there") would let you drop the individual `\citealp` citations for P1, P3, P5, P10, D1 and the repeated chapter-specific citations in Sections 4 and 6. That would bring the count to roughly 8--10, which is defensible for a paper that genuinely builds on the book. 17 is not.

The risk is real and specific to JHP: a reviewer who doesn't know the book will see 17 citations to the same author-year and suspect this is a chapter excerpt repackaged as a standalone paper. Even if it isn't, the *appearance* is damaging.

**Verdict:** Not adequately addressed. Reduce to 8--10.

### Conclusion landing

**Fixed, and well.** This was my sharpest criticism in Round 1: the conclusion aimed at the HPC programme ("interjections join the growing list...") rather than at historical pragmatics. The revision has added a dedicated historical-pragmatics paragraph (lines 998--1007):

> "For historical pragmatics specifically, the payoff is this: interjection formation involves at least three distinct diachronic processes... and HPC theory explains why they converge on a recognizable category despite their different trajectories."

This is exactly right. The conclusion now tells the JHP reader what they get: a framework that explains convergent category formation from distinct diachronic processes. The final sentence -- "The mechanisms are diachronic; the stability they produce is the kind of thing historical pragmatics is for" -- is strong, clean, and properly aimed.

The "growing list" language is gone. The final paragraph still mentions untested predictions (P5, P10), which is honest, but it ends on "better explained by mechanisms than by definitions," which leans slightly programme-ward. A half-sentence tacking back to diachronic application would perfect the landing. But this is a quibble, not a structural complaint.

**Verdict:** Substantially addressed. The conclusion now belongs at JHP.

## 2. Crank or Prophet?

The revision has moved the needle noticeably toward prophet. The worst crank symptom in Round 1 -- dynamical-systems language repeated three times as if repetition were evidence -- is gone. The surviving "attractor" is a minor blemish.

**Remaining tone issues:**

- The criterion-of-success paragraph (lines 321--324) is still one of the paper's best moments: "If it merely lists co-occurring properties, it has described a cluster, not demonstrated a kind." This is prophetic because it's falsifiable. It hasn't changed, and it shouldn't.

- The prototype-theory comparison (Section 3.2) has improved slightly: the HPC predictions now have named defeat conditions, which makes the comparison less dismissive and more substantive. But the section still reads as a checklist of prototype theory's limitations rather than an engagement with its strengths. A single sentence acknowledging what prototype theory *does* well (handling typicality effects, gradient acceptability, cross-linguistic variation in category boundaries) would make the contrast sharper by being fairer. Rapoport's Rules, if you like: re-express the position you're criticizing before you criticize it.

- "Thin-but-genuine" has survived as a framing device. I said in Round 1 that "thin" accumulates a defensive connotation. It still does. I count the word appearing in the section heading (6.2), in the body of that section, and in the conclusion. By the third occurrence, a hostile reader hears an apology. I still think "modestly stabilised" or "genuine HPC with fewer stabilisers" does the same work without the connotation of inadequacy. But I recognize you may be deliberately choosing honest modesty over salesmanship. If so, own it more aggressively: "thin" should be a virtue (parsimony), not a concession.

**Overall verdict on tone:** The paper no longer sounds like a convert preaching. It sounds like a careful analyst who has found a framework that resolves a genuine puzzle. That's the right register for JHP.

## 3. Remaining Problems

### Problem 1: Self-citation density remains too high

Addressed above. 17 citations to one's own book in a paper of this length is a rhetorical liability regardless of whether each citation is individually justified. The issue isn't intellectual honesty; it's how the paper reads to a reviewer who hasn't read the book. Consolidate. You'll lose nothing in precision and gain credibility.

### Problem 2: The abstract is still a placeholder

The abstract reads: "TODO: Write abstract here." This is obviously a draft marker, but I mention it because the abstract is the paper's most important paragraph for JHP reviewers. For a historical pragmatics venue, the abstract should foreground: (a) the diachronic convergence finding (three distinct processes, one category), (b) the stabiliser framework as a tool for historical pragmatics, and (c) the testable predictions. The HPC apparatus should be named but not explained in the abstract. "Homeostatic property cluster" is the framework; the *findings* are about interjection formation and maintenance. Lead with findings.

### Problem 3: The prototype-theory comparison still lacks charity

Section 3.2 is better than in Round 1 but remains the paper's weakest argumentative section. The four predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10) are genuine advantages of HPC over prototype theory, and the defeat conditions are well stated. But the section doesn't acknowledge prototype theory's actual achievements: it's been the dominant framework for gradient category membership for 50 years, it handles typicality effects elegantly, and it captures the intuition that *ouch* is a "better" interjection than *hello* without requiring mechanisms. A sentence or two saying this explicitly would make the subsequent critique sharper. You're not arguing that prototype theory is wrong; you're arguing it's incomplete. That's a different claim, and it needs to sound different.

---

## 4. One Paragraph of Advice

The paper is now 85% right, up from 70% in Round 1. The conclusion lands where it should, the dynamical-systems metaphors are nearly gone, and the overall register is careful rather than evangelical. Three things remain. First, consolidate the self-citations: 17 is still too many, 8--10 is the target, and the method is a single early signpost citation plus a few specific references where chapter-and-verse precision genuinely helps the reader (the perturbation test, the stabiliser concept, the P-predictions as a set). Second, write the abstract, and write it for historical pragmaticists, not for HPC converts: foreground the diachronic convergence finding and the testable predictions, not the framework's name. Third, give prototype theory its due in Section 3.2 before explaining what it can't do. Rapoport's Rules aren't just polite; they're rhetorically effective, because a reader who sees you engaging charitably with the alternative will trust your critique of it. These are all achievable in a single revision pass. The paper's core argument -- that interjections are a pragmatic kind, thin syntactically but rich interactionally, maintained by identifiable diachronic and synchronic mechanisms -- is strong. Get the packaging right and it will persuade.
