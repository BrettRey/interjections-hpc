# Rhetorical Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer persona:** Jeanne Fahnestock, author of *Rhetorical Figures in Science*
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. Key Rhetorical Moves

### Move 1: The Neglect Narrative (Bullokar to Huddleston & Pullum)

The paper opens with a four-century arc of declining attention: Bullokar (1586) included interjections; Jespersen (1924) denied them; Quirk et al. (1985) conceded them but called them "marginal and anomalous"; Huddleston & Pullum (2002) listed them but then (2005) dropped them because "there really isn't anything interesting for a grammar to say." The payoff line is a compressed antimetabole: "A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut."

**Evaluation.** This is one of the paper's strongest rhetorical performances. The neglect narrative does three things at once: it establishes exigence (the problem isn't solved), it creates a sympathy deficit that HPC can fill, and it reverses the valence of the evidence (what embarrassed classical theory is "the strongest evidence for HPC, not the weakest"). The Bullokar-to-H&P compression is elegant, with each sentence shorter and sharper than the last, creating a rhetorical acceleration that mirrors the claimed trajectory of "declining attention."

The risk is that the narrative oversells the neglect. Huddleston & Pullum (2002) did list interjections as a full lexical category with dedicated discussion; it was the *textbook* (2005) that dropped them, and textbooks cut many things for pedagogical reasons. The paper is honest about this ("real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut"), but the sentence works against its own candour: the antimetabole makes the cut sound principled when it may have been merely editorial. The distinction between "theoretically denied" and "pedagogically omitted" matters for the exigence claim, and the figure elides it.

### Move 2: The Perturbation Test

The perturbation test is the paper's central falsifiability device: "if a proposed stabiliser can be neutralized and the cluster remains intact, the stabiliser wasn't doing the work claimed for it." Two cases illustrate it: *pop* in *pop went the cork* (prosodic isolation neutralized, form reclassified as adverb) and *God* used referentially vs. expressively.

**Evaluation.** As a rhetorical move, the perturbation test works because it borrows the experimental structure of a controlled manipulation (neutralize one variable, observe consequences) without actually conducting an experiment. This is a figure of thought: the *test* is notional, operating on constructed examples and lexicographic decisions, not on experimental data. The *pop* case is particularly telling. What counts as "reclassification by some lexicographers" is a judgment call, not a perturbation result. The paper acknowledges this indirectly by citing Meinard (2015) with "cf." rather than claiming direct evidence.

The perturbation test does argumentative work in a second way: it distinguishes HPC from prototype theory. "Prototype theory offers no analogous test" is stated flatly, and the asymmetry is persuasive. But the test's falsifiability is undercut by its flexibility. If removing a stabiliser and seeing no change is supposed to defeat the claim, the paper needs to specify what would count as "the cluster remains intact." As presented, the defeat conditions are named but not operationalised, which makes the falsifiability more rhetorical than methodological.

### Move 3: The *bruh* Worked Example

The *bruh* passage (lines 697--726) is the paper's showpiece of projectibility. From two observations (prosodic isolation, stance meaning), five predictions are derived. Then the paper self-critically grades them: "(a) and (c) are close to entailments"; "(b) involves a genuine cross-domain leap"; "(d) and (e) are the strongest."

**Evaluation.** This is the most persuasive passage in the paper. It earns its place because it does exactly what the paper promises: it shows that categorising a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences. The self-grading is particularly effective. By conceding that some predictions are near-entailments, the paper inoculates itself against the objection that it's merely restating definitional properties. The honest ranking invites the reader to evaluate the strong predictions rather than dismiss the weak ones.

But there is a tension. Prediction (e) -- "Its etymological source is a content word (*brother*) that has undergone phonological reduction and semantic bleaching" -- is flagged as one of the strongest, available "to linguists who recognize the category, if not to naive speakers." This qualifier is important but underplayed. If the inference is available only to specialists, it's a different kind of projectibility from the others, which are available to any competent speaker. The paper needs this distinction for the field-relative architecture (Section 5.2), but it introduces it silently here rather than flagging it as a different epistemic type. The worked example thus smuggles in a shift from speaker-available inference to analyst-available inference without marking the transition.

### Move 4: The Subscript Notation (Interjection_syn, Interjection_sem, Interjection_int)

The subscript notation decomposes "interjection" into three field-relative HPCs. The culminating case is attributive *damn* in *the damn car*: "interjection_sem but not interjection_syn." The notation is called "not just notational" -- it "generates predictions that the individual frameworks can't produce in isolation."

**Evaluation.** This is the paper's most ambitious formal move. It transforms a terminological ambiguity (linguists use "interjection" to mean different things) into a theoretical architecture. The *damn*/*damn!* divergence is the strongest evidence: the subscript notation predicts exactly where the two HPCs converge (standalone *damn!*) and diverge (attributive *the damn car*), and neither CGEL's syntax nor Potts's semantics alone captures the full pattern.

The move earns its place in one respect: it resolves a genuine puzzle (why *damn* behaves differently in different constructions) and provides a principled vocabulary for the resolution. But it also risks overreach. Three subscripted HPCs for a single lexical category is a lot of theoretical machinery for a category the paper itself calls "thin." The paper's own hedging ("thin-but-genuine HPC") sits uneasily with the proliferation of subscripts. If the syntactic HPC is thin, the semantic and interactional HPCs may be thinner still, and the notation may be doing more work than the data can support. The paper gestures at this concern ("A thin syntactic HPC is still a syntactic HPC") but doesn't fully confront it.

### Move 5: The "Thin-but-Genuine" Hedge

The paper repeatedly characterises interjections as a "thin-but-genuine HPC," distinguished from both wastebasket categories (adverb) and robust skeleton categories (noun, verb). The comparison with adjectives is the anchoring analogy: "comparable to adjectives: fewer stabilisers, lower mechanism density, but real convergence and a passed perturbation test."

**Evaluation.** This is a calibration move: it manages expectations. The hedge is strategically positioned after the ambitious subscript architecture, where the reader might reasonably ask whether the paper is overselling. "Thin-but-genuine" answers: thin, yes, but the perturbation test was passed, and adverb (the real wastebasket) is the contrast class.

The risk is that "thin-but-genuine" does too much work. It's simultaneously a description (interjections have fewer stabilisers), an evaluation (but the ones they have are real), and an inoculation (don't judge them against nouns). The adjective comparison is promising but undeveloped: the paper asserts comparability with adjectives without unpacking what makes adjectives thin. If the reader doesn't already know the HPC book's analysis of adjectives, the comparison is an appeal to authority rather than an argument.

## 2. What's Persuasive

The paper's rhetoric is most effective in three places.

**First**, the *bruh* worked example. It is the paper's proof of concept, and the self-grading of predictions (near-entailments distinguished from genuine cross-domain leaps) is a model of intellectual honesty that builds trust. By the end of that passage, the reader believes that categorising something as an interjection does license non-trivial inferences.

**Second**, the *damn*/*damn!* divergence case. The subscript notation earns its keep here because it resolves a real puzzle: the same lexeme behaving differently in different constructions, with the divergence tracking which mechanisms are active. This is not a notational convenience; it is a prediction.

**Third**, the neglect narrative's reversal. The transition from "the category that embarrassed classical theory" to "the strongest evidence for HPC, not the weakest" is a genuine chiasmus of valuation, and it works because the paper delivers on it. By the end of Section 5, the reader can see that the fuzziness that embarrassed classical theory is precisely what HPC is designed to explain. The reversal is not merely asserted; it is earned.

## 3. What's Not Persuasive

**The perturbation test is under-operationalised.** The paper claims falsifiability -- "if no proposed stabiliser's removal would change the clustering, the 'homeostatic' part is empty" -- but the two illustrations (*pop*, *God*) are lexicographic judgments and constructed contrasts, not perturbation results. What would count as "the cluster remains intact"? What would count as a stabiliser's "removal"? Without operationalisation, the perturbation test is a thought experiment presented as a methodology. This matters because the perturbation test is the paper's main discriminator between HPC and prototype theory: "Prototype theory offers no analogous test." If the HPC test is itself notional, the advantage is rhetorical, not empirical.

**The subscript architecture risks multiplication beyond necessity.** Three HPCs for one lexical category requires justification beyond the observation that different fields track different properties. The paper provides one strong case (*damn*) where the subscripts do independent work, but the other subscripts (interjection_int, with *mm-hm* as the key case) are less developed. The claim "these are not three descriptions of the same category" is provocative but not fully defended: the paper needs to show that the three HPCs generate contradictory predictions for specific cases, not just different emphases.

**The "thin-but-genuine" hedge and the subscript architecture work against each other.** The hedge says: interjections are a minor category, don't expect too much. The subscript architecture says: interjections are so rich that a single HPC can't capture them -- you need three. The reader must simultaneously accept that the category is thin and that it is thick enough to fracture into three field-relative kinds. The paper doesn't acknowledge this tension. A sentence addressing it directly -- perhaps arguing that "thin" refers to the syntactic HPC alone while the pragmatic richness is carried by the other two -- would resolve the apparent contradiction.

**P3 (phase transition) is confounded and the paper knows it.** The acknowledgement that "phonological modifications may cluster partly because of taboo-avoidance pressure, not solely because of an intrinsic tipping point" is honest, but it undermines the prediction it accompanies. If the clustering in Gehweiler's data can be explained by an external social pressure (taboo avoidance) rather than by an intrinsic attractor dynamic, then P3 is not confirmed by this data. The paper's language -- "consistent with a phase transition" -- is appropriately cautious, but it sits inside a section whose rhetorical frame is "HPC predictions confirmed." The tension between the hedging and the framing weakens the passage.

## 4. The Paper's Implicit Contract with the Reader

The Introduction promises three things explicitly:

1. Interjections as a test case for HPC "at the margins" -- the embarrassing category is the best evidence.
2. Diachronic mechanisms as both recruitment pathways and synchronic stabilisers.
3. A distinctive form of projectibility -- "primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional."

It also establishes a criterion of success: "This paper succeeds if it shows that categorizing a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes."

**Does the Conclusion deliver?**

On (1): Yes. The reversal is complete. The paper shows that interjections' fuzziness is an HPC prediction, not an anomaly, and the perturbation test (even in its notional form) is more than prototype theory provides.

On (2): Partially. The recruitment pathways (Sections 4.1, 4.2) are well developed. The synchronic stabilisers (Section 4.2) are identified but unevenly supported: prosodic-syntactic coupling has historical evidence (Gehweiler), the non-referentiality ratchet is a plausible mechanism described in a single paragraph, and social indexing is interesting but ends with an open question ("whether social indexing preserves the cluster or merely preserves individual items"). The paper is honest about this unevenness, but the three stabilisers are not equally earned.

On (3): Yes, with a caveat. The pragmatic-kind claim is the paper's most original contribution, and it is grounded in the subscript architecture. But the claim depends on the interactional HPC (interjection_int) being the richest, and that HPC is the least developed of the three. The passage asserting it (Section 5.4) is more programmatic than demonstrative.

On the criterion of success: Yes. The *bruh* example demonstrates non-trivial inference from partial observation. This is the paper's core deliverable, and it is delivered.

The Conclusion also introduces two untested predictions (P5, P10) as "future work." This is standard, but it creates a slight asymmetry: the Introduction promises that "four [predictions] are testable for interjections" (Section 6.1), but only two receive even preliminary evidence, and the other two are deferred. The contract is fulfilled in letter but somewhat stretched in spirit.

---

## 5. Advice on the Paper's Rhetoric as a Whole

The paper's rhetorical structure is well designed and mostly well executed. Its strongest moves -- the neglect narrative, the *bruh* worked example, the *damn*/*damn!* divergence -- are genuinely persuasive because they deliver on specific promises rather than relying on accumulated assertion. Where the paper falters, it falters in the same way each time: it introduces a tool (the perturbation test, the subscript notation, the "thin-but-genuine" hedge) and then uses that tool at slightly more than the evidence warrants. The perturbation test is presented as falsifiability but operationalised as illustration. The subscript notation resolves the *damn* case convincingly but the other subscripts are more programmatic than demonstrated. The "thin-but-genuine" hedge calibrates expectations but also contradicts the subscript architecture's implicit claim that the category is rich enough to fracture. Each of these is a small overreach, and each could be fixed by one or two sentences of explicit qualification. The paper already knows how to self-qualify well (the *bruh* self-grading is a model); it just needs to apply that same discipline to its formal apparatus. The greatest rhetorical risk is not that the paper overclaims -- it mostly doesn't -- but that the reader senses the *potential* for overclaiming and discounts the genuine achievements accordingly.
