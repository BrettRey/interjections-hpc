# CGEL Grammarian Review (Round 2): "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster"

**Reviewer expertise:** English descriptive grammar (CGEL tradition), lexical categories, the category-function distinction, supplement function, peripheral word classes.

**Round:** 2 (reviewing revised outline dated 2026-03-14)

---

## 1. Round 1 concerns addressed?

### Concern A: Interjection phrase structure needs more precision

**Round 1 said:** The outline lumps *oh boy*, *holy cow*, and *damn these mosquitoes* as "interjection phrases" without distinguishing their syntactic structures. Is *oh boy* vocative, modifier-head, or fixed collocation? What licenses an adjective to modify an interjection in *holy cow* / *hot damn*?

**Status: Partially addressed.** The revised outline now gives *damn these mosquitoes* its proper CGEL analysis: "exceptional interjections that combine with an NP complement to form an interjection phrase" (Section 4.1, citing H&P 2002: 1361fn). That is correct and specific. But *oh boy* and *holy cow* still appear as unanalysed examples in Section 3 (syntax bullet). The internal structure question remains unaddressed: is *holy* an adjective modifying *cow* (still nominal), or is *holy cow* a fully lexicalised interjection with no internal constituency? CGEL does not settle this definitively, so the paper could acknowledge the gap. What it should not do is leave the reader assuming all "interjection phrases" have the same structure.

**Residual ask:** One sentence clarifying whether *oh boy* / *holy cow* are treated as internally structured phrases or as lexicalised units. If the analysis is uncertain, say so explicitly.

### Concern B: Syntactic projectibility too thin

**Round 1 said:** Three of the four projectibility inferences are pragmatic/semantic; only supplement function is syntactic. The paper should state positive and negative syntactic distributional facts for interjections with equal precision.

**Status: Addressed.** Section 5.1 now provides concrete syntactic predictions from partial observation of a novel form (*bruh*): non-inflecting, resists syntactic integration (*\*I think that bruh*), resists restrictive modification (*\*very bruh*, *\*the bruh*), available for standalone use. Section 5.3 adds four category-level contrasts with the function label "supplement": non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, diachronic trajectory predictions. These are specific and syntactically grounded. The asymmetry I flagged (positive facts for nouns, only negative facts for interjections) is still present, but the revision is right to lean into the negative distributional facts, because that is genuinely what the category licenses. The negative facts are non-trivial predictions.

### Concern C: Filler categorisation underplayed

**Round 1 said:** The paper reports the O'Connell & Kowal debate without taking a position. Are fillers peripheral members of the interjection cluster or a neighbouring but distinct cluster?

**Status: Partially addressed.** Fillers now appear in Section 6.3 (boundary disputes as mechanism overlap) and in Section 5.2 (lexicographic prediction, noting that dictionaries classify *um* differently). But the paper still does not take a clear position. The closest it comes is the general claim that "routine formulae are partial members of the interjection HPC, sharing enough mechanism support to cluster nearby but not enough to sit at the centre" (Section 6.3). This framing could accommodate fillers, but fillers are not routine formulae (they lack addressee-directedness and social predictability), so the analogy does not transfer automatically.

**Residual ask:** State explicitly whether fillers are (a) peripheral members of the interjection HPC, (b) a neighbouring cluster sharing some stabilisers, or (c) an open question. Any of these is defensible; silence is not.

### Concern D (Key Q&A question): What does the category label add beyond the function label?

**Round 1 asked:** If I know something is a supplement, I already get prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration. What does knowing it is an *interjection* add?

**Status: Directly and effectively addressed.** Section 5.3 is devoted to this question and gives a crisp answer with five specific contrasts between the category label and the function label. The strongest move: "The function label tells you about syntactic integration. The category label tells you about inference -- it licences a package of predictions that no other category-in-supplement-function supports." This is exactly the right framing. The five contrasts (non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, diachronic trajectory) are specific, testable, and correctly capture what CGEL's category-function architecture is designed to handle. This was the most important revision requested, and it has been done well.

## 2. One-sentence summary

The revised outline argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster sustained by diachronic recruitment pathways and synchronic feedback loops, whose distinctive theoretical contribution is that categorising a form as an interjection licenses primarily pragmatic (rather than truth-conditional) inferences that the function label "supplement" alone cannot provide.

---

## 3. Strengths

- **The category-vs-function section (5.3) is now the strongest part of the outline.** It does exactly what the CGEL framework demands: it shows that the category label "interjection" carries inferential content that no function label provides, by listing five specific contrasts. This is the clearest statement I have seen outside of CGEL itself of what lexical category labels *do* that function labels cannot, applied to a peripheral category. The paper now earns its right to use CGEL's architecture as more than a citation.

- **The stabiliser terminology and perturbation test give the mechanism story empirical bite.** Section 4.2's four named feedback loops, each with a specific perturbation consequence, are a genuine improvement. The *pop* example (Meinard 2015) -- reclassified as adverb when syntactically integrated -- is precisely the kind of evidence that shows the stabilisers are doing work. The perturbation test is no longer asserted; it is demonstrated.

- **The partial-observation inference strategy (Section 5.1) breaks the circularity that plagued the earlier version.** The *bruh* example is well chosen: it is novel enough that the reader cannot dismiss the predictions as memorised collocations, and the predictions are specific enough to be falsified. The key move -- observing *some* properties and predicting *others*, grounded in the mechanism that couples them -- is clearly stated.

---

## 4. Weaknesses

- **The treatment of verb-derived interjections (Path 2) still needs more syntactic precision.** Section 4.1 analyses *damn* as retaining an NP complement but losing subject and tense. That is correct, but it understates the syntactic complexity. CGEL notes that *damn* in *damn these mosquitoes* is "exceptional" precisely because complement-taking interjections are vanishingly rare (H&P 2002: 1361fn [needs verification]). The paper should note that *come on* and *look* (also listed in Path 2) do NOT retain complements in their interjection uses -- they have undergone complete syntactic deactivation. This is a genuine contrast between two sub-paths within Path 2, and the outline currently treats them as equivalent. Distinguishing *damn* (partial deactivation: complement retained, subject/tense lost) from *come on* / *look* (full deactivation: no complement, no argument structure) would strengthen the mechanism story: the degree of syntactic deactivation correlates with the degree of integration into the interjection cluster.

- **The four empirical predictions in Section 6.1 are well formulated but asymmetrically testable.** P1 (asymmetric fraying) and P3 (phase transition) require historical corpus data that may not exist at the necessary temporal granularity. P5 (conditioning recovers structure) requires variationist sociolinguistic data. P10 (hub-node asymmetry) requires experimental perturbation or at least corpus-based proxy measures. Only P5 seems immediately testable with existing resources (e.g., GloWbE data already cited in Section 4.3). The paper should be honest about which predictions are testable now and which are programmatic. There is no shame in programmatic predictions (that is what frameworks are for), but conflating the two undermines the "HPC generates predictions, prototype theory does not" argument if none of the predictions can actually be tested in this paper.

- **The Ameka-Wilkins resolution in Section 6.3 may be too quick.** The outline claims that HPC resolves the debate by treating routine formulae as "partial members" sharing some but not all stabilisers. This is a reasonable move, but it risks trivialising the dispute. Ameka (1992: 109--110 [needs verification]) argues that routine formulae differ from interjections in being "context-bound, addressee-directed, and speaker-directed" -- three properties, not one. If those three properties are each linked to distinct stabilisers, then routine formulae may constitute a neighbouring HPC rather than peripheral members of the interjection HPC. The paper should consider both options (peripheral membership vs. neighbouring cluster) and state which it adopts and why. The current formulation ("partial members") presupposes the answer.

---

## 5. Key question

You claim that interjections are a "thin-but-genuine HPC" comparable to adjectives (Section 6.2, citing Reynolds 2026, Ch 11). In CGEL's analysis, adjectives have a rich battery of positive syntactic diagnostics: attributive function, predicative complement function, modification by *very*, comparative/superlative inflection (H&P 2002: Ch 6). Interjections have almost exclusively *negative* diagnostics: no inflection, no argument structure, no embedding. If the thickness of an HPC is measured by the density of inter-property correlations, are interjections genuinely comparable to adjectives, or are they thinner still? What category on the Reynolds typology would have *fewer* stabilisers than interjections, and is there one?

---

## 6. Verdict

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The major structural and theoretical concerns from Round 1 have been addressed, several of them decisively. The category-vs-function analysis (Section 5.3) is now genuinely strong. The remaining issues -- verb-derived sub-path distinction, testability transparency for the four predictions, and the Ameka-Wilkins resolution -- are all addressable in drafting without restructuring. The outline is ready to be written up.
