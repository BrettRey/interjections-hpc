# Round 2 Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer expertise:** Historical pragmatics, grammaticalization, semantic change, diachronic pragmatics
**Target journal:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics (John Benjamins)
**Round:** 2 (revised outline)

---

## 1. Round 1 concerns addressed?

### Concern 1: Diachronic mechanisms section relied too heavily on a single case study (Gehweiler 2008 on *gee!*).

**Addressed.** Section 4.1 now analyses two distinct bleaching trajectories in comparable detail: Path 1 (religious noun > interjection, Gehweiler 2008) and Path 2 (verb > interjection, *damn*, with CGEL's complement-retention analysis). The two paths have genuinely different entry mechanisms (phonological modification vs. syntactic deactivation), which is exactly what was needed. The addition of *come on* and *look* as further verb-path exemplars strengthens the claim that the cluster is fed from multiple directions. This was the most important revision and it was done well.

### Concern 2: Engagement with grammaticalization theory was gestural rather than substantive.

**Partially addressed.** The revised outline now explicitly invokes Traugott & Dasher's Invited Inferencing Theory of Semantic Change and maps the subjectification scale (content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective) onto both bleaching paths. The addition of Heine & Kuteva (2002) to the key sources list signals intent to engage. However, the outline still does not resolve the tension I flagged: is interjection formation a species of grammaticalization in Traugott's sense, or something categorically different? Path 1 (Gehweiler) explicitly distinguishes gradual Invited Inferencing (speaker-based) from abrupt reanalysis (hearer-based), and the outline acknowledges this, but does not take a position. For JHP, this is not optional. The paper needs to say whether interjection formation is grammaticalization, degrammaticalization, or a sui generis process, and defend that claim. The outline gestures toward "pragmatic conventionalisation" as the umbrella but does not interrogate whether all interjection formation fits under it. [Note: I have not re-read Gehweiler 2008 or Traugott & Dasher 2002 for this review; claims about their specific arguments need verification against the sources.]

### Concern 3: Boundary-dispute material risked becoming a catalogue rather than an argument.

**Partially addressed.** Section 6.3 now frames boundary disputes as "mechanism overlap," which is a genuine analytical move beyond "boundaries are graded." The Ameka-Wilkins resolution (routine formulae as partial members sharing some but not all stabilisers) is exactly right and gives the boundary material argumentative teeth. However, the diachronic reframing I requested is still largely absent: the outline does not ask whether routine formulae are *entering* or *leaving* the interjection cluster over time, nor whether the direction of boundary-crossing tells us something about mechanism strength. For JHP, the synchronic framing of Section 6.3 is adequate but not ideal. The diachronic question about directionality of boundary-crossing would make it stronger.

### Concern 4: Recruitment vs. maintenance conflation.

**Fully addressed.** This was the most impressive structural revision. Section 4 is now explicitly split into 4.1 (recruitment pathways, diachronic) and 4.2 (synchronic feedback loops, maintenance), with a clear statement that Boyd's framework requires the latter and the former explains the trajectory. The four named feedback loops (prosodic-syntactic coupling, non-referentiality cascade, onomatopoeia chain, social indexing) are specific, individually arguable, and each has a perturbation-test consequence. The perturbation test in particular (what happens if you neutralise prosodic isolation? the form starts behaving like a verb or adverb) is exactly what my Round 1 Q&A question was probing. This resolves my central concern.

### Concern 5: HPC vs. prototype comparison was undeveloped.

**Fully addressed.** Section 2.2 now provides a substantive comparison that goes beyond "prototype theory describes gradience, HPC explains it." The four empirical predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10) with named defeat conditions in Section 6.1 are a genuine contribution. The perturbation test as a discriminator between the two frameworks is well-articulated. This is now a strength rather than a weakness.

### Additional notes from Round 1:

- **Gehweiler (2008) published in JHP:** Not explicitly addressed in the revision text, but the expanded engagement with Gehweiler suggests the connection will be made.
- **Dingemanse (2020) compatibility with HPC realism:** Now briefly addressed in Section 4.3 (the "landscape" metaphor is given HPC content). Still needs a paragraph in the paper itself.
- **COHA/GloWbE data presented quantitatively:** Still gestural ("COHA data," "GloWbE"). The outline does not commit to figures or quantitative analysis. For JHP, this remains a gap.
- **Heine & Kuteva (2002):** Now in the key sources list. Good.

## 2. One-sentence summary

The revised outline argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster, sustained by both diachronic recruitment pathways (semantic bleaching via multiple entry mechanisms) and synchronic feedback loops (prosodic-syntactic coupling, non-referentiality cascades, social indexing), yielding primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional projectibility.

---

## 3. Strengths

- **The recruitment/maintenance distinction (Sections 4.1--4.2) is now the structural backbone of the diachronic argument.** The four named feedback loops are specific enough to be individually falsifiable, and the perturbation test gives each one empirical consequences. This is what a JHP reviewer wants to see: not just "bleaching happens" but "here is why the cluster coheres once items are in it." The *pop* example (Meinard 2015, reclassified as adverb when prosodically integrated) is a particularly effective illustration of what perturbation looks like in practice.

- **The projectibility argument has been transformed from a climactic afterthought into the organising question.** Threading it through Sections 1, 2, 3, and 5 solves the structural problem. The partial-observation inference move (Section 5.1) breaks the circularity I did not flag but would have: observing *some* properties in *bruh* and predicting *others* is genuinely non-circular and concretely demonstrates what the category buys you. The field-relative projectibility breakdown (interactional, diachronic, lexicographic) is a smart way to make the abstract point tangible for different readerships.

- **The empirical predictions with defeat conditions (Section 6.1) give the paper testable commitments.** P3 (phase transition in genesis) is particularly well-suited to JHP: it asks whether there is a historical tipping point in bleaching trajectories, which is an empirically addressable question using corpus data. Stating the defeat condition ("historical data shows monotonic, continuous co-change with no inflection point") shows the author is not merely fitting the framework to the data post hoc.

---

## 4. Weaknesses

- **The paper still has not decided its position on the grammaticalization question.** Is interjection formation grammaticalization? The outline invokes Traugott & Dasher's IITSC and applies the subjectification scale, but does not commit. Path 1 (noun > interjection) involves loss of referential content and gain of procedural/expressive meaning, which looks like grammaticalization. Path 2 (verb > interjection via *damn*) involves loss of syntactic structure (no subject, no tense) but retention of complement-taking ability, which looks like partial degrammaticalization or something else entirely. The onomatopoeia pathway (Section 4.2, loop 3) is not grammaticalization at all: nonce coinages enter the cluster without any prior lexical history. A JHP reviewer will press on this: are there three different processes here, and if so, what unifies them beyond "they produce interjections"? The HPC answer should be that the unifier is the *cluster itself* (convergent outcomes from different pathways), but the outline needs to say this explicitly and address whether that convergence is grammaticalization or something that subsumes it.

- **The corpus evidence remains promissory.** COHA for *nay*/*yeah*, GloWbE for regional variants, Gehweiler's corpus data for phase transitions -- all are mentioned but none are specified as to methodology or presentation format. For JHP, even a simple frequency plot would be stronger than a textual assertion. If the paper cannot include quantitative corpus analysis (understandable at outline stage), it should at minimum specify what data will be presented and in what form. The P3 prediction (phase transition) is only testable if the diachronic data is granular enough to distinguish a tipping point from a smooth cline. What time resolution does COHA offer for the relevant forms? [Note: I have not verified COHA's coverage of these specific items.]

- **Section 5.3 (category vs. function) may be too CGEL-internal for JHP's readership.** The argument that "interjection" (category) adds inferential content beyond "supplement" (function) is important within the CGEL framework, but JHP's readership may not share the assumption that the category/function distinction is the right one to be making. Many JHP readers will come from Construction Grammar, interactional linguistics, or usage-based traditions where the category/function separation is less central. The argument needs to be framed in terms that do not presuppose CGEL architecture, or it needs a brief justification of why the category/function distinction matters for diachronic analysis specifically.

---

## 5. Key question

You identify four synchronic feedback loops as maintenance mechanisms. But only one of them (social indexing, loop 4) is clearly *diachronic* in the sense that it operates over historical time and is empirically traceable. The others (prosodic-syntactic coupling, non-referentiality cascade, onomatopoeia chain) are stated as synchronic generalisations that presumably *have* diachronic consequences, but their diachronic operation is not demonstrated. For a paper in JHP, can you provide historical evidence that these feedback loops have been operative across attested periods of English? For instance, is there evidence from Middle English or Early Modern English that prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration co-evolved, rather than one simply following the other? Without diachronic attestation, the "homeostatic" claim rests on synchronic plausibility, which is a weaker foundation for this journal.

---

## 6. Verdict: **Accept with minor revisions**

**Justification:** The revision has addressed the central structural and argumentative weaknesses of the original outline. The recruitment/maintenance distinction, the multiple bleaching paths, the perturbation test, the non-circular projectibility argument, and the empirical predictions with defeat conditions collectively produce an outline that is ready for drafting. The remaining weaknesses (position on grammaticalization, corpus evidence specificity, CGEL-internal framing of 5.3) are issues that can be resolved during the writing process and do not require further structural revision. This is now a paper that JHP should want to publish: it extends a paper published in JHP (Gehweiler 2008), addresses a perennial problem in historical pragmatics (how categories are sustained diachronically), and offers a theoretical framework that generates testable predictions. The author should attend to the grammaticalization question early in the drafting process, as it will determine the shape of Section 4's argument.

## Additional notes

1. **Frame the paper explicitly as extending Gehweiler (2008).** The outline now does substantial work with Gehweiler, but the introduction should state clearly: "This paper extends the single-case analysis of Gehweiler (2008, published in this journal) to a general theoretical claim about the interjection category." This positions the paper as a natural sequel in JHP's own lineage.

2. **The "thin-but-genuine HPC" framing (Section 6.2) is an important hedge.** It pre-empts the obvious objection that interjections are a weaker case than nouns or verbs. By placing interjections on a typology of word-class robustness (comparable to adjectives, not to the wastebasket "adverb"), the paper avoids overclaiming. Keep this.

3. **The *bruh* example (Section 5.1) is excellent.** It is contemporary, instantly recognisable, and demonstrates partial-observation inference concretely. Consider whether a second example from a different register or historical period would strengthen the case -- perhaps a 19th-century interjection whose trajectory is now completed.

4. **Engage explicitly with Norrick (2009) if applicable.** [Source grounding flag: I have not verified whether Norrick 2009 addresses interjections as a category; this is a suggestion to check, not a confirmed citation.] The conversational-routine and interjection literature in JHP's orbit may include work that should be acknowledged even if not centrally engaged.
