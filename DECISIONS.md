# DECISIONS.md -- English Interjections as HPC

2026-03-14 — Target journal: Journal of Historical Pragmatics (Benjamins). Chosen for fit with diachronic dimension (semantic bleaching paths, nay→yeah shift) and because JHP already publishes interjection work (Gehweiler 2008 on gee!). Brett already has multiple papers in ELL and JL.

2026-03-14 — The HPC angle on interjections: the property cluster (non-referential, non-inflecting, prosodically isolated, syntactically supplemental, emotively laden) is maintained by identifiable mechanisms (semantic bleaching, prosodic isolation reinforcing syntactic independence). The distinctive contribution is that interjections push HPC harder than "respectable" categories because they're widely denied or marginalised as a category. The projectibility question is unusual: inference is primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional.

2026-03-14 — Primary empirical base: Brett's Wikipedia "Good Article" on English interjections, which covers phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics, variation, and the history of the category in English grammars. Corpus data from GloWbE and COHA already present.

2026-03-14 — Paper structure: problem-first (neglect narrative, then empirical richness, then HPC as solution). Diachronic emphasis (foregrounding bleaching paths and mechanism trajectories as JHP centrepiece). Projectibility as climax of HPC section (cluster → mechanisms → projectibility). Boundary disputes integrated throughout rather than a separate section.

2026-03-14 — Outline approved (OUTLINE.md). Seven sections: Introduction, HPC theory in brief, The property cluster, Diachronic mechanisms, Projectibility, Discussion, Conclusion. Estimated ~13--15 pages.

2026-03-14 — Review board (5 reviewers, all R&R) identified 5 consensus issues: (1) projectibility structurally subordinate to mechanisms, (2) projectibility section circular, (3) recruitment != maintenance, (4) need more bleaching paths, (5) need HPC-vs-prototype differentiation. Key contradiction: projectibility reviewer wants it as organising question vs. historical pragmaticist wants more mechanism depth. Resolution: mechanisms are the centrepiece content, projectibility is the question they answer.

2026-03-14 — HPC book consultation (Ch 6, 7, 8, 11, Appendix). 2026-03-14 — Field-relative projectibility reframed using subscript notation: interjection_syn (syntactic, CGEL), interjection_sem (semantic, overlapping Potts's expressives), interjection_int (interactional, overlapping response tokens/liminal signs). The subscript notation makes explicit that these are distinct HPCs with overlapping but non-identical extensions, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes. Parallels the book's noun/name distinction but uses subscripts rather than different words because every field uses the term "interjection." Replaces the weaker "three fields' predictions" framing. Potts (2007) / Kaplan (1999) on expressives need source verification before drafting.
