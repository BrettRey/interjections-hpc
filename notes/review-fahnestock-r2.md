# Round 2 Rhetorical Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer persona:** Jeanne Fahnestock, author of *Rhetorical Figures in Science*
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Reviewed:** Restructured draft (post-Round 1 revisions)

---

## 1. Round 1 Concerns Addressed?

My Round 1 review raised four substantive problems. Here is where each stands.

**The perturbation test as thought experiment, not methodology.** Round 1 objected that the perturbation test was "presented as a methodology" but "operationalised as illustration." The revised paper has *not* changed the perturbation test's epistemic status; *pop* and *God* remain the two cases, and neither is operationalised beyond lexicographic judgment. But the paper has done something subtler. Section 3.1 now frames the perturbation test as a *prediction format*: "if a proposed stabiliser can be neutralized and the cluster remains intact, the stabiliser wasn't doing the work claimed for it." The key word is "yields": the test is presented as a consequence of the stabiliser concept, not as an independent methodology. This is an improvement. It stops short of claiming experimental status and instead positions the test as a *criterion of adequacy* for proposed stabilisers. I would still like a sentence in Section 4.3 acknowledging that the two illustrations are informal demonstrations rather than controlled perturbations, but the framing shift is genuine and reduces the overreach I identified.

**"Thin-but-genuine" vs. three-HPCs tension.** Round 1 flagged that the "thin-but-genuine" hedge and the subscript architecture work against each other: thin implies modest, three HPCs implies rich. The revised paper does not explicitly address this tension. The passage in Section 6.2 still reads: "They aren't a robust skeleton category like nouns or verbs... But they are a genuine thin HPC, comparable to adjectives." This comes *after* Section 5, which has just demonstrated that three distinct HPCs with non-identical extensions track the same lexemes. The reader who remembers the subscript architecture will ask: thin according to which subscript? The paper *implicitly* resolves this (Section 5.4 argues that "thin" applies to interjection_syn, while the pragmatic richness is carried by the other two), but it never says so explicitly at the point where the tension is sharpest. One sentence in Section 6.2 would fix this: something like "The thinness is syntactic; the pragmatic and interactional HPCs are richer, which is precisely why the subscript decomposition is needed." Unaddressed.

**P3 confound undermining the section's framing.** Round 1 noted that the honest acknowledgement of the taboo-avoidance confound sits inside a section whose rhetorical frame is "HPC predictions confirmed." The revised paper has improved this. The framing in Section 6.1 is now more measured: "The evidence already in hand is consistent with the prediction" (for P1), "Gehweiler's data speaks to this" (for P3), and "preliminary evidence" (for P10). The explicit defeat conditions ("Defeat: historical data shows monotonic, continuous co-change with no inflection point") help, and the confound is stated clearly. The section no longer reads as "confirmed"; it reads as "consistent but not settled." This is a real fix.

**The subscript architecture under-defended for interjection_int.** Round 1 asked for contradictory predictions across the three HPCs, not just different emphases. The *damn*/*damn!* case remains the only fully worked divergence, and interjection_int is still the thinnest of the three (the *mm-hm* case is gestured at but not developed). The paper has not expanded this. The risk is reduced, however, because the revised paper's rhetoric is less assertive about the three-HPC architecture: "The subscript notation makes explicit that these are distinct HPCs, not subtypes of a single category" is a claim about *notation*, not a strong ontological commitment. The reader can take the subscripts as a useful heuristic even if interjection_int isn't fully demonstrated. Partially addressed.

## 2. The Paper's Implicit Contract

The restructured Introduction promises:

- (a) Interjections as a test case for HPC "at the margins."
- (b) Diachronic mechanisms extending Gehweiler's single-case analysis to a general claim.
- (c) A distinctive form of projectibility, "primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional."
- A criterion of success: categorizing a novel form as an interjection should license non-trivial inferences for specific purposes.

**Does the Conclusion deliver?**

On (a): fully. The neglect narrative's reversal is complete, and the paper earns the reversal through the perturbation test and the boundary-dispute analysis (Section 6.3). The Conclusion's opening sentence ("English interjections aren't marginal or anomalous") lands because the preceding 25 pages have demonstrated it.

On (b): substantially. The paper adds Path 2 (verb-to-interjection via syntactic deactivation) and the convergent-pathways argument (Section 4.1.3), generalising Gehweiler's single case to a claim about convergent recruitment. The onomatopoeia/nonce-coinage pathway (Section 4.4) is the strongest extension because it is categorically different from the bleaching paths: no prior lexical history, no subjectification, yet the same cluster profile. The Conclusion's sentence about "three diachronically distinct processes" accurately reflects what was delivered.

On (c): delivered but fragile. The pragmatic-kind argument (Section 5.4) is the paper's most original contribution. Its grounding, however, depends on a chain: mechanisms generate predictions within the domains they operate in; the interjection mechanisms are prosodic, interactional, and social; therefore the predictions are pragmatic. This chain is valid but each link is asserted in a sentence or two rather than demonstrated. The Conclusion restates the claim without strengthening it. The reader who is already persuaded by the *bruh* example will accept this; the sceptical reader may feel the "pragmatic kind" label outruns its evidence.

On the criterion of success: met. The *bruh* worked example (Section 5.1) is the paper's proof of concept and remains its most convincing passage. The self-grading of predictions (near-entailments vs. genuine cross-domain leaps) is still the paper's rhetorical high point.

One new concern. The Conclusion introduces the phrase "the kind of thing historical pragmatics is for" as a closing flourish aimed at JHP's readership. The phrase is effective as a bid for disciplinary relevance, but it makes a promissory claim that the paper hasn't fully earned: the paper argues that diachronic mechanisms recruit items into the interjection cluster, but it doesn't demonstrate that historical pragmatics *as a field* has the tools to test the HPC predictions (P5, P10) that remain open. The closing gesture slightly overextends the contract.

## 3. Remaining Rhetorical Problems

### Problem 1: The thin/thick contradiction remains unmarked

This is the residue of my Round 1 concern. The paper calls interjections "thin-but-genuine" (Section 6.2) after having argued that they require three subscripted HPCs (Section 3.3). The implicit resolution is that "thin" applies to the syntactic HPC alone, but the paper never states this at the moment when the tension is most salient. The result is that the reader must reconstruct the resolution herself, and a less charitable reader may not bother. The fix is one sentence in Section 6.2, inserted after "comparable to adjectives": clarifying that the thinness is a property of interjection_syn, while the pragmatic and interactional HPCs carry the richer inferential load. This would also reinforce the pragmatic-kind argument from Section 5.4.

### Problem 2: The *bruh* example grades predictions but doesn't grade evidence

The *bruh* self-grading is effective because it distinguishes near-entailments from genuine cross-domain leaps. But it grades the *predictions* without grading the *evidence* for those predictions. Predictions (a)-(c) are testable by any competent speaker of English. Prediction (d) (interactional behaviour from a phonological observation) is testable only by observing actual conversations. Prediction (e) (etymological source) is available only to linguists. The passage silently shifts from speaker-competence evidence to corpus evidence to specialist knowledge without flagging the transition. This matters because the paper's criterion of success is about "specific purposes," and the purposes differ across the predictions. A brief parenthetical noting the evidential difference -- perhaps distinguishing speaker-available from analyst-available predictions -- would sharpen the passage and anticipate a reviewer who asks "projectible for whom?"

### Problem 3: Section 4.2 stabilisers are unevenly developed

Of the three synchronic stabilisers, the prosodic-syntactic coupling has historical evidence from Gehweiler, the non-referentiality ratchet gets a single paragraph of mechanistic description, and social indexing ends with an open question ("whether social indexing preserves the cluster or merely preserves individual items"). The paper is honest about the unevenness, but the architecture creates an expectation of parallel development that the text doesn't meet. This is not a factual problem (the honest open question is better than a fabricated answer), but it is a *pacing* problem: the reader arrives at the perturbation test (Section 4.3) having been convinced by one stabiliser, intrigued by another, and uncertain about the third. The section's persuasive force is front-loaded. A transitional sentence before Section 4.3, acknowledging that the stabilisers are at different stages of evidential support, would manage the reader's expectations and prevent the perturbation test from seeming to confirm all three equally.

---

## 4. Advice

The restructured paper is substantially better than the version I reviewed in Round 1. The P3 confound is now honestly framed, the defeat conditions are named rather than implied, and the self-grading of the *bruh* predictions remains the paper's most persuasive rhetorical move. What the paper still needs is *internal cross-reference at moments of tension*. The thin/thick contradiction (Section 6.2 vs. Section 3.3) is resolvable by one sentence that connects the two passages. The evidence-grading gap in the *bruh* example is fixable by a parenthetical. The stabiliser unevenness is manageable by a transitional sentence. None of these requires new argumentation or new data; they require the paper to say explicitly what it already implies. The paper's distinctive strength -- its willingness to self-qualify -- is already in place. It needs to apply that same discipline at three more pressure points. Do that, and the paper's rhetoric will match its ambition.
