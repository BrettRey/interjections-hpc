# Projectibility Review, Round 2: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer role:** Projectibility specialist
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Round:** 2 (revised outline)

## 1. Round 1 concerns addressed?

### Concern 1: Projectibility structurally subordinate to mechanisms

**Addressed.** This was the central structural complaint: the mechanisms section was labelled "the JHP centrepiece" and ran 3--4 pages, while projectibility got 1.5--2 pages and came late. The revision makes three structural changes that together solve the problem. First, the strategy label is now "projectibility as organising question," not "projectibility as climax." Second, Section 1 reframes the paper's central question as "what does categorising something as an interjection let you *predict*?" Third, Section 2.1 states an explicit criterion of success: "the paper succeeds if it shows that categorising a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes. If it merely lists co-occurring properties, it has described a cluster, not demonstrated a kind." This criterion then structures everything that follows. Section 3 evaluates each property for its *inferential contribution*, not just its co-occurrence. Section 5 is expanded (2--2.5 pages, now with four subsections) and explicitly connects back to the criterion. The mechanisms section (now retitled "Stabilisers") remains substantial (3.5--4 pages), but this is appropriate for a JHP paper where diachronic detail is expected. The difference is that the mechanisms are now framed as *explaining why the inferences succeed*, not as being the point in themselves. The sentence "the first two legs matter *because they make the third possible*" (Section 2.1) sets the right frame.

**Verdict: addressed.**

### Concern 2: Projectibility section circular

**Addressed.** Round 1 identified a specific circularity: the projectibility section amounted to "if X is an interjection, it has interjection properties." The revision breaks this circularity via three distinct moves, each substantive.

First, Section 5.1 introduces partial-observation inference with the *bruh* example. The key move: observing *some* cluster properties (prosodic isolation, stance meaning) in a novel form licenses inference to *other* properties (non-inflection, resistance to syntactic integration, resistance to restrictive modification, etymological bleaching). This is genuine projectibility -- the inference goes beyond the observed properties because the maintaining mechanisms couple them. The sentence "the predictions succeed because the maintaining mechanisms couple the properties" connects the mechanisms section forward to the inferential payoff, which was exactly what was missing in Round 1.

Second, Section 5.2 specifies three concrete inferential purposes (interactional, diachronic, lexicographic) -- precisely the candidates I flagged in my Round 1 review. Each purpose is developed with a specific prediction and a named source. This answers "for whom and for what purpose."

Third, Section 5.3 draws the category-vs-function distinction (what does "interjection" add beyond "supplement"?), which is a non-trivial question in the CGEL tradition and provides genuine theoretical content. The answer -- that the category label licenses a package of predictions that no other category-in-supplement-function supports -- is exactly the right framing.

**Verdict: addressed.**

### Concern 3: Recruitment conflated with maintenance

**Addressed.** Section 4 is now split into 4.1 (recruitment pathways), 4.2 (synchronic feedback loops), and 4.3 (dynamic stability). The opening paragraph states the distinction explicitly: "how items *enter* the cluster (diachronic recruitment) and what keeps the cluster *coherent* (synchronic maintenance)." The perturbation test is applied in 4.2 with two concrete examples (*pop* reclassified when prosodic isolation is neutralised; *God* reverting to noun when referential content is restored). This is clean.

**Verdict: addressed.**

### Concern 4: Need second bleaching path

**Addressed.** Section 4.1 now analyses two trajectories: Path 1 (religious noun > interjection, Gehweiler 2008) and Path 2 (verb > interjection, *damn*, *come on*, *look*). The different entry mechanisms (phonological modification for Path 1 vs. syntactic deactivation for Path 2) show that the cluster is fed from multiple directions, which is a genuine HPC prediction (multiple pathways to the same attractor basin).

**Verdict: addressed.**

### Concern 5: HPC vs. prototype undeveloped

**Addressed.** Section 2.2 now makes the comparison substantive: prototype theory captures gradience but generates no predictions about (a) the order of property erosion, (b) tipping points in category genesis, (c) whether apparent gradience decomposes under conditioning, (d) hub-node asymmetry. Section 6.1 follows through with four testable predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10), each with a named defeat condition. The framing is right: prototype theory accommodates the data; HPC generates predictions from it. The perturbation test is offered as a discriminator that prototype theory cannot replicate.

**Verdict: addressed.**

### Round 1 recommendation (specific inferential purposes with worked examples)

**Addressed.** Section 5.1 provides the *bruh* worked example with five specific predictions. Section 5.2 provides three field-specific inferential purposes (interactional, diachronic, lexicographic), each with a concrete prediction and a consequence. This is exactly what was missing.

**Verdict: addressed.**

### Round 1 recommendation ("pragmatic kind" needs argumentation, not assertion)

**Substantially addressed.** Section 5.4 now develops the "pragmatic kind" claim with a paragraph that defines the concept (projectibility about *use* rather than *denotation*), connects it to the content/procedural distinction (Traugott & Dasher), and provides the colour/animacy analogy (different HPCs project in different domains, determined by the maintaining mechanisms). This is no longer a bare assertion. See residual concern below.

**Verdict: substantially addressed.**

## 2. One-sentence summary

The revised outline argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine HPC whose projectibility is distinctively pragmatic -- licensing non-trivial inferences about prosodic behaviour, interactional distribution, and stance expression from partial observation of cluster properties -- and that the diachronic recruitment pathways and synchronic feedback loops explain *why* those inferences succeed.

---

## 3. Strengths

- **Projectibility now genuinely organises the paper.** The criterion of success in Section 2.1 ("the paper succeeds if...") functions as a contract with the reader, and the paper's structure honours it. Section 3 evaluates each property for inferential contribution. Section 5 pays off the promise. The conclusion returns to the organising question. This is a paper about what the category lets you predict, with mechanisms as supporting evidence for why the predictions work. That is the correct framing.

- **The partial-observation inference move (Section 5.1) is the strongest single addition.** The *bruh* example concretely demonstrates that observing prosodic isolation and stance meaning in a novel form licenses inference to non-inflection, resistance to embedding, resistance to modification, and etymological bleaching. These predictions go beyond restating the cluster and are non-trivially coupled by the maintaining mechanisms. This is Boyd's point made concrete. It also shows that projectibility is not retrospective classification but prospective inference -- exactly the distinction that separates HPC from taxonomy.

- **The category-vs-function distinction (Section 5.3) is a genuinely novel contribution to the CGEL literature.** The question "what does the category label 'interjection' add beyond the function label 'supplement'?" has not, to my knowledge, been asked in these terms. The answer -- that the category label licenses a package of predictions (non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, diachronic trajectory) that no other category-in-supplement-function supports -- cleanly demonstrates the inferential surplus of the HPC category label. This will land with syntacticians and is a genuine two-audience contribution.

---

## 4. Weaknesses

- **The "pragmatic kind" claim (Section 5.4) remains the thinnest part of the projectibility argument.** It is now developed (not merely asserted), but it still reads as a parallel to colour/animacy rather than an independent argument. The claim that interjections are a "pragmatic kind" -- that their domain of projectibility is interactional use rather than denotation -- is the paper's most theoretically ambitious contribution, and it deserves more than a single subsection. Two specific gaps: (a) the claim that the domain of projectibility is "set by the maintaining mechanisms" needs a worked-through example showing *how* the mechanisms determine the domain, not just the assertion that they do; (b) the connection to Traugott and Dasher's content/procedural distinction is gestured at ("interjections are procedural, not conceptual") but not developed. Does the HPC framework predict that procedural meaning categories will be pragmatic kinds? If so, are there other procedural-meaning categories that are also pragmatic HPCs? If not, what is special about interjections? Without this, the "pragmatic kind" concept risks being a relabelling rather than a genuine theoretical extension. [Note: the Traugott & Dasher claim needs source verification -- I have not read the source and cannot confirm the content/procedural framing is theirs.]

- **The four predictions in Section 6.1 are appropriate but untested, and the paper does not commit to testing them.** The predictions (asymmetric fraying, phase transition, conditioning recovers structure, hub-node asymmetry) are well-chosen and each has a defeat condition. But the outline does not indicate whether the paper will present any evidence for or against them, or whether they are offered as a research agenda. If the latter, the paper risks the criticism that HPC generates predictions in principle but has not been shown to generate confirmed predictions for interjections. Even one prediction partially supported by existing evidence (e.g., P1 tested against the *damn* case, where complement retention suggests that syntactic properties erode before prosodic ones) would significantly strengthen the argument. The outline has the resources to do this -- the Gehweiler four-stage model and the CGEL *damn* analysis both speak to P1 and P3 -- but does not exploit them.

- **Section 5.2 lists three fields' inferential purposes (interactional, diachronic, lexicographic), but the lexicographic case is weaker than the other two.** The interactional prediction (Stivers 2019) is specific and testable: turn-initial distribution, backchannel availability, intonation contour. The diachronic prediction (Gehweiler 2008) is specific and testable: etymological bleaching, phonological divergence, constrained trajectory. The lexicographic prediction ("resist standard part-of-speech diagnostics, lack inflectional paradigms, require pragmatic glossing") is less a prediction and more a description of existing lexicographic difficulty. A lexicographer already knows these things about interjections; the HPC label does not generate a new inference for them so much as explain why they have the trouble they have. Consider whether the lexicographic case is better framed as "explanation of existing difficulty" rather than "prediction," or whether a different third field (e.g., language teaching, NLP/computational pragmatics, or child language acquisition) would provide a more genuinely predictive purpose.

---

## 5. Key question I would ask at Q&A

"You introduce the concept of a 'pragmatic kind' -- a category whose projectibility is about interactional use rather than denotation. This is a theoretical extension of HPC, not just an application. My question: is 'pragmatic kind' a natural kind *of* kinds, or is it just a label for where interjections happen to fall on a continuum of projectibility domains? Put differently: does HPC theory predict that there should be pragmatic kinds, or does it merely accommodate them? If it predicts them, what is the mechanism-level explanation for why some categories project pragmatically? If it merely accommodates them, the 'pragmatic kind' concept does less theoretical work than the paper claims."

---

## 6. Verdict

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The revision has addressed every Round 1 concern. Projectibility is now the organising question, not the climax. The circularity is broken by partial-observation inference and field-relative purposes. Recruitment and maintenance are cleanly distinguished. The HPC-vs-prototype comparison is substantive and generates testable predictions. The "pragmatic kind" claim is developed rather than merely asserted.

The three residual weaknesses are real but manageable at the drafting stage: (a) the "pragmatic kind" subsection needs another half-page of argumentation connecting it to the content/procedural literature; (b) at least one of the four predictions should be partially supported with existing evidence rather than left as a research agenda; (c) the lexicographic purpose case should be reconsidered or reframed. None of these require structural reorganisation. The outline is ready to draft.
