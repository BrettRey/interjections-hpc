**Approved:** 2026-03-14

## 1. Introduction (~1.5 pages)

- The neglect narrative: Bullokar (1586) included interjections; by the 20th century they're denied (Jespersen 1924), dismissed (H&P Student's 2005), or conceded as "marginal and anomalous" (Quirk et al. 1985: 67).
- The problem: classical category theory demands necessary and sufficient conditions. Interjections resist this, so they get marginalised.
- Reframe: the paper's central question is not just "are interjections a category?" but "what does categorising something as an interjection let you *predict*?" A category that supports no inferences is a filing label, not a kind (Reynolds 2026; cf. Peirce's "the meaning of a sign is the habit it produces").
- Preview the solution: HPC theory offers a framework where graded membership, fuzzy boundaries, and multiple mechanisms are *predicted*, not anomalous. The contribution is threefold: (a) interjections as a test case for HPC at the margins, (b) the diachronic mechanisms that sustain the cluster, (c) a distinctive form of projectibility -- primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional.
- Roadmap.

## 2. HPC theory and why projectibility matters (~2 pages)

### 2.1 The framework

- Boyd (1999): natural kinds as property clusters sustained by homeostatic mechanisms, not by essences.
- Three-part slogan: "a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes" (Reynolds 2026). The first two legs matter *because they make the third possible*. Projectibility is the payoff. "A category just is whatever supports reliable inference across novel instances. The cluster is the visible trace; the inferential role is the substance" (Reynolds 2026, Ch 7).
- **Stabiliser terminology** (Reynolds 2026, Ch 6): "something counts as a stabiliser if removing it would change the clustering -- if the category would fragment, drift, or dissolve." A stabiliser is a mechanism insofar as it has causal depth, but the term foregrounds maintenance. The perturbation test: what happens if you remove a proposed stabiliser?
- **Criterion of success for this paper:** the paper succeeds if it shows that categorising a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes. If it merely lists co-occurring properties, it has described a cluster, not demonstrated a kind.

### 2.2 HPC vs. prototype theory

- **What prototype theory captures:** graded membership, better-and-worse exemplars, fuzzy boundaries via overlapping similarity spaces (Rosch 1975; Taylor 2003). Prototype theory was developed for lexical categories and handles the gradience data well.
- **What prototype theory misses:** mechanisms and mechanism-derived predictions. Prototype theory describes *that* properties cluster but not *why* they cluster in the pattern they do. It generates no predictions about:
  - The *order* in which properties erode when items leave the category (asymmetric fraying, Reynolds 2026 P1)
  - Whether category genesis shows tipping points or smooth clines (phase transition, Reynolds 2026 P3)
  - Whether apparent gradience decomposes under social conditioning (Reynolds 2026 P5)
  - Whether one property is more central to cluster stability than others (hub-node asymmetry, Reynolds 2026 P10)
- These predictions are tested for interjections in Section 6. Prototype theory cannot generate them.
- **The perturbation test as discriminator:** The interjection-as-HPC claim is falsifiable: if no proposed stabiliser's removal would change the clustering, the "homeostatic" part is empty (Reynolds 2026, D1). Prototype theory offers no analogous test.

### 2.3 Field-relative projectibility

- Boyd (1999: 150): "A kind may be natural 'from the point of view of' some discipline or disciplinary matrix but not 'from the point of view of' another." His example: jade is a natural kind in gemology but not in geology (jadeite and nephrite are chemically distinct). Natural kinds are relative to disciplinary matrices. Reynolds (2026, Ch 7) develops this for linguistics: "there are genuinely different HPCs that happen to overlap in extension, each projectible in its own domain." The book's noun/name example: syntacticians track *noun* (heads NPs, takes determiners, inflects for number); semanticists track *name* (picks out individuals, resists descriptive modification, creates referential opacity). Same words, different HPCs, different mechanisms, different projectibility. Colour projects for ecological purposes but not grammatical ones; animacy projects for grammatical purposes (Prasertsom, Smith & Culbertson 2026; cf. Kay & McDaniel 1978; Seifart 2010). The domain of projectibility is determined by the maintaining mechanisms, not by the extension.
- **This matters for interjections.** Multiple fields recognise a category whose extension is roughly the same set of lexemes, but each field tracks different properties, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes. We distinguish these with subscripts:
  - **Interjection\textsubscript{syn}** -- the syntactician's category (CGEL, Quirk et al.). Distributional cluster: supplement function, non-inflecting, non-argument-taking. Maintained by prosodic-syntactic coupling and structural analogy. Projects syntactic behaviour of novel forms.
  - **Interjection\textsubscript{sem}** -- the semanticist's category, overlapping with *expressives* (Potts 2007, developing Kaplan 1999). Use-conditional meaning cluster: independent of at-issue content, nondisplaceable (predicated of the utterance situation), perspective-dependent (speaker-oriented), descriptively ineffable (Potts 2007: 166). Maintained by semantic bleaching pathways. Projects meaning type. Different extension from interjection\textsubscript{syn}: *damn* in *the damn car* is an expressive attributive adjective (interjection\textsubscript{sem}) but not interjection\textsubscript{syn}; conversely, *damn!* standalone is both.
  - **Interjection\textsubscript{int}** -- the interactional linguist's category, overlapping with *response tokens* (Stivers 2019) and *liminal signs* (Dingemanse 2020). Sequential/stance cluster: turn-initial, backchannel, stance display. Maintained by conversational routines and social indexing. Projects interactional distribution. Different extension from interjection\textsubscript{syn}: CA categories include items (*mm-hm*) that may or may not be interjection\textsubscript{syn}, and exclude items (*damn these mosquitoes!*) whose interactional profile differs from the standalone use.
- The subscript notation makes explicit that these are distinct HPCs, not subtypes of a single category. A syntactician and a conversation analyst can carve the same words differently without either being wrong -- because they're tracking different HPCs (cf. Reynolds 2026, Ch 11 on noun vs. name).
- Note prior HPC applications in linguistics (countability, definiteness, grammaticality: Reynolds 2026) without dwelling. This paper extends the programme to a category where the field-relative split is particularly visible.

## 3. The property cluster (~3 pages)

Survey the co-occurring properties, drawing on the Wikipedia article and sources. Each property is now evaluated not just for co-occurrence but for its contribution to inference: if you observe this property in a novel form, what else can you predict?

- **Phonology**: prosodic isolation, atypical phonemes (clicks, glottal stops, voiceless bilabial fricative). *Inferential contribution:* prosodic isolation is a strong cue to syntactic non-integration and non-referentiality; atypical phonemes signal peripheral category membership. Boundary case: some other peripheral elements share prosodic distinctiveness (Wilkins 1992 via Ameka).
- **Morphology**: non-inflecting, non-deriving; remnant morphology (*heavens*, *bollocks*). *Inferential contribution:* non-inflection predicts non-predicativity and non-argumenthood (forms that don't inflect don't fill argument or predicate slots in the way nouns and verbs do). Boundary case: adverbs also don't inflect (Ameka 1992: 106), but adverbs can form constituents with other words (Meinard 2015: 152).
- **Syntax**: supplement function, rare complements (*damn these mosquitoes*), interjection phrases (*oh boy*, *holy cow*). *Inferential contribution:* supplement function predicts prosodic isolation and resistance to embedding (*that*-complementation, coordination with non-interjections). The CGEL analysis of verb-derived interjections (H&P 2002: 1361fn) shows that even exceptional syntactic behaviour (complement-taking) is constrained: no subject, no tense.
- **Semantics**: non-referential, emotive, internal states (Leech 2006). *Inferential contribution:* non-referentiality predicts that the form will not take determiners, quantifiers, or restrictive modifiers -- it has nothing to refer to. Boundary case with nouns: bleached religious terms (*Jesus*, *God*) -- interjection use is non-referential (Gehweiler 2008: 72).
- **Pragmatics**: exclamatory/imperative force, backchanneling, greeting, agreement. *Inferential contribution:* emotive/interpersonal force predicts turn-initial or standalone distribution, stance expression, and availability for backchanneling (Stivers 2019). Boundary case: routine formulae (Ameka vs. Wilkins) -- formulae share prosodic isolation and pragmatic function but add addressee-directedness and social predictability (Coulmas 1981; Ameka 1992: 109--110). They share some but not all stabilisers with the interjection cluster.

Close by noting: these properties co-occur *statistically*, not necessarily. Every property is hedged (Wilkins's "commonly," "typically," "usually," "generally"). This is what HPC predicts. Crucially, the properties are not independent: observing some licenses inference to others, because the maintaining mechanisms couple them. The next section explains how.

## 4. Stabilisers: recruitment and maintenance (~3.5--4 pages, the JHP centrepiece)

The distinction between how items *enter* the cluster (diachronic recruitment) and what keeps the cluster *coherent* (synchronic maintenance) is critical. Boyd's framework requires the latter; the former explains the trajectory (Reynolds 2026, Ch 6: "The pathways are historical; the stability is mechanistic. Both are needed").

### 4.1 Recruitment pathways (diachronic)

Two bleaching trajectories analysed in detail, showing multiple entry points into the cluster:

**Path 1: Religious noun > interjection (Gehweiler 2008).** Four-stage model: proper name (*Jesus*) > exclamatory use (*Jesus!*) > phonological modification (*Jeez*, *Gee*) > primary interjection (*gee!*). Two sub-mechanisms operate: gradual Invited Inferencing (speaker-based: the expressive use is conversationally implicated, then conventionalised) vs. abrupt reanalysis (hearer-based: the hearer parses the exclamation as a standalone unit, not as an invocation). The four stages progressively install cluster properties: non-referentiality arrives at stage 2, phonological divergence at stage 3, full prosodic independence at stage 4. This is a *constrained trajectory*, not a random walk -- each stage makes the next more probable (philosopher of science reviewer's suggestion). Apply P3 (phase transition): is there a historical moment where the properties snap into a self-sustaining equilibrium? Gehweiler's corpus data may speak to this.

**Path 2: Verb > interjection.** *Damn* (verb > interjection): retains NP complement (*damn these mosquitoes!*) but no subject, no tense inflection. CGEL's analysis: "exceptional interjections that combine with an NP complement to form an interjection phrase" (H&P 2002: 1361fn). The complement retention shows *partial* bleaching -- the form carries some but not all verb properties into the interjection cluster. The entry mechanism is different from Path 1: syntactic deactivation (loss of subject, tense, agreement) rather than phonological modification. Also *come on* (imperative > interjection), *look* (imperative > discourse marker/interjection). These show that the interjection cluster is fed from multiple directions, as HPC predicts.

**Pragmatic conventionalisation** (Traugott & Dasher's Invited Inferencing Theory of Semantic Change): content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective. Interjections as an endpoint of this trajectory. The subjectification scale applies to both paths: religious terms lose propositional content and gain expressive force; verb forms lose event-structural content and gain interpersonal force.

### 4.2 Synchronic feedback loops (maintenance)

What keeps the cluster coherent *now*, once items are in it? Four feedback loops:

1. **Prosodic isolation ↔ syntactic non-integration.** Each reinforces the other. Prosodic separation makes syntactic integration harder (a form in its own intonation unit is less available for subcategorisation); syntactic independence licenses prosodic isolation (a form with no argument-structural requirements can stand alone). Gehweiler (2008: 85): sentence-initial position of invocations contributed to the emergence of the interjection -- the prosodic slot preceded and enabled the syntactic independence.

2. **Non-referentiality → loss of argument structure → syntactic independence.** Bleaching removes referential content, which removes the semantic motivation for argument structure, which makes the form syntactically free-standing. This is a one-directional cascade, but it is maintained by the fact that once argument structure is lost, there is no pressure to restore it -- the form occupies a different functional niche.

3. **Free coinage via onomatopoeia → phonological atypicality → prosodic isolation.** Nonce interjections can be freely created (Quirk et al. 1985: 74), and these coinages tend to use atypical phonemes (clicks, glottal stops, voiceless bilabial fricatives), which reinforces the prosodic distinctiveness of the cluster. This is a recruitment mechanism that simultaneously maintains the cluster's phonological signature.

4. **Social indexing → register marking → preservation.** Regional interjections (*lah* in Singapore/Malaysia, *yaar* in India/Pakistan, *haba* in Nigeria, *word* in African-American English: Lauture 2020) survive because they do identity work. The social-indexing mechanism identified for quotatives (Reynolds 2026, Ch 6) operates here too: forms that mark in-group solidarity resist replacement.

**Perturbation test.** What would happen if a stabiliser were removed?
- Neutralise prosodic isolation (integrate the form into the clause): the form starts behaving like a verb or adverb. This is what happens with the *pop* in *pop went the cork* (Meinard 2015: reclassified as adverb by some lexicographers precisely because it is syntactically integrated).
- Restore referential content: the form re-enters the noun category. *God* used referentially is a noun; *God!* used expressively is an interjection. The category boundary tracks the mechanism boundary.
- Each stabiliser's removal produces distinct consequences, which is the signature of a genuine mechanism (Reynolds 2026, Ch 6: perturbation grid).

### 4.3 Dynamic stability

- *Nay* → *yeah* (COHA data): individual members enter and leave, but the cluster persists. The category is an attractor in dynamical-systems terms.
- Regional variation (GloWbE): *lah*, *yaar*, *haba* -- the category is instantiated differently across populations but the property cluster is recognisable. P5 (conditioning recovers structure): conditioning on region should decompose the apparent gradience.
- Free coinage: the cluster is continuously replenished via onomatopoeia. Nonce forms immediately adopt the cluster profile (prosodic isolation, non-referentiality, emotive force). This is the strongest evidence that the cluster is an attractor, not a historical accident.
- Dingemanse (2020): liminal signs as interactional resources; liminality is functional, not defective. Cultural evolutionary processes "sample the landscape of possible resources." HPC provides the framework: the "landscape" is a property space with attractor basins maintained by the mechanisms above.

## 5. Projectibility: what the category lets you predict (~2--2.5 pages)

This is the payoff of the organising question posed in Section 1 and the criterion of success stated in Section 2.

### 5.1 Partial-observation inference (breaking circularity)

Projectibility is not "if X is an interjection, it has interjection properties" (circular). It is: partial observation of *some* cluster properties in a novel form licenses inference to *other* properties. This is Boyd's point: the category supports induction beyond the observed instances.

Concrete example: a speaker encounters *bruh* (a recently conventionalised form). Observing that it is prosodically isolated and carries stance meaning, they can predict:
- It will be non-inflecting (you don't say **bruhs* or **bruhed*)
- It will resist syntactic integration (**I think that bruh* is anomalous)
- It will resist restrictive modification (**very bruh*, **the bruh* are ungrammatical as interjection uses)
- It will be available for turn-initial or standalone use
- Its etymological source is a content word (*brother*) that has undergone phonological reduction and semantic bleaching

These are non-trivial predictions -- they go beyond restating the observed properties. The predictions succeed because the maintaining mechanisms couple the properties: prosodic isolation and stance expression co-occur because the same mechanism (loss of referential content → loss of argument structure → syntactic independence → prosodic freedom) produces both.

### 5.2 Field-relative projectibility: three HPCs, one extension

The subscript notation from Section 2.3 pays off here. The same lexemes participate in distinct HPCs, each with its own inferential payoffs:

- **Interjection\textsubscript{syn} projects syntactic behaviour.** If a novel form is categorised as interjection\textsubscript{syn}, a syntactician can predict: supplement function, resistance to *that*-complementation, resistance to coordination with non-interjections, no argument structure (or at most a bare NP complement, as with *damn*). These are distributional predictions grounded in the prosodic-syntactic coupling mechanism.
- **Interjection\textsubscript{sem} projects meaning type.** If a form is categorised as interjection\textsubscript{sem} (expressive), a semanticist can predict: use-conditional rather than truth-conditional contribution, speaker-oriented attitude (perspective dependence), nondisplaceability (the attitude is predicated of the utterance situation), descriptive ineffability (Potts 2007: 166). These are semantic predictions grounded in the bleaching mechanism that strips truth-conditional content. The extension difference matters: *damn* in *the damn car* is an expressive attributive adjective (interjection\textsubscript{sem}) but not interjection\textsubscript{syn}; standalone *damn!* is both.
- **Interjection\textsubscript{int} projects interactional distribution.** If a form is categorised as interjection\textsubscript{int}, a conversation analyst can predict: turn-initial or standalone positioning, own intonation contour, stance display rather than propositional content, availability for backchanneling (Stivers 2019). These are interactional predictions grounded in conversational routines and social indexing. The extension difference matters: *mm-hm* is interjection\textsubscript{int} (a backchannel) but its status as interjection\textsubscript{syn} is debated (O'Connell & Kowal 2005).

The point: these are not three ways of describing the same category. They are three distinct HPCs with overlapping but non-identical extensions, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes. The overlap is explained by the fact that some mechanisms (prosodic isolation, non-referentiality) contribute to multiple HPCs simultaneously. The non-overlap is explained by the fact that other mechanisms are field-specific (structural analogy maintains interjection\textsubscript{syn} but not interjection\textsubscript{int}; conversational routines maintain interjection\textsubscript{int} but not interjection\textsubscript{syn}).

### 5.3 Category vs. function

The question (from the CGEL tradition): what does the category label "interjection" add beyond the function label "supplement"? Supplements of any category (NPs, AdjPs, relative clauses) share prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration. The answer: the *category* label adds what the *function* label cannot provide:

- **Non-referentiality**: supplements can be referential (*a university professor, Dr. Brown,...*); interjections cannot.
- **Emotive/interpersonal force**: not all supplements express stance; interjections characteristically do.
- **Free coinage via onomatopoeia**: unique to this category. No other category in supplement function can be freely coined.
- **Non-inflection**: NP supplements inflect freely; interjections don't.
- **Diachronic trajectory predictions**: knowing something is a supplement makes no prediction about its etymology; knowing it is an interjection does (bleaching paths, phonological reduction).

The function label tells you about syntactic integration. The category label tells you about inference -- it licences a package of predictions that no other category-in-supplement-function supports.

### 5.4 Interjections as a pragmatic kind

The extension to HPC theory: not all kinds project in the same domain. Nouns project morphosyntactically (takes determiners, inflects for number, fills argument slots). Interjections project pragmatically (prosodic behaviour, interactional distribution, stance expression). The subscript notation makes this vivid: while noun\textsubscript{syn} and noun\textsubscript{sem} (name) are both robust HPCs with thick clusters, interjection\textsubscript{syn} is a thin HPC whose richest projectibility is in the pragmatic domain (interjection\textsubscript{int}).

This connects to the content/procedural distinction: interjections are procedural, not conceptual (Traugott & Dasher). Their projectibility is about how forms will be *used*, not what they will *denote*. The interjection\textsubscript{sem} (expressive) cluster is the semantic reflex of this: use-conditional meaning is the semantic signature of procedural content.

This is the interjection parallel to the colour/animacy case: animacy projects grammatically (argument realisation, reference tracking); colour projects ecologically (edibility, toxicity) but not grammatically (Reynolds 2026, Ch 7; Prasertsom, Smith & Culbertson 2026; Seifart 2010). The domain of projectibility is set by the maintaining mechanisms. Interjections are maintained by prosodic, interactional, and social-indexing mechanisms, so their strongest projectibility is in those domains.

## 6. Discussion (~1.5 pages)

### 6.1 Empirical predictions: HPC vs. prototype

Prototype theory accommodates the interjection data (graded membership, fuzzy boundaries, typical-vs-atypical exemplars). HPC generates specific predictions that prototype theory does not. Four are testable for interjections:

- **P1, Asymmetric fraying:** If items drift away from the interjection cluster, tight diagnostics (non-inflection, complement resistance) should erode before loose ones (prosodic isolation). *Defeat:* diagnostics erode in no principled order.
- **P3, Phase transition in genesis:** Bleaching into interjectionhood should show a tipping point -- a stage where co-occurring properties snap into equilibrium -- not a smooth cline. *Defeat:* historical data shows monotonic, continuous co-change with no inflection point.
- **P5, Conditioning recovers structure:** Apparent gradience at the interjection boundary (fillers, routine formulae) should decompose when conditioned on register, dialect, or community. *Defeat:* conditioned and pooled models perform identically.
- **P10, Hub-node asymmetry:** One property is more central to cluster stability. Candidates: prosodic isolation or non-referentiality. Removing the hub destabilises the cluster more than removing peripheral properties. *Defeat:* all properties contribute equally; no perturbation asymmetry.

Each prediction has a named defeat condition. Prototype theory generates none of them. This is what HPC adds.

### 6.2 Interjections as a thin-but-genuine HPC

Placing interjections on the word-class typology: not a wastebasket ("adverb" -- no shared mechanism, no convergence: Reynolds 2026, Ch 11), not a robust skeleton category (nouns, verbs -- full mechanism convergence), but a thin-but-genuine HPC, comparable to adjectives (Reynolds 2026, Ch 11). Lower mechanism density, but the convergence is real and the perturbation test is passed.

### 6.3 Boundary disputes as mechanism overlap

Brief: the graded boundaries with nouns, verbs, adverbs, fillers, and routine formulae reflect partial mechanism overlap, not anomaly. Routine formulae share prosodic isolation and pragmatic function but add addressee-directedness (Ameka 1992: 109--110) and are maintained by social conventions that interjections need not be. They share some but not all stabilisers with the interjection cluster. The Ameka-Wilkins debate is resolved: formulae are partial members of the interjection HPC, sharing enough mechanism support to cluster nearby but not enough to sit at the centre.

## 7. Conclusion (~0.5 page)

- Interjections are a thin-but-genuine HPC: a property cluster maintained by both diachronic recruitment pathways (semantic bleaching, pragmatic conventionalisation) and synchronic feedback loops (prosodic-syntactic coupling, non-referentiality cascades, social indexing).
- The category that embarrassed classical theory is not marginal or anomalous. It is a natural fit for HPC -- and its pragmatic projectibility extends the theory to a new domain of inference.
- The organising question: what does categorising something as an interjection let you predict? Primarily pragmatic inferences -- about prosodic behaviour, interactional distribution, and stance expression -- that are reliable, non-trivial, and grounded in identifiable mechanisms.
