# Review: English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster

**Reviewer persona:** Wendy Lesser, founder of *The Threepenny Review*
**Focus:** Prose quality, structure, economy
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. Overall Impression

This is a tightly argued paper whose prose is better than most academic linguistics writing, but it has developed a tic: it keeps telling you what it's about to do, what it just did, and why both matter. The architecture is visible -- too visible. The reader who needs this much scaffolding probably shouldn't be reading the *Journal of Historical Pragmatics*. Strip the scaffolding and you have about 7,500 words of genuinely interesting argument trying to get out from under 9,000 words of paper.

---

## 2. Best Sentences/Passages

**"A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut."** (Section 1, line 44)
This earns its place. It's compressed, ironic, and does argumentative work -- it captures the paradox that motivates the entire paper in a single clause. It also has rhythm: the parallel structure ("real enough ... thin enough") is doing the thinking for you.

**"The items change; the category doesn't."** (Section 4.4, line 647)
Perfect. Six words. This is the dynamic stability argument distilled. The semicolon is exactly right.

**"The cluster is an attractor, not a historical accident."** (Section 4.4, line 670)
Clean, makes the theoretical point without jargon, and closes a passage of empirical evidence with a claim that feels earned rather than asserted.

**"The function label tells you about syntactic integration. The category label tells you about inference."** (Section 5.3, lines 836-837)
This is the sentence I'd put on the back cover. It works because it's parallel, concrete, and it makes the reader feel the difference rather than merely describing it.

**"Both analysts were right about the data. The disagreement was about the framework."** (Section 6.3, lines 1006-1007)
Good diplomatic prose that also happens to be an argument. This is how you resolve a longstanding debate without condescending to either side.

## 3. Worst Sentences/Passages

**"The contribution of this paper is threefold: (a) interjections as a test case for HPC at the margins -- the category that embarrassed classical theory is the strongest evidence for HPC, not the weakest; (b) the diachronic mechanisms (semantic bleaching, pragmatic conventionalization) that sustain the cluster, analyzed as both recruitment pathways and synchronic stabilisers; and (c) a distinctive form of projectibility -- primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional -- that extends HPC theory to a new domain of inference."** (Section 1, lines 75-83)
This is a 90-word sentence doing the work of a salesman, not a writer. "The contribution of this paper is threefold" is academic throat-clearing at its purest. The reader can see the contributions for herself if the paper is any good. And the (a)/(b)/(c) structure, with parenthetical glosses inside each item, creates a sentence no human being would speak aloud. Cut the whole thing. The road map paragraph below already does this work.

**"The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces HPC theory and explains why projectibility is the organizing question. Section 3 surveys the interjection property cluster. Section 4 identifies the stabilisers..."** (Section 1, lines 85-94)
A full ten-line road map. I have never read one of these that made me more interested in reading the paper. They exist because authors are insecure, not because readers need them. The section headings already tell me the structure. Kill it.

**"Section 3 showed what each property, individually, lets you infer. Section 4 identified the mechanisms that couple those properties. This section asks a different question: what does the package license, and for whom?"** (Section 5, lines 681-684)
This is the third time we've been reminded what Sections 3 and 4 did. The reader has just read them. She knows. The only sentence that does new work here is the question at the end, and even that is a restatement of the criterion of success from Section 2. Start the section with the partial-observation inference argument and let the structure speak.

**"These are not three descriptions of the same category. They are three HPCs with overlapping but non-identical extensions, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes."** (Section 5.2, lines 782-784)
The first sentence is fine. The second piles up three noun phrases, each with a participial modifier, creating a sentence that feels like a list masquerading as prose. By the time you reach "projectible for different purposes," you've forgotten the subject. Break it or trim it.

**"The overlap is explained by the fact that some mechanisms (prosodic isolation, non-referentiality) contribute to multiple HPCs simultaneously. The non-overlap is explained by the fact that other mechanisms are field-specific..."** (Section 5.2, lines 784-789)
"Is explained by the fact that" appears twice in consecutive sentences. This is padding. "The overlap exists because..." would do the same work in fewer words.

## 4. Structural Assessment

**Section 1 (Introduction):** Does too much. The Bullokar opening is excellent. The decline-of-attention narrative works. But after the question is posed ("what does categorizing something as an interjection let you predict?"), the introduction keeps going for another 25 lines of contribution-listing and road-mapping that add nothing the reader won't get from the sections themselves. The introduction should end at roughly line 69.

**Section 2 (HPC theory):** Earns its place, but Section 2.1 could be tighter. The three-part slogan and the perturbation test are essential setup. The Slater paragraph (lines 124-133) feels dutiful rather than necessary -- if you're following Boyd and Reynolds, a footnote acknowledging the alternative view would suffice. Section 2.2 (HPC vs. prototype) is well done. Section 2.3 (field-relative projectibility) is essential and interesting, but the bullet-pointed subscript definitions feel like they belong in Section 5, where they're used. Introducing them here and then re-explaining them in Section 5 creates redundancy.

**Section 3 (The property cluster):** The most disciplined section. Each subsection follows the same pattern (property, evidence, inferential contribution), and the pattern earns its repetition because each "inferential contribution" paragraph does different work. This is where the paper is most convincing. Section 3.6 (co-occurrence, not necessity) is a good capstone and does not overstay.

**Section 4 (Stabilisers):** Strong in places, overwritten in others. Section 4.1.1 (Path 1) is the best stretch of the paper -- the Gehweiler stages are compelling and well-narrated. Section 4.1.3 (convergent pathways) is baggy. The Traugott & Dasher material (lines 536-560) is a detour that adds terminological overhead (subjectification, pragmaticalization, degrammaticalization) without changing the argument. If the point is "three different processes converge on the same cluster," you can say that without the full Traugott apparatus. Section 4.4 (dynamic stability) is excellent.

**Section 5 (Projectibility):** The heart of the paper and the most original contribution. But it's preceded by so much setup that the reader arrives slightly fatigued. Section 5.1 is good. Section 5.2 re-explains the subscript notation from 2.3 -- decide where it lives and put it there once. Section 5.4 (interjections as a pragmatic kind) is genuinely interesting but could be tighter; the animacy/colour analogy at the end (lines 876-880) is a good sentence in search of a shorter paragraph.

**Section 6 (Discussion):** Section 6.1 (empirical predictions) is necessary and well-structured, with named defeat conditions that give the paper falsifiability. Section 6.2 (thin-but-genuine HPC) makes a good point in too few words -- it's the paragraph that should feel like a payoff, but it's over before it registers. Section 6.3 (boundary disputes) is satisfying.

**Section 7 (Conclusion):** Competent but repetitive. It recapitulates the argument faithfully. The final two paragraphs (untested predictions and the pragmatic-kind claim) do new-ish work. The rest is summary, and a reader who has made it this far doesn't need it.

**Where attention flags:** In the road map (Section 1, lines 85-94). In the Traugott & Dasher material (Section 4.1.3). In the re-explanation of subscripts (Section 5.2). In the opening recap of Section 5.

## 5. The Cut List

To tighten by 10% (~100 lines / ~1,000 words):

1. **The "threefold contribution" paragraph** (Section 1, lines 75-83). Delete entirely. The road map and the paper itself communicate the contributions. **Saves ~10 lines.**

2. **The road map paragraph** (Section 1, lines 85-94). Delete entirely. **Saves ~10 lines.**

3. **The Slater paragraph** (Section 2.1, lines 124-133). Condense to a footnote: "Slater (2015) argues stable clustering suffices without mechanisms; we follow Boyd and Reynolds in requiring stabilisers because they generate the perturbation predictions tested in Section 6." **Saves ~7 lines.**

4. **The subscript definitions in Section 2.3** (lines 187-208). Move the bullet points to Section 5.2, where they do work. In 2.3, introduce the idea of field-relative projectibility with the jade example and the noun/name contrast, then say the subscript notation will be developed in Section 5. **Saves ~10 lines of duplication.**

5. **Recap openings.** Cut the "Section 3 showed... Section 4 identified..." opening of Section 5 (lines 681-684). Cut "Section 3 documented a cluster..." opening of Section 4 (lines 437-438). Trust the reader. **Saves ~5 lines.**

6. **The Traugott & Dasher material in Section 4.1.3** (lines 536-560). The convergence point can be made in two sentences rather than a full paragraph. Keep the final four lines ("Three diachronically distinct processes... HPC theory can"). Cut the Traugott apparatus. **Saves ~15 lines.**

7. **The second explanation of "is explained by the fact that"** (Section 5.2, lines 784-793). Tighten to: "The overlap reflects shared mechanisms (prosodic isolation, non-referentiality). The divergence reflects field-specific ones: structural analogy maintains interjection_syn; conversational routines maintain interjection_int." **Saves ~5 lines.**

8. **The conclusion's recapitulation** (Section 7, lines 1012-1031). Keep the final paragraph on untested predictions. Compress the first two paragraphs to five lines total. **Saves ~10 lines.**

9. **Section 5.2's Potts/damn discussion** (lines 744-766). This is thorough but overlong. The attributive *damn* example appears three times in the paper (3.6, 5.2, 5.2 again). Consolidate. **Saves ~8 lines.**

10. **Minor tightening throughout.** "It's maintained by mechanisms that keep the properties clustered" (line 103-104) -- "maintained" and "keep clustered" say the same thing. Dozens of similar small redundancies. **Saves ~10 lines.**

**Total estimated savings: ~90-100 lines (~10%).**

---

## 6. One Sentence of Advice

Stop telling the reader what you're about to argue and just argue it -- the paper's best moments are all compressed, concrete claims, and its worst moments are all meta-commentary about the paper's own structure.
