# Section 4 Review: Stabilisers -- Recruitment and Maintenance

**Reviewer expertise:** Historical pragmatics, grammaticalization theory, diachronic semantics
**Scope:** Section 4 only (lines 257--440 of `main.tex`)
**Round:** Section-level review of drafted text (post-R2 outline review)

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 4 delivers the structural backbone that Round 2 called for (recruitment/maintenance split, perturbation tests, convergent pathways), but the drafted text under-delivers on the two commitments that matter most for JHP: substantive engagement with grammaticalization theory and corpus-grounded diachronic evidence.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The recruitment/maintenance architecture is clear and well-executed.** The explicit division between 4.1 (diachronic recruitment) and 4.2 (synchronic maintenance) directly addresses Round 1's conflation concern and gives the section genuine analytical structure. The three named maintenance mechanisms (prosodic-syntactic coupling, non-referentiality ratchet, social indexing) are individually arguable and distinct in kind. The "ratchet" metaphor for non-referentiality (one-directional cascade, not feedback loop) is a particularly honest analytical move: not every stabiliser needs to be bidirectional, and saying so shows theoretical sophistication.

- **The perturbation test (Section 4.3) is the section's best contribution.** The two cases (*pop* losing prosodic isolation and being reclassified as an adverb; *God* regaining referential content and re-entering the noun category) demonstrate that removing a stabiliser produces category-appropriate consequences, not arbitrary drift. The observation that "each stabiliser's removal produces distinct consequences" (prosodic integration yields adverb-like reclassification, referential restoration yields noun-like reclassification) is exactly the kind of differential prediction that distinguishes HPC from mere description. This is the paragraph that earns the section its theoretical weight.

- **The convergent-pathways framing (Section 4.1.3) makes the right theoretical move.** Stating that "different mechanisms can recruit items into the same kind, producing the same cluster of co-occurring properties through different routes" is the HPC payoff for diachronic linguistics. It answers the question a grammaticalization scholar would ask: "So what? Bleaching happens through multiple paths; that's well known." The answer is that convergence on a shared property profile is evidence of attractor dynamics, not coincidence. The Traugott & Dasher positioning (content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective) is correctly applied to both paths. This is the right move.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The grammaticalization positioning remains uncommitted, as flagged in Round 2.** Section 4.1.3 states: "But interjection formation is not simply grammaticalization. Onomatopoeia (Section 4.3) creates interjections with no prior lexical history at all -- no bleaching, no deactivation, no subjectification." This is an important observation, but it is immediately followed by a retreat: "What unifies the pathways isn't the diachronic process but the cluster itself." For JHP, this is where the argument needs to dig in, not step back. The paper should explicitly address whether Path 1 (noun > interjection) qualifies as grammaticalization in Traugott's technical sense (gain of procedural meaning, loss of referential content, increase in subjectivity -- yes, it does), whether Path 2 (verb > interjection via *damn*) is better analysed as degrammaticalization or pragmaticalization (loss of syntactic structure suggests degrammaticalization; gain of expressive force suggests pragmaticalization), and whether the onomatopoeia pathway is categorically different (it is: no prior lexical item, no cline, no gradualness). The theoretical contribution should be: HPC theory explains why processes that are diachronically distinct (grammaticalization, degrammaticalization, nonce coinage) converge on the same synchronic category. That claim is present in embryo but is not stated with the force and specificity JHP requires.

  **Actionable:** Add 3--5 sentences to Section 4.1.3 that explicitly classify each pathway (Path 1 = grammaticalization sensu Traugott; Path 2 = pragmaticalization with partial degrammaticalization; onomatopoeia = neither), then argue that HPC theory is the framework that unifies processes that grammaticalization theory alone cannot subsume.

- **The Traugott & Dasher engagement is invoked but not worked through.** The subjectification trajectory (content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective) appears in a single sentence in Section 4.1.3. For JHP, this needs to be demonstrated, not merely asserted. For Path 1, the demonstration would look like: Stage 1 (invocation) = content meaning (addressing an entity) + subjective stance (reverence); Stage 2 (exclamation) = loss of content, gain of procedural meaning (expressive force); Stage 3--4 (phonological modification) = full procedural meaning, intersubjective (hearers interpret it as a conventional expression, not an invocation). For Path 2, the subjectification is different: *damn* as a verb is a speech act (content meaning: "I condemn X"); *damn* as an interjection is a stance marker (procedural meaning: "I am frustrated"). The paper gestures at this ("Religious terms lose propositional content and gain expressive force. Verb forms lose event-structural content and gain interpersonal force") but does not connect the stages to Traugott & Dasher's specific framework. The connection is available and would strengthen the JHP case considerably.

  **Actionable:** Expand the Traugott & Dasher engagement in Section 4.1.3 from 2 sentences to a short paragraph (6--8 sentences) that maps each path onto the subjectification cline with stage-by-stage analysis. This is the kind of detail JHP reviewers expect.

- **The corpus evidence is still gestural, not grounded.** Three corpus claims appear in Section 4.4:
  (a) COHA shows *nay* declining and *yeah* rising (lines 421--424);
  (b) GloWbE shows regional distribution of *lah*, *yaar*, *haba* (lines 427--431);
  (c) Gehweiler's data shows phonological modifications clustering in the 17th--18th centuries (lines 301--305).

  None of these is presented with specific frequencies, date ranges, or methodological notes. The *nay*/*yeah* claim is plausible but unquantified: what does "most common interjections in 1820" mean? Top 5? Top 10? By raw frequency or normalised? The GloWbE claim is similarly unspecified: is *lah* tagged as an interjection in GloWbE, or is this based on the author's reading of concordance lines? Gehweiler's "80--85" page reference is the strongest because it cites a specific source, but the text paraphrases rather than engages with Gehweiler's actual data presentation. For JHP, corpus claims need at minimum: (i) which corpus, which version, (ii) search terms and any disambiguation, (iii) normalised frequencies or at least raw counts with corpus size. Without this, the dynamic-stability argument rests on assertion.

  **Actionable:** Either add a brief methodology note (even a footnote) specifying how the corpus data was obtained and what the numbers are, or flag these as preliminary observations that will be substantiated. For the *nay*/*yeah* claim, a frequency table or figure would be ideal; at minimum, normalised per-million-word frequencies for the endpoints (1820s, 2010s) would anchor the claim. For GloWbE, specify whether the tagging is POS-based or manual.

## 4. Key question

The Gehweiler engagement is the natural anchor for JHP, since Gehweiler (2008) was published in this journal. The current draft engages substantively with Gehweiler's four-stage trajectory and with her distinction between Invited Inferencing and abrupt reanalysis (lines 307--313). This is good, and it is more than gestural. But the engagement is primarily *descriptive*: the paper takes Gehweiler's stages and maps them onto HPC vocabulary (non-referentiality at stage 2, phonological divergence at stage 3, full independence at stage 4). What is missing is *critical* engagement. Does Gehweiler's analysis support or challenge HPC? Specifically: Gehweiler distinguishes gradual Invited Inferencing from abrupt reanalysis as two sub-mechanisms within Path 1. The paper acknowledges this (lines 308--313) but does not ask: does HPC predict that *both* sub-mechanisms should feed the same cluster? If so, why? If not, is this a problem for the theory? A JHP reviewer familiar with Gehweiler's work will ask exactly this question. The answer, I think, is that HPC predicts convergent recruitment (different mechanisms can feed the same cluster, per Boyd 1999), and the Gehweiler case provides direct evidence: two distinct cognitive processes (gradual pragmatic inference vs. abrupt structural reanalysis) both produce the same cluster profile. But this answer needs to be stated.

**In short:** The Gehweiler engagement is substantive enough for a first submission, but it needs one more turn of the analytical screw: from "Gehweiler's stages map onto HPC" to "Gehweiler's two sub-mechanisms provide direct evidence for convergent recruitment, which is an HPC prediction."

---

## 5. Verdict: **Needs revision** (targeted, not structural)

The section's architecture is sound and does not need restructuring. The three revisions needed are all expansions within existing subsections:

1. **Section 4.1.3 (Convergent pathways):** Classify each pathway explicitly vis-a-vis grammaticalization theory (3--5 sentences). State that HPC subsumes what grammaticalization theory alone cannot unify. This is the paper's theoretical contribution to JHP and it must be explicit.

2. **Section 4.1.3 (Convergent pathways):** Work through the Traugott & Dasher subjectification cline stage by stage for both paths (6--8 sentences). JHP reviewers will expect this level of engagement with the framework they know best.

3. **Section 4.4 (Dynamic stability):** Ground the corpus claims with specific numbers, methodology, or at minimum a footnote acknowledging that the data presentation is preliminary. The *nay*/*yeah* turnover is the section's most vivid illustration of dynamic stability, but it is currently an unanchored assertion.

An optional but valuable addition: In Section 4.1.1, add one sentence making explicit that Gehweiler's two sub-mechanisms (Invited Inferencing and abrupt reanalysis) constitute convergent recruitment within a single pathway, which is itself evidence for the HPC claim.

**Estimated scope:** ~300--400 additional words, all within existing subsections. No new subsections needed. The section is 80% of where it needs to be for JHP; the remaining 20% is depth, not breadth.

## Additional observations

1. **The "ratchet" metaphor (Section 4.2.2) is well-chosen but needs one caveat.** Are there attested cases of de-interjectionalization where a form re-enters a content category? If so, the ratchet metaphor needs qualification. If not, that absence is itself evidence for the ratchet claim and should be stated.

2. **The Dingemanse (2020) engagement in Section 4.4 is brief but appropriate.** The "liminal signs" framing is compatible with HPC, and the paper correctly notes this without over-interpreting it. For JHP, Dingemanse's interactional-linguistics perspective provides useful triangulation. Consider whether a sentence connecting Dingemanse's "cultural evolutionary processes" to the recruitment pathways would strengthen the link.

3. **The social-indexing stabiliser (Section 4.2.3) is the most diachronically promising of the three maintenance mechanisms**, because social-indexing processes are historically traceable (dialectological records, sociolinguistic interviews, corpus evidence of regional forms). If the paper can provide even one attested case of a socially indexed interjection persisting over multiple generations while functionally equivalent alternatives were replaced, the stabiliser claim would be substantially strengthened. The *lah* case is the obvious candidate, but it needs historical depth beyond synchronic attestation in GloWbE.

4. **Section 4.3 (Perturbation test) should cross-reference Section 3.6 (Co-occurrence, not necessity) more explicitly.** The *damn* example appears in both sections with different analytical purposes (Section 3.6: coupling breaks when mechanisms are neutralised; Section 4.3: removing a stabiliser produces category-appropriate reclassification). Making this cross-reference explicit would show the reader that the perturbation test is not ad hoc but follows from the cluster analysis in Section 3.
