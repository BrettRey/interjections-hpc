# CGEL Grammarian Review: Section 3 ("The property cluster")

**Reviewer expertise:** English descriptive grammar (CGEL tradition), lexical categories, the category-function distinction, supplement function, peripheral word classes.

**Round:** Section 3 draft review (2026-03-14)
**Scope:** Lines 44--221 of `main.tex` (Section 3 only)

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 3 is a well-organized, empirically grounded survey of five co-occurring properties of English interjections, with clear inferential-contribution paragraphs after each subsection that advance the paper's HPC argument, though it leaves two important CGEL-related issues underspecified (interjection phrase structure and the category-function distinction in the syntax subsection).

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The inferential-contribution paragraphs after each subsection are the section's strongest feature.** Each subsection closes by asking what observing this property licenses you to infer about the others. This is not merely descriptive cataloguing; it is doing the theoretical work the paper promises. The phonology paragraph (lines 74--81) is particularly effective: it concedes that prosodic isolation is shared with vocatives and appositives, then pivots to the convergence claim. This is exactly the right move for an HPC argument, which does not require unique properties, only reliably co-occurring ones.

- **The Ameka-Wilkins routine formulae discussion (lines 184--198) is well handled.** The paper presents both sides (Ameka's three-way distinction, Wilkins's subtype analysis), then provides its own HPC framing (shared properties plus additional mechanisms). This is the kind of boundary dispute that HPC is designed to resolve, and the paper treats it with appropriate nuance. One residual concern from my Round 2 review (whether routine formulae are peripheral members or a neighbouring cluster) is not fully resolved here, but Section 3 is the right place to introduce the problem rather than settle it.

- **The co-occurrence subsection (lines 206--221) is an effective close.** Citing Wilkins's hedged characterizations ("commonly," "typically," "usually," "generally") and then noting that this is precisely what HPC predicts is a clean argumentative move. The three named mechanisms (prosodic isolation reinforcing syntactic non-integration, non-referentiality removing the need for argument structure, pragmatic conventionalization entrenching emotive function) give the reader a preview of the stabiliser discussion in Section 4 without pre-empting it.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The syntax subsection (lines 106--141) conflates category and function in a way that undercuts the paper's own theoretical commitments.** Line 108 says interjections "function as supplements," which is correct. But the subsection then discusses interjection phrases (lines 129--135) without ever distinguishing the lexical category "interjection" from the phrasal category "interjection phrase" (IntjP). CGEL (H&P 2002: 1361 [needs verification]) treats *damn these mosquitoes* as an interjection phrase headed by *damn* (interjection) with an NP complement. The draft gets the complement analysis right (lines 130--131), but it never states that *damn* is the head and the whole expression is an IntjP. This matters because the paper's argument in Section 5.3 (per the outline) will depend on the category-function distinction being sharp. If Section 3 is loose about phrase structure, Section 5 will have to do remedial clarification.

  **Actionable fix:** Add one sentence after line 131 along the lines of: "In CGEL's analysis, *damn* here is the head of an interjection phrase (IntjP) taking an NP complement -- one of the very few cases where interjections project beyond a single word." This would also address the residual ask from my Round 2 review (Concern A) about clarifying interjection phrase structures.

- **The syntax subsection's examples (lines 118--123) demonstrate supplement function but never show interjection-specific syntactic behaviour.** All three examples in (\ref{ex:supplements}) illustrate supplement function for different categories: interjection, relative clause, NP. The point is that supplement function is shared. But the subsection never provides a syntactic test that *distinguishes* interjection supplements from other supplements. The inferential-contribution paragraph (lines 137--141) offers two negative predictions (*\*I think that damn*, *\*damn and quickly*), which are good, but they are stated in the commentary, not demonstrated in examples. Adding them as labelled examples would make the syntactic claims more precise and testable.

  **Actionable fix:** Promote the two negative predictions at lines 140--141 to numbered examples, parallel to (\ref{ex:supplements}). These are the syntactic facts that do the work; they deserve example status, not parenthetical mention.

- **The semantics subsection (lines 143--165) attributes "non-referentiality" as a defining semantic property without addressing the counter-cases that the paper itself will need to handle.** The claim that interjections "don't refer" (line 145) is stated categorically, but several forms in the pragmatics subsection's GloWbE list (line 179) are arguably referential or at least propositional: *yes* and *no* contribute truth-conditional content (they affirm or deny propositions), and *oh* in many uses signals recognition of new information rather than expressing an internal state. If non-referentiality is a cluster property rather than a necessary condition, the subsection should say so explicitly. As written, lines 144--145 ("English interjections don't refer") reads as an exceptionless generalization, which is inconsistent with the co-occurrence-not-necessity framing at lines 208--213.

  **Actionable fix:** Soften lines 144--145 to something like "English interjections characteristically don't refer" (parallel to the hedged formulations in the other subsections). Then add a brief note acknowledging that *yes*, *no*, and some other forms are more propositional than emotive, which is why non-referentiality is a cluster property rather than a definitional requirement.

---

## 4. Key question

The syntax subsection argues that supplement function is shared across categories and that what distinguishes interjection supplements is "their combination with the other cluster properties" (lines 124--127). This is the right HPC move, but it raises a precision question: does the paper treat "supplement" as a function that interjections *characteristically* occupy, or as one they *always* occupy? The hedged framing at lines 108 ("characteristically function as supplements") suggests the former, but lines 124--127 seem to presuppose the latter by treating supplement function as the syntactic member of the cluster without qualification. Which is it? If supplement function is characteristic rather than exceptionless, what are the exceptions? Standalone interjections (*Ouch!* uttered alone) are not supplements of anything -- they are complete utterances. Backchannels (*uh-huh*, *mm-hmm*) are interactional tokens that may not have a host clause to be supplementary to. If these are not supplements, then "supplement function" is a property of integrated interjections only, and standalone interjections need a different syntactic characterization (possibly "available as a complete non-clausal utterance"). This would actually *strengthen* the cluster argument by adding a sixth property, but the paper needs to be explicit about it.

---

## 5. Verdict

**Needs revision (minor, addressable without restructuring).**

The section is well written, empirically well sourced, and argumentatively effective. The inferential-contribution paragraphs are its signature strength. Three revisions are needed before this section is ready:

1. **Interjection phrase structure** (syntax subsection): Add one sentence clarifying the head-complement analysis of *damn these mosquitoes* as an IntjP, and acknowledge the unresolved internal-structure question for lexicalised forms like *oh boy* / *holy cow*.

2. **Syntactic examples for negative predictions** (syntax subsection): Promote *\*I think that damn* and *\*damn and quickly* to numbered examples. These are the syntactic facts that distinguish interjection supplements from other supplements; they need example status.

3. **Non-referentiality hedging** (semantics subsection): Change "don't refer" to "characteristically don't refer" and add a brief note about *yes*/*no* and other propositional interjections, consistent with the co-occurrence-not-necessity framing in Section 3.6.

None of these requires restructuring. All are one-to-three-sentence additions or modifications. After these changes, the section will be ready for integration with Sections 4 and 5.
