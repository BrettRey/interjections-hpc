## 1. How Projectibility Is Defined and Distinguished from Mere Property Clustering

The chapter's central slogan:

> "A linguistic category is a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes."

The three parts of this slogan are not on a par; the first two (profile and mechanisms) matter **because they make the third (projectibility) possible**. Projectibility is the payoff of the whole system.

**Key definitional passage (lines 12-13):**

> "This is the core of what a category is: not a container or a definition, but a licence to infer."

**Projectibility vs. mere clustering (lines 46-51):**

> "The first term, *profile*, names what you can observe: properties cluster, members resemble each other, regularities recur. But surface clustering isn't yet an explanation. Any two objects share indefinitely many properties, and some clusters are only descriptive conveniences. The profile is the visible pattern, not yet an account of why that pattern holds."

> "What *stabilized by mechanisms* adds is the missing explanatory layer. [...] *Projectible relative to purposes* then names the payoff. A category supports induction, but only for questions those mechanisms underwrite."

**Projectibility is not a bonus feature -- it IS the substance (lines 19-22):**

> "This isn't a further property that HPCs happen to have. A category just is whatever supports reliable inference across novel instances. [...] The cluster is the visible trace; the inferential role is the substance."

**Peirce connection (line 22):**

> "Peirce saw this a century ago: the meaning of a sign is the habit it produces -- the inferences it licenses. A category that doesn't support inference isn't a category at all; it's a filing label."

**Metaphysics vs. epistemology distinction (line 53):**

> "Mechanism is metaphysics -- what keeps the cluster clustered. Projectibility is epistemology -- what inferences the cluster licenses. A category can be maintained without being projectible, if the mechanisms don't align with your question, and a pattern can seem projectible without being maintained, if you've mistaken correlation for causation."

## 2. The "Good Bet" Concept

The "good bet" is introduced via the opening vignette about Mr. Tagomi (from Philip K. Dick) and the *pedicab* example (line 14):

> "You encounter *car*. It takes determinatives, pluralizes, functions as an argument. You've never seen *pedicab* -- but you bet it behaves likewise. You're usually right. The category *noun* lets you project from instances you've seen to instances you haven't. That's the bet, and it usually pays off."

**What makes the bet good:** The bet is good when it is **grounded in mechanisms** rather than in definitions or mere correlations. The Polish aspect case study (Section 7.2) is the central demonstration: textbook semantic definitions ("boundedness," "totality") fail to predict usage (only 77% for perfective), while mechanism-based models (distributional cue structures, lexical entrenchment) succeed.

**The bet fails when:** The characterization misses the maintaining mechanisms. Example: Polish aspect-abstract models fail because the textbook semantic invariants don't track the actual distributional mechanisms. The bet also fails when mechanisms are too weak or variable (nonce formations), in which case what remains is a "class" rather than a "category" (line 382).

**Key passage on mechanism-grounded betting (line 108):**

> "The traditional label *perfective* remains useful -- you can introspect about it, metalinguistic discourse depends on it -- but its psycholinguistic reality needn't rest on invariant semantic content. The good bet is on the consistency of the distributional patterns that maintain it."

## 3. Pragmatic vs. Truth-Conditional Projectibility

This distinction is **not explicitly thematized under those exact terms** in Chapter 7. However, the chapter makes a closely related distinction through **field-relative projectibility** (Section 7.5):

**Proper nouns vs. proper names (lines 222-246):**

- Semantics projects: rigid reference, opacity, individual-tracking (proper names)
- Syntax projects: heads NP, fills argument slot, triggers agreement (proper nouns)

> "A syntactician *knows* that *Brett* is a proper name. But that semantic fact doesn't project for syntactic purposes. What projects syntactically is the distributional chain."

**Colour vs. animacy (lines 263-282):**

- Colour projects for **ecological purposes** (predicting edibility, danger, health)
- Animacy projects for **grammatical purposes** (argument realization, reference tracking, event-structure encoding)
- Colour does NOT project for grammatical purposes, which is why it never grammaticalizes

> "Knowing something is animate predicts a cluster of other properties: self-initiated motion, goal-directedness, organic composition, susceptibility to certain event roles. These predictions hold regardless of what specific entity you're considering. Knowing something is red predicts little beyond its surface appearance -- and what it does predict (ripeness, toxicity) is object-relative."

**The definiteness thread (lines 392-394) explicitly forecasts this distinction for Part III:**

> "definiteness generalizations should be projectible for semanticists studying identifiability and uniqueness, while generalizations tied to the morphosyntactic form cluster should be projectible for syntacticians studying *there*-resistance and partitive licensing -- and not automatically transferable across those purposes."

While the chapter doesn't use the exact phrase "pragmatic projectibility" vs. "truth-conditional projectibility," the architecture clearly supports that distinction: different mechanisms stabilize different property clusters, and different clusters project for different inferential purposes (semantic/truth-conditional vs. distributional/syntactic vs. pragmatic/interactional).

## 4. Circularity Risks

### 4a. Goodman's circularity (lines 323-329)

The chapter explicitly addresses Goodman's *grue* problem and its circularity:

> "Goodman's own answer invoked entrenchment: *green* is projectible because it's been projected successfully in the past, while *grue* isn't. The circularity is deliberate -- projectibility is bootstrapped from track record."

**The maintenance view breaks the circle:**

> "A habit isn't self-grounding; it is sustained by something. The stream's habit of flowing in a particular channel persists not because it has flowed there before but because water, gravity, and geology maintain it. *Green* is projectible not because speakers have projected it before but because mechanisms sustain the would-be: chromium traces interact with light in stable ways, and a visual system tuned by selection detects the reflectance."

### 4b. Interest-relativity circularity (lines 334-341)

Craver (2009) and Onishi & Serpico (2022) raise the worry that mechanism-based accounts inherit context-dependence:

> "a category is projectible relative to the mechanism you're interested in, and the mechanism you're interested in is whichever one makes the category projectible."

**Reynolds' response -- interest picks the question, not the answer:**

> "The key distinction is between choosing a level because it answers your question, and choosing a level because it privileges your category. [...] Interest picks the question; it doesn't fabricate the mechanism that answers it."

> "This is where the maintenance view improves on conventionalism. Interest selects among real causal structures; it doesn't invent them."

### 4c. The turkey parable (lines 315-321)

Liu Cixin's turkey illustrates the danger of projectibility without mechanistic grounding:

> "The turkey's law is perfectly confirmed by all available evidence. The problem lies in its grounding: the law isn't tied to the mechanism that produces the feeding pattern."

### 4d. Lemeire's objection (line 344)

> "Lemeire (2018) argues that no purely epistemic theory -- one that defines categories solely by their inductive usefulness -- can account for naturalness. The objection is serious because projectibility alone is too permissive. What's needed is the right *sort* of projectibility -- the sort that comes from genuine homeostatic mechanisms rather than accidental correlations."

## 5. Concrete Examples of Projectible Inferences from Linguistic Categories

### 5a. Nouns (line 14)
Encountering *car* lets you project to *pedicab*: takes determinatives, pluralizes, functions as argument.

### 5b. Polish aspect (lines 60-108)
- Textbook definitions (boundedness, totality) project imperfective well (98%) but perfective poorly (77%).
- Mechanism-based model: distributional cue--outcome associations (lexical bias, tense context, morphological prefix cues) project much better.
- "The good bet is on the consistency of the distributional patterns that maintain it."

### 5c. Causative alternation (line 173)
Ambridge et al. (2020): directness-of-causation mechanism projects across 5 typologically unrelated languages, even predicting judgments in a 6th (Balinese) without exposure. Definitional verb classes don't project cross-linguistically.

### 5d. Morphological syncretism (line 175)
Saldana et al. (2022): learnability gradient tracks semantic similarity, not feature-system category membership.

### 5e. Grammatical relations in BERT (line 177)
Chi et al. (2020): grammatical relations (subject, object) emerge spontaneously in multilingual BERT. A probe trained on English identifies grammatical relations in French. Distributed learning projects; formal definitions are epiphenomenal.

### 5f. NPIs as counter-example (lines 36-39)
NPIs are NOT projectible as a single category: Hoeksema (2012) documents 12 distinct licensing patterns. "Models succeed at NPI prediction only to the extent they learn each item's distribution, not the category."

### 5g. Constituency (lines 254-256)
Knowing *the cat* is a noun phrase lets you predict pronominalization, fronting, coordination. Projects for "what groups with what."

### 5h. Dependency (lines 257-258)
Dependency length predicts reading time and comprehension difficulty. Projects for "what costs what."

### 5i. Colour categories (lines 268-280)
Colour projects for ecological purposes (fruit ripeness, toxicity). Does NOT project for grammatical purposes (no language has colour-based verb agreement).

### 5j. Degrees of projectibility (lines 354-366)
- High: English determinatives (closed class, stable, predictable)
- Medium: major categories (nouns, verbs, adjectives) -- projectible at core, variable at margins
- Low: nonce formations, idiolectal forms, constructions undergoing change
- Cross-linguistically: English adjectives --> German adjectives (moderate confidence) --> Japanese adjective-like words (lower confidence)

## 6. Relation to Boyd's Original HPC Framework

The chapter builds on Boyd (1999) but makes several extensions:

**Boyd is cited** (line referenced in CLAUDE.md and the chapter's index entries) as the originator of HPC theory. The chapter's core move is to foreground **projectibility** as the payoff of HPC membership, rather than treating clustering and mechanism as ends in themselves.

**Key extensions beyond Boyd:**

1. **Projectibility as the substance, not a bonus** (line 20): "This isn't a further property that HPCs happen to have. A category just is whatever supports reliable inference across novel instances."

2. **Field-relative projectibility** (Section 7.5): Boyd's framework doesn't explicitly address how the same extension can support different HPCs for different fields. Reynolds adds this: "there are genuinely different HPCs that happen to overlap in extension, each projectible in its own domain."

3. **Degrees of projectibility** (Section 7.7.3): Categories aren't equally projectible -- the framework predicts a gradient graded by entrenchment strength.

4. **The volcanic island metaphor** for mechanistic drift: what built a category may differ from what now maintains it, but it remains an HPC as long as SOME mechanisms maintain it.

5. **Three diagnostic checks** (lines 299-303):
   - Cluster check: Does property covariance hold?
   - Homeostasis check: Does the cluster reconstitute under perturbation?
   - Projectibility check: Are there counterfactual-supporting inferences in new contexts?

6. **Integration with Peirce's semiotics** (Section 7.4): Boyd's framework is given a Peircean interpretation where projectibility = the interpretant (inferential habit), the cluster = the sign, and the mechanism = what sustains the habit.

## 7. "Projectibility for a Purpose" -- What Purposes Are Identified

The chapter identifies several distinct purposes for which projectibility can be evaluated:

### Linguistic/analytical purposes:
1. **Predicting corpus distributions** (Polish aspect -- which verb form appears)
2. **Predicting native-speaker judgments** (gap-filling tasks)
3. **Teaching language learners** (Polish aspect pedagogy)
4. **Syntactic prediction** (distributional behavior: what heads NP, fills argument slots, triggers agreement)
5. **Semantic prediction** (rigid reference, opacity, individual-tracking)
6. **Psycholinguistic prediction** (processing difficulty, reading time via dependency length)
7. **Cross-linguistic comparison** (causative alternation, comparative concepts)
8. **Historical/diachronic analysis** (aspect as inherited label)

### Non-linguistic purposes mentioned:
9. **Ecological/survival prediction** (colour categories: edibility, toxicity, mate health)
10. **Culinary prediction** (tomato as vegetable: flavour pairings, cooking methods)
11. **Botanical prediction** (tomato as fruit: ripening, seed dispersal, hormone response)

### Key framing (lines 292-293):

> "The framework doesn't multiply categories recklessly. It asks, for each analytical purpose, what cluster of properties is maintained by what mechanisms, and whether that cluster projects for the question at hand."

### Robustness criteria for genuine mechanistic categories (line 346):

> "A genuine mechanistic category -- as opposed to a convenient label -- should exhibit *learning transfer*, *intervention stability*, and *cross-context generalization*. Train on one subset and test on another: does it transfer? Intervene on the mechanism: does the pattern shift as predicted? Apply the generalization in a new context: does it hold?"

## 8. Additional Notable Points

### Category vs. Class distinction (line 382):
> "When that happens, what remains is a *class* rather than a category: a label without enough causal structure behind it."

### Poetic naturalism connection (line 261):
Carroll's (2016) "poetic naturalism" is invoked: "a sparse naturalistic metaphysics paired with a permission structure for higher-level vocabularies." The HPC framework specifies HOW those patterns stay put.

### The "nerboun" test (line 329):
A pseudo-category "nerboun" (noun if acquired before age 5, verb otherwise) is not projectible because no mechanism produces that pattern.

### Sean Carroll & Parola on emergence (line 342):
> "descriptions that remain predictive because they discard the right information rather than arbitrary information."

### Preview of Part III applications (lines 288-291):
- Countability: semantic individuation vs. morphosyntactic count-marking (furniture)
- Definiteness: semantic definiteness vs. morphosyntactic form cluster
- Word classes: noun/verb stability vs. adjective variability
- Pro-form gender: designatum-driven vs. antecedent-driven maintenance
