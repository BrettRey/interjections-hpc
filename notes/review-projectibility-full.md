# Projectibility Review: Complete Paper

**Reviewer role:** Projectibility Reviewer (R1, R2, section reviews completed; first review of complete draft)
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**File reviewed:** `main.tex` (complete draft, ~1015 lines)

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper delivers on its projectibility promise more consistently than I expected from the section-level reviews: projectibility is set up as the organizing question in Sections 1--2, threaded through Section 3 via per-property "Inferential contribution" paragraphs, grounded mechanistically in Section 4, and cashed out in Section 5 with the partial-observation test, the subscript architecture, and the pragmatic-kind argument -- but the conclusion and discussion pull back toward mechanisms and boundary-dispute resolution rather than pressing the projectibility payoff to its sharpest form.

## 2. Strengths

- **The criterion of success (Section 2.1) does real work.** The paper explicitly states that it succeeds only if categorizing a novel form as an interjection "licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes," and the *bruh* demonstration in Section 5.1 directly answers this criterion. This is not decorative framing; it organizes the argument.

- **The "Inferential contribution" paragraphs in Section 3 are effective.** Each property subsection ends by asking what observing that property lets you predict about the rest of the cluster. This prevents Section 3 from degenerating into a descriptive catalogue. The paragraphs are uneven in quality (phonology and syntax are strong; morphology is weaker because non-inflection is widespread), but the structural decision to include them was correct and keeps projectibility visible at the property level.

- **The subscript architecture (Section 5.2) is the paper's most original contribution.** Distinguishing interjection_syn, interjection_sem, and interjection_int as three HPCs with overlapping extensions, different mechanisms, and different inferential payoffs is genuinely novel. The attributive *damn* case, where the subscripts diverge (interjection_sem predictions hold, interjection_syn predictions fail), demonstrates that the notation isn't just a labelling exercise -- it generates testable divergence predictions that neither CGEL nor Potts alone can produce.

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The Discussion (Section 6) loses the projectibility thread.** Section 6.1 frames the HPC-vs.-prototype comparison around "mechanism-derived predictions" -- P1, P3, P5, P10 -- which are genuine predictions, but they are predictions *about the category's behaviour over time and across conditioning variables*, not predictions *about novel instances*. The organizing question was: what does categorizing a form as an interjection let you *infer*? The HPC predictions in Section 6.1 answer a different question: what does HPC theory predict about the *dynamics* of the category? Both questions matter, but they aren't the same question, and the paper doesn't flag the shift. **Actionable fix:** Add a paragraph at the start of Section 6.1 distinguishing two kinds of prediction: (a) instance-level projectibility (Section 5's question: observing some properties, infer others) and (b) category-level predictions (Section 6's question: what HPC predicts about category dynamics). Make explicit that both are HPC payoffs, but only (a) is projectibility in the paper's technical sense.

- **The Conclusion doesn't cash out in predictions.** The final paragraph restates that the answer is "primarily pragmatic" and names the domains (prosodic behaviour, interactional distribution, stance expression). But it doesn't give a single concrete prediction. Compare the *bruh* demonstration in Section 5.1, which is vivid and specific. The conclusion should mirror that specificity. As it stands, the last paragraph is a summary of the paper's claims, not a demonstration of what the paper's framework *does*. **Actionable fix:** Add one concrete prospective prediction to the conclusion. For example: "If a novel form enters English with prosodic isolation and stance meaning (as *oof* recently did), the interjection HPC predicts it will resist inflection, resist syntactic embedding, and be available for backchanneling -- predictions that are testable and non-trivial." One sentence. The paper has earned it.

- **Section 5.3 (Category vs. function) is a strong section, but it isn't framed as a projectibility argument until halfway through.** The opening question ("what does the category label add beyond the function label?") is a CGEL question, not a projectibility question. The answer *is* a projectibility argument (the category licences inferences the function doesn't), but the framing buries the lead. **Actionable fix:** Open Section 5.3 with: "The sharpest test of projectibility is whether the category label adds predictive power beyond what its syntactic function already provides." Then the CGEL question follows naturally as an instance of that test.

## 4. Key question

The paper's criterion of success requires "non-trivial inferences for specific purposes." Section 5 delivers this for linguists (syntacticians, semanticists, conversation analysts). But who else benefits from the category? The paper is targeted at the Journal of Historical Pragmatics, whose readers will care about diachronic pathways and pragmatic conventionalization. Section 5.2's subscript architecture is primarily synchronic. Does projectibility extend to historical inference? Section 4.1's constrained trajectory already implies it: knowing a form is at stage 2 (Gehweiler) lets you predict what downstream properties are absent. This is projectibility applied diachronically, and it's exactly the kind of thing JHP readers would find compelling. But it's buried in Section 4, not promoted to Section 5. **Should the paper explicitly develop diachronic projectibility as a fourth subscript or as a cross-cutting dimension?**

## 5. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit.**

The projectibility thread holds from Introduction through Section 5. It is genuinely the organizing question, not just a framing device. Sections 1--2 set up exactly what Sections 3--5 deliver. The subscript architecture is original and well-demonstrated.

But the thread frays in two places: the Discussion shifts from instance-level projectibility to category-level dynamics without flagging the change, and the Conclusion summarizes rather than predicts. Neither problem is structural. Both are fixable with targeted additions (one paragraph in the Discussion, one sentence in the Conclusion, one reframing sentence in Section 5.3). The diachronic-projectibility gap (key question above) is a missed opportunity for the JHP audience but can be addressed with a paragraph in Section 5.

The paper is close. The projectibility architecture works. The fixes are editorial, not architectural.
