# Review: Historical Pragmaticist (Full Draft)

**Reviewer persona:** Historical pragmaticist specialising in grammaticalization, semantic change, and diachronic pragmatics.

**Paper:** English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster (complete draft)

**Target journal:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics (John Benjamins)

**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections constitute a homeostatic property cluster (HPC) rather than a marginal or anomalous category, identifying diachronic recruitment pathways (grammaticalization, pragmaticalization, nonce coinage) and synchronic stabilisers as the mechanisms that maintain the cluster, with the distinctive theoretical contribution being that interjection projectibility is primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The convergent-pathways argument is genuinely compelling.** The demonstration that grammaticalization (Path 1: noun > interjection via bleaching), pragmaticalization (Path 2: verb > interjection via syntactic deactivation), and nonce coinage (onomatopoeia) all converge on the same synchronic property profile is the paper's strongest empirical contribution. The observation that "grammaticalization theory alone can't subsume all three" but HPC can (Section 4.1.3) is well argued and directly relevant to JHP's readership, who are accustomed to grammaticalization as the default explanatory framework. This is a genuine theoretical insight, not just a relabelling.

- **The Traugott & Dasher integration is well handled.** The paper situates its two recruitment pathways within Traugott & Dasher's (2002) subjectification trajectory (content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective) but is careful to distinguish their diachronic trajectories: Path 1 is grammaticalization in the technical sense; Path 2 is pragmaticalization with partial degrammaticalization. This nuance will be appreciated by JHP readers who are sensitive to the overextension of "grammaticalization" to processes that don't involve the development of grammatical function. The paper avoids the trap of claiming all interjection-formation is grammaticalization.

- **The perturbation test brings falsifiability to diachronic claims.** The dual perturbation cases (neutralise prosodic isolation: *pop went the cork* reclassified as adverb; restore referential content: *God* reverts to noun) are concrete and effective. Too many papers in historical pragmatics describe pathways without specifying what would count as evidence against the proposed mechanism. The perturbation test, borrowed from the HPC framework, is a welcome methodological contribution.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The Gehweiler engagement is adequate in content but shallow in critique.** The paper draws on Gehweiler (2008) for the four-stage trajectory and the Invited Inferencing vs. reanalysis distinction, but it never interrogates Gehweiler's analysis. Three questions that a JHP reviewer would expect to see addressed: (a) Gehweiler's corpus evidence is drawn from a relatively narrow set of religious-oath euphemisms (*Jesus > Jeez/Gee*, *God > Gosh/Golly*); does this path generalise beyond the religious-oath domain, or is it a culturally specific subtype? The paper treats it as representative, but a single semantic domain is thin ground for a general pathway. (b) Gehweiler's four stages are presented as if they are uncontroversial, but the boundary between stages 1 and 2 (invocation vs. exclamatory use) is difficult to operationalise in historical data, as she herself acknowledges (p. 77). The paper should note this difficulty rather than treating the stages as cleanly sequential. (c) The claim that "the phonological modifications cluster in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries" (Section 6.1, citing Gehweiler pp. 80--85) is presented as evidence for a phase transition (P3), but the paper doesn't engage with the sociohistorical confound: taboo avoidance intensified during exactly this period (Puritan cultural influence in England and New England), so the clustering may reflect external social pressure rather than an inherent tipping point in the diachronic process. This distinction matters for the HPC argument, because if the clustering is driven by external taboo norms rather than by internal mechanism dynamics, it is not evidence for P3 as stated.

- **The diachronic content, while substantive, is concentrated in a narrow empirical window.** For a paper targeting the Journal of Historical Pragmatics, the historical data is surprisingly limited. The two recruitment pathways are analysed with only two primary cases each (Path 1: *Jesus > gee*; Path 2: *damn*, plus brief mentions of *come on* and *look*). The COHA data on *nay > yeah* is mentioned but not analysed in any detail (Section 4.4 gives it two sentences and a footnote). The paper acknowledges that the COHA interjection tag "captures a conventional inventory, not a theoretically motivated one," which is an important caveat, but then the caveat undermines the evidential force of the data it accompanies. JHP readers will want at least one fully worked diachronic case study with dated attestations, frequency data, and collocational analysis. The *gee* case could serve this role, but the paper relies entirely on Gehweiler's analysis rather than presenting independent corpus evidence. A concrete recommendation: either (a) add a figure showing frequency trajectories from COHA or EEBO/ECCO for at least one form undergoing the bleaching pathway (this would be strong evidence for P3), or (b) add a second fully developed pathway beyond the religious-oath domain (e.g., the development of *well* as an interjection/discourse marker, which has a rich diachronic literature including Jucker 1997 and Defour 2007, both published in outlets familiar to JHP readers).

- **The paper lacks engagement with the broader grammaticalization literature on interjections.** The diachronic claims are framed almost exclusively through Gehweiler (2008) and Traugott & Dasher (2002). Missing from the discussion are several works that JHP reviewers will expect to see:
  - Wierzbicka (1992), who argues that interjections have language-specific semantic content (not just "bleached" content), which complicates the non-referentiality claim.
  - Norrick (2009) on interjections in conversation, which provides interactional data relevant to the interjection_int HPC.
  - The broader degrammaticalization literature (Norde 2009), which is relevant to Path 2 (the paper calls it "partial degrammaticalization" without citing the theoretical literature on degrammaticalization itself).
  - Heine & Kuteva (2002), which is listed in the outline's key sources but absent from the draft. If the outline planned to use it for cross-linguistic grammaticalization patterns, its absence weakens the diachronic generality claim.
  - Jucker & Taavitsainen (2013) on historical pragmatics methodology, which would strengthen the methodological framing for JHP.

---

## 4. Key question

**Is the diachronic content sufficient to justify placement in JHP rather than, say, the *Journal of Pragmatics* or *Linguistics and Philosophy*?**

The paper's theoretical contribution (HPC applied to interjections, pragmatic projectibility) could appear in any linguistics journal. What earns it a place in JHP specifically is the claim that diachronic mechanisms are central to the analysis: "the pathways are historical; the stability is mechanistic" (Section 4, quoting Reynolds 2026). But the historical content is concentrated in Section 4.1, which relies almost entirely on Gehweiler (2008) for its primary case study and provides only brief sketches of Path 2. The synchronic stabilisers (Section 4.2) are interesting but not diachronic. The dynamic stability data (Section 4.4) is gestured at rather than analysed. If the paper is to earn its place in JHP, it needs either (a) deeper engagement with the historical data (original corpus work, dated attestations, frequency trajectories) or (b) a more sustained argument that the HPC framework illuminates diachronic pragmatics in ways that existing frameworks (grammaticalization theory, pragmaticalization, Invited Inferencing) do not. Currently the paper does (b) in places (the convergent-pathways argument, the phase-transition prediction) but doesn't follow through with the empirical depth that would make these claims convincing to a historically oriented audience.

---

## 5. Verdict: **Revise & Resubmit**

The paper has a genuine theoretical contribution (the convergent-pathways argument, the perturbation test, pragmatic projectibility as an extension of HPC theory) and is clearly written. But it does not yet earn its place in JHP. The diachronic content is too thin and too reliant on a single secondary source (Gehweiler 2008) to satisfy JHP's readership. The revisions needed are substantial but feasible:

1. **Deepen the Gehweiler engagement:** Interrogate the stages, note the taboo-avoidance confound for P3, and consider whether the pathway generalises beyond religious oaths.
2. **Add empirical depth to at least one diachronic case:** Original corpus data (COHA, EEBO, ECCO) with dated attestations and frequency trajectories. This is what JHP publishes; the paper needs to speak the journal's empirical language.
3. **Engage the missing diachronic literature:** Norde (2009) on degrammaticalization, Wierzbicka (1992) on language-specific interjection semantics, Jucker & Taavitsainen (2013) on historical pragmatics methodology. These are not optional for a JHP submission.
4. **Strengthen the dynamic stability section:** The *nay > yeah* shift is mentioned in two sentences. Either develop it with COHA frequency data across decades or cut the claim.

The theoretical architecture is sound. The paper needs more historical flesh on the bones.
