Brett is the primary author of the Wikipedia "Good Article" on English interjections, which surveys the category across phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics, and variation, with corpus data (GloWbE, COHA) and historical coverage (Bullokar 1586 through CGEL 2002). ### Target

Journal of Historical Pragmatics (John Benjamins). Gehweiler (2008) on *gee!* already published there; the diachronic mechanisms (bleaching paths, category membership shifts over time) are central to the journal's scope.

### Core Idea

Interjections are a particularly revealing test case for HPC theory because they've been denied category status (Jespersen), dismissed as uninteresting (Huddleston & Pullum Student's), or conceded as "marginal and anomalous" (Quirk et al.). If HPC can account for interjections, it handles the hard cases. The key contributions:

1. **Property cluster identification:** Non-referential, non-inflecting, prosodically isolated, syntactically supplemental, emotively laden, freely coined via onomatopoeia.
2. **Mechanisms (stabilizers):** Semantic bleaching (noun-to-interjection paths), prosodic isolation reinforcing syntactic independence, pragmatic conventionalisation. Need synchronic feedback loops, not just diachronic recruitment pathways.
3. **Projectibility:** What does categorising something as an interjection let you infer? Primarily pragmatic (prosodic behaviour, syntactic non-integration, emotive/interpersonal force) rather than truth-conditional. This is the paper's distinctive theoretical contribution. Must be framed as the criterion of success from Section 2 onward.
4. **Diachronic dimension:** Bleaching paths as mechanism trajectories; the nay-to-yeah shift and regional variation (lah, yaar, haba) as evidence of the cluster's dynamic stability.
5. **Boundary disputes as HPC predictions:** Graded boundaries are predicted by HPC, not anomalous.
