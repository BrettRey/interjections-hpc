# Review: Section 5 (Projectibility: what the category lets you predict)

**Reviewer role:** Philosopher of science specialising in natural kinds theory, HPC theory, and scientific realism.
**Scope:** Section 5 only (lines 501-662 of `main.tex`), with Sections 3-4 as context.
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Round:** Section-level review of drafted text

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 5 delivers the theoretical payoff promised since the introduction, and the field-relative projectibility architecture (5.2) is the paper's most original philosophical contribution, but the "pragmatic kind" move in 5.4 needs sharper engagement with the metaphysics it implicitly invokes and the Potts/Kaplan discussion in 5.2 is too thin to carry the weight the argument places on it.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The partial-observation inference move (5.1) decisively breaks circularity.** The *bruh* example at lines 519-533 is exactly what the R2 review asked for, and the drafted text improves on the outline by grounding each prediction in a named mechanism: the non-referentiality ratchet connects loss of referential content to loss of argument structure to syntactic independence; the prosodic-syntactic coupling connects syntactic independence to prosodic isolation (lines 536-541). The predictions are non-trivial (they go beyond restating the observed properties), they are specific (starred forms, distributional restrictions), and they succeed *because* the maintaining mechanisms couple the properties. This is textbook HPC projectibility, executed cleanly. The diachronic prediction (line 531-532: the etymological source is a content word that has undergone bleaching) is a particularly nice touch -- it demonstrates that HPC projectibility is not limited to synchronic distribution but extends to historical inference, which is directly relevant to the JHP audience.

- **The field-relative architecture (5.2) is the paper's most philosophically substantial move.** The subscript notation (interjection_syn, interjection_sem, interjection_int) operationalises Boyd's disciplinary relativism (1999: 150) in a way that Boyd himself did not develop. Boyd's jade example (natural kind in gemology, not in geology) is suggestive but underdeveloped; it names the phenomenon without showing how different disciplinary HPCs relate to each other. This paper does what Boyd didn't: it explains the overlap (shared mechanisms: prosodic isolation, non-referentiality contribute to multiple HPCs) and the non-overlap (field-specific mechanisms: structural analogy maintains interjection_syn but not interjection_int; conversational routines maintain interjection_int but not interjection_syn). The extension differences are concrete -- *damn* in *the damn car* is interjection_sem but not interjection_syn; *mm-hm* is interjection_int but debatably interjection_syn -- and they are generated by the mechanism architecture, not stipulated. This is a genuine contribution to the HPC literature, not just to interjection studies.

- **The category-vs-function argument (5.3) has real philosophical bite.** It could have been a merely taxonomic point internal to the CGEL tradition, but it isn't. The five-item list at lines 608-622 demonstrates that the category label licences a package of inferences that the function label alone cannot provide: non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, diachronic trajectory. The key move is at lines 624-631, where the paper generalises beyond CGEL: "any framework that distinguishes what kind of thing an expression is from what job it's doing in a particular clause will face the same question, and the HPC answer is the same." This connects to the more general philosophical question about whether natural-kind categories earn their keep beyond their functional roles -- the answer being that categories project inferentially in ways that functional descriptions cannot. That is a philosophically respectable claim and it is well executed here.

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The Potts (2007) / Kaplan (1999) engagement in 5.2 is too thin.** Lines 559-570 introduce interjection_sem by invoking Potts's expressives framework and listing four properties: use-conditional contribution, perspective dependence, nondisplaceability, descriptive ineffability. All four properties are cited to a single page (Potts 2007: 166), which is a summary page in Potts's own work, not the place where the arguments are developed. More importantly, the paper treats Potts's expressive semantics as if it straightforwardly delivers a property cluster, but Potts's framework is intensional (expressives are a type in a multi-dimensional semantics), not extensional in the way HPC requires. The paper needs to address a bridging question: *Is Potts's semantic type a cluster of co-occurring properties (as HPC requires) or a definition that assigns items to a category by stipulation?* If the four Potts properties co-occur because of an underlying mechanism (bleaching, as line 566 suggests), then interjection_sem is a genuine HPC. If they co-occur because Potts defines expressives as items having all four properties, then interjection_sem is a definitional category dressed up in HPC clothing. The paper appears to intend the former reading (the bleaching mechanism grounds the co-occurrence), but it doesn't make this explicit. Two sentences would suffice: "Potts defines expressives by these four properties; we treat them as a cluster sustained by the bleaching mechanism from Section 4.1, not as a definition. The test is whether items can have some of these properties without others -- and they can: *damn* in *the damn car* is perspective-dependent and use-conditional but arguably not nondisplaceable in reported speech." This would also show that interjection_sem has graded membership, which is the HPC signature.

  Additionally, Kaplan (1999) is cited in the outline (Section 2.3) as a precursor to Potts but does not appear in the drafted Section 5 at all. Kaplan's demonstratives paper is relevant because it introduces the notion of "expressive content" that Potts develops, and because Kaplan's treatment raises a question the paper should address: Kaplan treats expressives as a semantic *type*, not as a natural kind. The paper should make clear that it is not claiming interjection_sem is a semantic type in Kaplan/Potts's sense but rather that the semantic type picks out a cluster whose coherence is explained by HPC mechanisms. This is a non-trivial philosophical distinction and the paper should not leave it implicit.

- **The "pragmatic kind" claim (5.4) hovers between a genuine theoretical extension and a relabelling.** The core claim at lines 635-644 is that interjections project pragmatically rather than morphosyntactically, and that this represents "a genuine extension of HPC theory" (line 660). But the argumentative work that would make this a genuine extension is compressed into a single paragraph (lines 653-662), and it relies on an analogy (animacy/colour) rather than on a direct argument about what the extension amounts to metaphysically.

  The question is this: does "pragmatic kind" name a kind whose projectibility domain happens to be pragmatic (in the way that a biological kind's projectibility domain happens to be ecological), or does it name a *different type of kind* -- one whose metaphysical status differs from morphosyntactic kinds? The paper seems to intend the former (the domain of projectibility is set by the maintaining mechanisms, and interjections happen to be maintained by pragmatic mechanisms), which is the conservative and defensible reading. But lines 660-662 gesture toward the latter: "the framework accommodates kinds whose inferential payoff is pragmatic rather than truth-conditional." This could be read as claiming that HPC theory needs to be *extended* to accommodate pragmatic kinds, which implies they are metaphysically different. If so, the extension needs an argument: what is it about pragmatic projectibility that standard HPC theory doesn't already accommodate? Boyd's framework is domain-general -- it doesn't restrict projectibility to truth-conditional inference. The paper may be pushing at an open door.

  The fix is to be explicit about which reading is intended. If pragmatic kinds are just kinds whose mechanisms happen to be pragmatic (conservative reading), then the "extension" is merely a new application, not a theoretical innovation, and the paper should say so modestly. If pragmatic kinds are genuinely novel (strong reading), the paper needs to explain what property of pragmatic projectibility resists accommodation by existing HPC theory. As drafted, the section claims novelty without earning it.

- **The relationship between 5.2 (three HPCs) and 5.4 (pragmatic kind) is underspecified.** Section 5.2 argues that interjection_syn, interjection_sem, and interjection_int are three distinct HPCs. Section 5.4 argues that interjection_syn is a "thin HPC" whose richest projectibility is pragmatic (interjection_int). But if interjection_int is a separate HPC from interjection_syn (as 5.2 claims), then it isn't clear what it means to say that interjection_syn's richest projectibility is "in the pragmatic domain." The richest projectibility belongs to interjection_int, which is a different HPC. The paper seems to slide between two framings: (a) interjection is one kind with multiple projectibility domains (5.4's framing), and (b) interjection is three kinds with overlapping extensions (5.2's framing). These are different claims, and the paper needs to choose or explicitly reconcile them. One possible reconciliation: "Interjection_syn is a thin HPC on its own terms, but the same forms that belong to interjection_syn typically also belong to interjection_int, and it is this overlap that generates the richest inferential payoff. The pragmatic projectibility is not a property of interjection_syn itself but of the intersection of interjection_syn and interjection_int." This would preserve both the 5.2 architecture and the 5.4 claim, but the paper needs to say it explicitly.

---

## 4. Key question

Section 5.2 claims that interjection_syn, interjection_sem, and interjection_int are "not three descriptions of the same category" but "three HPCs with overlapping but non-identical extensions, maintained by different mechanisms, projectible for different purposes" (lines 586-588). This is a strong claim. It means that there is no single HPC that *is* "the interjection category" -- there are three kinds that happen to overlap. But the rest of the paper (Sections 3-4, and 5.4) treats "the interjection cluster" as a single object with a single set of mechanisms and a single perturbation test. Which is it? Is there one interjection HPC with multiple projectibility domains, or three interjection HPCs with overlapping extensions? The answer matters for the paper's contribution: if one HPC, the field-relative architecture is about how different disciplines extract different inferences from the same kind (which is interesting but not novel -- Boyd already said this). If three HPCs, the paper is claiming that a single traditional category fragments into multiple natural kinds under HPC analysis (which is novel and would be the paper's strongest philosophical contribution, but it needs more development to be convincing).

---

## 5. Verdict

**Needs revision.** The section is close to final but has three issues that require paragraph-level attention:

1. **Potts/Kaplan bridge (5.2, lines 559-570):** Add 2-3 sentences distinguishing Potts's intensional type from an HPC cluster, grounding the co-occurrence of expressive properties in the bleaching mechanism, and noting that graded membership in interjection_sem is evidence for the HPC reading. If Kaplan (1999) is retained in the reference list, it should appear here or be dropped.

2. **Pragmatic kind: conservative or strong reading (5.4, lines 635-662):** Disambiguate. If conservative (pragmatic kinds are just kinds with pragmatic mechanisms), tone down "genuine extension" language. If strong (pragmatic kinds are metaphysically novel), provide an argument for what standard HPC theory can't already accommodate. Currently the section claims novelty without earning it.

3. **One-HPC-or-three reconciliation (5.2 vs. 5.4):** Add a sentence or two in 5.4 clarifying the relationship. The most natural reading is that the "pragmatic kind" claim is about the intersection of the three HPCs, not about interjection_syn alone. Make this explicit.

None of these require structural changes. They are paragraph-level revisions that sharpen philosophical precision without altering the section's architecture. The partial-observation inference (5.1) and category-vs-function argument (5.3) are ready as drafted.
