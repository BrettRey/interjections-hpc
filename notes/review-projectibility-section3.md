# Projectibility Review: Section 3 (The property cluster)

**Reviewer role:** Projectibility specialist
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Scope:** Section 3 only (lines 44--221 of `main.tex`)
**Context:** Round 2 outline review accepted with minor revisions; this reviews the drafted text against the approved structure.

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 3 delivers on the outline's promise that each property subsection would contain an "inferential contribution" component, but the inferential paragraphs vary in quality: the best ones (semantics, phonology) license genuinely non-trivial predictions, while the weakest (pragmatics) restate the property rather than licensing inference from it.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The organising question is visible.** The section's opening paragraph (lines 46--52) explicitly states the two-question structure: "does it co-occur with the others, and does observing it in a novel form licence inference to the rest of the cluster?" This is exactly the framing the outline specified, and it means the reader knows from the start that this section is about projectibility, not taxonomy. The word "inference" appears in every subsection. The section does not read as a catalogue.

- **The semantics inferential paragraph (lines 159--165) is the strongest.** It makes genuinely non-trivial negative predictions: a non-referential form has nothing for a determiner to operate on, nothing for a quantifier to bind, nothing for a restrictive modifier to restrict. These are distributional consequences that follow from the semantic property but are syntactic in nature -- exactly the kind of cross-domain inference that makes HPC projectibility non-circular. The final sentence ("they distinguish interjections from nouns that have undergone only partial bleaching") anchors the predictions to a real diagnostic problem, which is good.

- **Each subsection honestly acknowledges the property's non-uniqueness.** Prosodic isolation is shared with vocatives and appositives (line 78). Non-inflection is shared with adverbs (line 101). Supplement function is shared with relative clauses, NPs, and AdjPs (lines 114--127). This is exactly right for an HPC paper: no single property is diagnostic; the cluster is. The closing subsection (3.6, "Co-occurrence, not necessity") clinches this with the Wilkins (1992) hedge-word inventory. The rhetorical structure works: each subsection says "here is a property, here is what it lets you infer, here is why it alone is not sufficient," and then 3.6 pulls it together.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The pragmatics inferential paragraph (lines 200--204) is the weakest of the five.** It states: "Emotive or interpersonal force predicts turn-initial or standalone distribution, stance expression, and availability for backchanneling." This is thin. Turn-initial distribution and stance expression are near-restatements of the property itself: if something carries speaker stance, predicting that it "expresses stance" is circular. The prediction needs to go *across* domains, as the semantics paragraph does. What does emotive/interpersonal force predict about *morphology* (e.g., resistance to derivation into adjective form: *ouchish, *damnful)? About *phonology* (e.g., tolerance of marked intonation contours that would be anomalous on a content word)? About *diachronic trajectory* (e.g., resistance to semantic re-enrichment -- once bleached to stance meaning, items don't regain referential content)? Any of these would be a cross-domain inference that is genuinely non-trivial. As written, the pragmatics paragraph predicts pragmatics from pragmatics.

- **The syntax inferential paragraph (lines 137--141) is specific but narrow.** The two predictions -- resistance to *that*-complementation and resistance to coordination with non-interjections -- are concrete and testable, which is good. But they are both negative syntactic predictions from a syntactic property. The outline's criterion was that each property should license inference "to the rest of the cluster," not just to further consequences within its own domain. What does supplement function predict about *semantics* (e.g., non-truth-conditionality: the proposition expressed by the clause is unchanged if the supplement is removed)? About *pragmatics* (e.g., the form will be interpreted as expressing speaker attitude rather than contributing to at-issue content)? One cross-domain prediction would strengthen this paragraph substantially.

- **The section does not explicitly set up Section 5.** The closing subsection (3.6) transitions to Section 4 by naming the maintaining mechanisms and flagging the "inferential payoffs they produce." But it does not say what Section 5 will add that Section 3 has not already provided. The reader might reasonably ask: "You've already told me what each property lets me infer. What more is there?" The answer, from the outline, is partial-observation inference (observing *some* properties and predicting *others* in a novel form), field-relative projectibility, and the category-vs-function distinction. Section 3.6 should plant at least a seed: something like "the inferential contributions above are property-by-property; Section 5 asks what the *package* of predictions amounts to, and for whom." Without this, Section 5 risks feeling redundant rather than cumulative.

---

## 4. Key question about projectibility handling

Do the inferential contribution paragraphs demonstrate *cross-domain* inference (observing a phonological property and predicting a syntactic one), or do they mostly stay within the same domain? Cross-domain inference is the hallmark of HPC projectibility -- it is what distinguishes the cluster from a list. The phonology and semantics paragraphs achieve this (prosodic isolation predicts syntactic non-integration; non-referentiality predicts distributional restrictions). The morphology paragraph partially achieves it (non-inflection predicts non-argumenthood). The syntax and pragmatics paragraphs do not: supplement function predicts further syntactic restrictions; emotive force predicts further pragmatic behaviour. This asymmetry should be addressed in revision.

---

## 5. Verdict

**Needs revision (minor, targeted).**

The section is structurally sound and honours the outline's commitment to projectibility as the organising question. The opening framing, the five inferential-contribution paragraphs, and the closing co-occurrence subsection are all in the right shape. Three specific revisions would bring it to standard:

1. **Revise the pragmatics inferential paragraph** to include at least one cross-domain prediction (morphological, phonological, or diachronic). The current paragraph predicts pragmatics from pragmatics.

2. **Add one cross-domain prediction to the syntax inferential paragraph.** Supplement function should predict something outside syntax -- plausibly non-truth-conditionality (semantic) or speaker-attitude interpretation (pragmatic).

3. **Add a forward reference to Section 5 in the closing subsection (3.6).** Distinguish property-by-property inference (this section) from package-level inference (Section 5) so the reader knows the argument is not yet complete.

None of these require restructuring. Each is a paragraph-level edit. The section is close.
