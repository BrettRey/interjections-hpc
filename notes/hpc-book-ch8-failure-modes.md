## 1. Failure Modes Identified

The chapter identifies **three failure modes** for putative HPC categories, organized by which diagnostic(s) they fail:

### 1a. Thin Classes ("The Smoke Ring")
- **Definition:** A class where the signal exists (speakers recognize it, linguists name it) but the property cluster is "ghostly" -- no robust homeostatic loop maintains it.
- **Diagnostic failure:** Fails homeostasis. "We can't name their stabilizers -- because there are none, or none sufficient to produce stable clustering."
- **Key metaphor:** "Smoke ring" -- has structure, but ephemeral, generated by transient convergence of other mechanisms rather than a dedicated feedback loop.
- **Case study:** Preposition copying/pruning in English (e.g., "from which to choose from"). Radford & Felser (2012) document ~15 copying tokens and 67 pruning tokens in ~300 hours of broadcast speech. These are byproducts of competition between pied-piping and stranding, not targets of maintenance.
- **The perturbation test (verbatim):** "If English lost preposition copying tomorrow -- if speakers simply stopped producing it -- no feedback loop would kick in to restore it. No acquisition pressure, no social correction, no functional gap. It would vanish without a trace."
- **Recommended response:** "Stop treating the pattern as a category... Thin patterns don't need theoretical treatment because they lack the causal structure that would make treatment meaningful. Document them, but don't reify them."

### 1b. Fat Classes ("The Wastebasket")
- **Definition:** A label that lumps distinct causal clusters into a single bin. "Every taxonomy has a drawer labelled 'misc.' The mistake is to infer that 'misc.' is a lineage."
- **Diagnostic failure:** Fails projectibility. "Learning one subclass doesn't let you predict others."
- **Case study:** The traditional category "Adverb" -- lumps manner adverbs (quickly), degree words (very), and sentence adverbs (unfortunately) under one label, despite radically different mechanisms maintaining each subgroup.
- **Key passage (verbatim):** "Three clusters, three sets of mechanisms. The label *adverb* lumps them only because they share a negative property: they're modifiers that aren't adjectives."
- **Projectibility failure demonstrated:** "Learning the distribution of *quickly* -- that it can appear sentence-finally (*She ran quickly*) -- generates a prediction for other 'adverbs.' But the prediction fails catastrophically for degree words: **It was good very.* And for modal adverbs: **She left probably.* The pattern doesn't travel."
- **Recommended response:** "Decompose. *Adverb* isn't a kind; the clusters inside it -- around *quickly*, around *very*, around *thus* -- might be, each maintained by its own mechanism braid."
- **Sympathetic framing (verbatim):** "A note of sympathy for the original grammarians. They weren't being lazy... Students need labels. Textbooks need categories. The pressure to provide *some* answer -- any answer -- is real."

### 1c. Negative Classes ("The Complement Class")
- **Definition:** Categories defined by what they aren't -- complement classes.
- **Diagnostic failure:** Fails BOTH diagnostics. "They miss projectibility because members share no positive properties to generalise from. They miss homeostasis because no mechanism can maintain 'lacking X' -- what would such a mechanism even look like?"
- **Key passage (verbatim):** "There is no such thing as a 'homeostatic property cluster of absence.' The absence of a property is typically not a property, and certainly not one that mechanisms can stabilize."
- **Case study 1:** Left Branch Condition -- not a constraint but a "gap of abstinence." Competitors (pied-piping, headless substitution) cover the semantic space completely. "The LBE option isn't 'blocked' by a constraint; it's pre-empted by the traffic on the other roads."
- **Case study 2:** Non-finite clause -- groups infinitivals, plain forms after modals, past participles, gerund-participles. "Four clause types, four sets of environments, four sets of mechanisms. The only thing they share is a negative: their heads lack primary tense inflection."
- **Recommended response:** "Reframe. *Non-finite clause* names a contrast, not a unity. Replace it with positive categories -- *infinitival complement*, *perfect construction*, *progressive construction* -- that actually have mechanisms to maintain them."

## 2. HPC vs. Prototype Theory

The chapter does not explicitly contrast HPC with prototype theory at length, but there are several points of implicit and explicit contrast:

- **Dyirbal language death (verbatim):** "Schmidt (1985) documented Dyirbal in decline: peripheral category links broke off first while central members held, producing the edge-inward collapse that Lakoff (ch. 6) later cited as evidence for radial category structure. Lakoff noted the pattern but had no theory of the mechanisms whose weakening produces it. The maintenance account supplies one: the mechanisms that build the cluster are the ones whose loss produces the fraying."

This is the most direct HPC-vs-prototype passage. It acknowledges prototype theory's descriptive success (edge-inward erosion) but argues HPC provides the **causal-mechanistic explanation** that prototype theory lacks. Prototype theory describes the *what* (graded structure); HPC explains the *why* (mechanism-maintained clustering).

- **Informative absences (verbatim):** "Even while a category is fully maintained, its boundary cases show *informative absences* -- properties that are missing not at random but in configurations that map the mechanism landscape. A borderline adjective in English that takes comparative morphology but resists predicative use isn't randomly incomplete; it's missing the properties supported by the mechanisms weakest in that region of the cluster."

This reframes prototype effects mechanistically -- gradience is not just a typicality gradient but a map of differential mechanism strength.

## 3. Prediction vs. Accommodation

This is a central concern of the chapter, addressed through the **two-diagnostic test** and the **inflation problem**.

### The Inflation Problem (= "merely accommodates" objection)
- **Verbatim:** "If the criteria for being a mechanism-maintained kind are too loose, the framework explains nothing because it excludes nothing. If every stable pattern counts as an HPC, then 'HPC' just means 'pattern we have a name for.' That's a large portion of the index."
- The chapter's explicit goal is to provide **criteria that make HPC falsifiable** -- categories that fail the diagnostics are NOT HPCs.

### The Two-Diagnostic Test
The framework demands categories pass BOTH:
1. **Projectibility diagnostic:** "Can we predict new data from old?" -- train on one dataset, test on held-out set. If performance exceeds shuffled-label baseline, projectible.
2. **Homeostasis diagnostic:** "Can we name the stabilizers?" -- identify specific feedback loops; predict what happens if a stabilizer is removed (perturbation sensitivity).

**Why both are needed (verbatim):**
- "A grouping might be projectible but not homeostatic. Consider a corpus artifact... The projectibility was real but local; the homeostasis was illusory."
- "A grouping might be homeostatic but not projectible. Consider a category maintained by a single, weak mechanism that operates inconsistently across speakers."

### Falsifiability Built In
- **Verbatim:** "The two-diagnostic test gives a strict decision procedure for HPC claims: apply projectibility and homeostasis criteria; if the pattern fails, it's not an HPC category. This creates a research programme with clear success conditions. You can be wrong in a way that's detectable -- and that's what makes the framework scientific."

### Convention-Driven Projectibility Confound
- **Verbatim:** "Projectibility across corpora can be an artefact of shared conventions -- genre pipelines, annotation guidelines, register selection, editorial norms -- rather than evidence of genuine mechanistic clustering... A category that predicts well across the British National Corpus, the Corpus of Contemporary American English, and the Penn Treebank may do so because all three were annotated by linguists trained in the same tradition, not because the category tracks stable structure in ordinary speech."

## 4. Genuine HPC Analysis vs. Superficial Relabelling

This is the chapter's core target. Several key passages:

### The Inflation Problem
- **Verbatim:** "The danger of a good framework is that you start seeing it everywhere... Soon even the paper clips look homeostatic."
- **Verbatim:** "Words that won't hold still -- that's one failure mode. Words that *seem* to hold still only because we gave them a label -- that's another. This chapter is about telling them apart."

### The Chair/Dessert/Weapon Analogy
The chapter distinguishes between:
- **Useful categories** (chair, dessert, weapon) -- group by function, not by shared mechanisms
- **Epistemic kinds** (Khalidi's term) -- serve epistemic purposes but don't track causal structure
- **Genuine HPC kinds** -- maintained by feedback mechanisms

**Key passage (verbatim):** "What makes something a *chair*?... The category tracks *function*, not underlying structure -- and this isn't the 'functional pressure' that... identifies as a stabilizer; that applies when function shapes what gets transmitted, not when function is merely the criterion by which a user groups pre-existing objects."

### Material Reinforcement Trap
- **Verbatim:** "Some labels don't just circulate in heads; they circulate in curricula, exams, style guides, tagsets, and parsers. Once a category gets its own exam question, it starts to feel eternal."
- But: "The useful distinction is between a *linguistic kind* (maintained in the usage system of a speech community) and a *metalinguistic/pedagogical kind* (maintained by institutions and practices about language). Both can be real nodes in causal networks. They differ in what they explain and predict."

### The Decision Rule (Step 6 of Audit)
- **Verbatim:** "If the only 'mechanism' story available is analyst convenience or measurement convenience, classify as a thin or fat convenience class, not an HPC category."

### Meta-Occam Principle
- **Verbatim:** "The *meta-Occam* principle also acts as a filter: if a proposed kind requires a bespoke stabilizer -- a mechanism that applies only to it, with no parallel elsewhere -- that's evidence of explanatory gerrymandering, not discovery."

## 5. When HPC is the Wrong Framework (When Categories are NOT HPC Kinds)

The chapter is quite explicit about this:

### Categories That Are NOT HPC Kinds
1. **Functional groupings without shared mechanisms:** chair, dessert, weapon -- useful but not maintained by shared causal structure.
2. **Epistemic kinds** (Khalidi 2013) -- "they serve our epistemic purposes -- classification, description, prediction -- but don't necessarily track causal structure in the world."
3. **Thin patterns:** byproducts of other mechanisms (preposition copying), too weakly maintained to count.
4. **Fat labels:** wastebasket categories that lump distinct causal clusters (adverb).
5. **Negative classes:** complement classes defined by absence (non-finite clause).
6. **Convention-driven projectibility:** patterns that predict well in corpora but lack causal grounding.
7. **The Madagascar Fallacy:** treating an ecosystem (English Grammar) as a single HPC when it's composed of many independent HPCs.

### The Madagascar Fallacy
- **Verbatim:** "Biological species are the paradigmatic HPC categories. *Lemur catta* (the ring-tailed lemur) is a homeostatic cluster maintained by interbreeding, shared ecology, and genetic transmission. But consider 'the fauna of Madagascar.'... The fauna of Madagascar isn't a species; it's an airline route plus a geological history."
- **Applied:** "Language is an ecosystem. The HPCs are the components -- constructions, lexemes, phoneme contrasts, sometimes families or paradigms -- not the grammar as a whole."

### Grain Errors
- **Verbatim:** "The failure modes aren't just ways a category can miss; they are also what grain errors look like. Zoom in too far and you start reifying smoke rings... Zoom out too far and you build wastebaskets... Choose the wrong logical operation -- defining by absence -- and you get complement classes."

## 6. How the Chapter Addresses the "Decorative Framework" Objection

The "decorative framework" objection (= HPC is just redescription / relabelling without explanatory gain) is addressed in multiple ways:

### 1. The Two-Diagnostic Test as Falsification Criterion
The chapter's primary response: HPC is NOT decorative because it is **falsifiable**. The two-diagnostic test provides a "strict decision procedure" that can reject categories. The framework "explains nothing if it excludes nothing" -- so the chapter devotes itself to showing what it excludes. Three failure modes (thin, fat, negative) are things the framework rules OUT.

### 2. The Audit Template
A 7-step reusable audit (Table 8.2) operationalizes HPC claims. Step 7 explicitly requires "falsifiers / stress tests" -- "What pattern would force reclassification?" This is not decorative; it structures a research programme.

### 3. Concrete Predictions That Non-HPC Accounts Don't Make
- **Perturbation sensitivity:** Genuine HPCs should fray when mechanisms weaken. Heritage language attrition (Polinsky 2018) and language death (Schmidt 1985 on Dyirbal) provide natural experiments confirming this prediction.
- **Edge-inward erosion pattern:** HPC predicts that weakened transmission will erode peripheral members first, core members last. This is confirmed empirically and was described (but not explained) by prototype theory.
- **Informative absences:** Boundary cases have structured gaps that map the mechanism landscape -- not random incompleteness.

### 4. Methodological Payoff
- **For typology:** Reframes cross-linguistic comparison. Stop asking "Is Subject universal?" and start asking "Is Subject in Language L maintained by the same mechanisms as in Language M?" This is a different (and answerable) question.
- **For theory:** "Labels without mechanisms are descriptive, not explanatory. If you claim that *voice* is a category in Language L, you need more than distributional evidence. You need to identify the mechanisms maintaining it."
- The framework "doesn't demand more evidence; it demands a different kind of evidence."

### 5. Explicit Anti-Inflation Discipline
The chapter is itself the anti-decoration argument. Its entire structure demonstrates that HPC is not a rubber stamp: it identifies and diagnoses categories that FAIL to be HPCs, provides worked examples, and specifies how to respond to each type of failure. The framework's value lies precisely in its capacity to say "no."

### 6. The O'Connor Citation
- O'Connor (2019) is cited as formalizing "how categories optimised for coordination can diverge from categories that track real structure" using evolutionary game theory. This grounds the inflation problem theoretically: useful labels can systematically diverge from real kinds.

## 7. Additional Notable Content

### The HPC-Kind Audit Template (7 steps)
1. Target + scope
2. Property cluster (profile): 8-15 candidate properties
3. Homeostatic stabilizers: acquisition, entrenchment, alignment, transmission, institutional enforcement
4. Projectibility claims: which would-bes does C sustain?
5. Boundary behaviour: expected borderline cases
6. Failure-mode gate: thin/fat/negative?
7. Falsifiers / stress tests: what would force reclassification?

### Coupling Typology (Section 8.6)
Three types of form-meaning coupling in HPCs:
- **Hard coupling** (phoneme contrasts): form directly realizes contrastive value; sharp boundaries, high projectibility
- **Loose coupling** (grammatical categories): form and meaning connected conventionally, can drift independently; tolerates gradience
- **Composite coupling** (constructions): form-value pairings stabilized by use; the construction-grammar sweet spot

These form a continuum defined by "how much work the interpretant contributes."

### Levels and Bearers (Section 8.7)
Four explanatory levels, each with distinct interventions:
1. Individual level: speaker's conditional disposition
2. Population level: distribution of dispositions across speakers/networks/time
3. Social conditioning: dialect, register, discourse community
4. Institutional: schooling, exams, style guides, tagsets, parsers

### Huddleston's "otherwise" Puzzle
Used as a demonstration case for why fat classes fail: "Huddleston's 'quite a puzzle' is the sound a wastebasket category makes when you ask it to behave like a kind."

### The Intuitions Caution (Scott-Phillips 2024)
- Acceptability judgments are "byproducts of relevance-seeking cognition -- they track whether a sentence *could* have optimal relevance, not whether it violates a grammatical essence."
- "Just because speakers agree that something is 'wrong' doesn't mean we've identified a real category."

### Sullivan (2016) Parallel to Cognitive Neuroscience
- Construct labels like "spatial memory" or "working memory" achieve institutional consensus while the causal reality splinters. Same problem as linguistic fat classes.
- But linguistics has a forcing function that neuroscience doesn't: communicative pressure. "If my *noun* doesn't align with yours, utterances misfire and learning stalls."

### Key Closing Line
"Categories generate habits; classes are merely named."
