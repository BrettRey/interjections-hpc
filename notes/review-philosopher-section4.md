# Review: Section 4 (Stabilisers: recruitment and maintenance)

**Reviewer role:** Philosopher of science specialising in natural kinds theory, HPC theory, and scientific realism.
**Scope:** Section 4 only (lines 257-447 of `main.tex`)
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Round:** Section-level review of drafted text

---

## 1. One-sentence assessment

Section 4 delivers a structurally sound recruitment/maintenance architecture with genuine perturbation tests, but it handles the ratchet-vs-feedback-loop distinction more honestly than the Round 2 outline did while still leaving the Boyd-vs-Slater positioning implicit in a way that will trouble philosophically informed readers.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The recruitment/maintenance distinction is clearly drawn and structurally enforced.** Subsections 4.1 and 4.2 do different theoretical work and the text makes the division explicit at lines 260-264: "how do items enter the cluster" vs. "what keeps the cluster coherent once they're in it." The Boyd citation at line 263-264 ("the pathways are historical; the stability is mechanistic") anchors the distinction in the right literature. This is a significant improvement over the outline, where the distinction was announced but the text could have blurred it. Here, the subsection architecture prevents drift.

- **The ratchet is called a ratchet.** Lines 375-380 explicitly say "This is a one-directional cascade, not a feedback loop" and "It's a ratchet rather than a cycle." This was my central concern in Round 2 (where the outline called all four stabilisers "feedback loops" without distinguishing their causal topology). The drafted text is honest about the asymmetry and then immediately argues that the ratchet contributes to cluster stability anyway -- it explains irreversibility, not active restoration. This is exactly the right move. The paper does not overclaim bidirectional homeostasis where the mechanism is unidirectional.

- **The perturbation test produces distinct signatures for distinct stabilisers.** Lines 411-414 make the key observation: prosodic integration leads to adverb-like reclassification (*pop*), referential restoration leads to noun-like reclassification (*God*). The distinctness of the consequences is the signature of genuine mechanisms rather than redescription. A paper that merely listed co-occurring properties could not generate this prediction. This is perhaps the section's strongest philosophical contribution.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The prosodic-syntactic coupling (4.2.1, lines 359-369) is asserted as bidirectional but the evidence is asymmetric.** The text says "these two properties reinforce each other bidirectionally" (line 361) and provides one direction clearly (prosodic separation makes syntactic integration harder, lines 362-363) and the reverse (syntactic independence licenses prosodic isolation, lines 363-365). But the Gehweiler citation at lines 366-368 ("sentence-initial position of invocations contributed to the emergence of the interjection") is evidence for the prosody-to-syntax direction only. The reverse direction (syntax licensing prosody) is stated but not evidenced. For a genuine feedback loop, both directions need support. This is not a fatal problem -- the reverse direction is plausible -- but the paper should either provide evidence for the syntax-to-prosody direction or downgrade the claim from "bidirectional reinforcement" to "primarily prosody-driven with plausible syntactic reinforcement."

- **Social indexing (4.2.3, lines 382-391) preserves lexical items, not property co-occurrence.** This was flagged in Round 2 and remains unresolved in the drafted text. The argument is that regional interjections survive because they do identity work. But survival of *lah* in Singapore is not the same as maintenance of the property cluster. The claim needs an additional step: does social indexing preserve the *clustering* (i.e., would removing social indexing cause properties to decouple), or does it merely preserve individual items that happen to have the cluster profile? The perturbation test in 4.4 does not test this stabiliser -- both perturbation examples target prosodic isolation and non-referentiality. Without a perturbation test for social indexing, its status as a cluster stabiliser (rather than a lexical preserver) remains underdetermined. One possible perturbation: does a regional interjection lose its cluster properties when adopted by out-group speakers who use it ironically or as a quotation? If so, social indexing is genuinely maintaining the cluster; if not, it's just maintaining inventory.

- **The convergent pathways subsection (4.1.3, lines 335-352) does important theoretical work but risks overstating convergence.** The Traugott & Dasher subjectification trajectory is invoked (lines 344-347), but the text immediately concedes that onomatopoeia creates interjections without any prior lexical history (lines 348-350). This is presented as evidence that "what unifies the pathways isn't the diachronic process but the cluster itself" (lines 351-352). But this claim is stronger than the evidence warrants. What unifies the pathways could be the cluster (an attractor basin in property space) or it could be the communicative niche (a functional gap that different processes fill). The distinction matters: if the cluster is an attractor, you predict that forms near the basin will be pulled in; if the niche is doing the work, you predict that communicative needs will be filled by whatever means are available. These make different empirical predictions about forms that are near the cluster but not in it (e.g., fillers like *um*, *uh*). The paper should be careful not to slide from "convergent outcomes" to "attractor basin" without marking the inferential step.

---

## 4. Key question

You now have three types of stabiliser: a genuine bidirectional feedback loop (prosodic-syntactic coupling), a unidirectional ratchet (non-referentiality cascade), and a social mechanism (social indexing). Boyd's original HPC account was designed for biological kinds where the homeostatic mechanisms are feedback loops in a fairly strict sense (gene flow, developmental constraints, ecological selection). You've expanded the mechanism inventory to include ratchets and social-identity processes. This is fine -- even necessary for linguistic kinds -- but it raises a question about the framework's boundaries.

**Is there any causal process that would *not* count as a stabiliser on your account?** If bleaching is a stabiliser, prosodic coupling is a stabiliser, social indexing is a stabiliser, and onomatopoeia is a stabiliser (recruitment-as-maintenance, per the outline), then the category "stabiliser" risks becoming vacuous. What would a non-stabiliser look like? The perturbation test gives you the right tool to answer this: a genuine stabiliser is one whose removal changes the clustering. But you only apply the perturbation test to two of your three stabilisers. Apply it to all three, or explain why the third resists perturbation testing, and the boundary question is answered.

---

## 5. Verdict

**Needs minor revision.**

The section is well-architected and philosophically serious. The recruitment/maintenance distinction is clear, the ratchet is honestly labelled, and the perturbation test produces genuinely distinct signatures. Three issues need attention before the section is fully defensible:

1. **Prosodic-syntactic coupling:** Provide evidence for the syntax-to-prosody direction or downgrade the bidirectionality claim. (Lines 361-369.)
2. **Social indexing:** Either apply the perturbation test to this stabiliser or explicitly flag that its status as a cluster-stabiliser (vs. item-preserver) is an open question. (Lines 382-391; 393-414.)
3. **Attractor basin language:** Mark the inferential step from "convergent outcomes" to "attractor basin" in 4.1.3 and 4.5 (lines 351-352, 437-440). The step is warranted but needs to be visible.

None of these require structural changes. They are paragraph-level revisions. The section is close to final.
