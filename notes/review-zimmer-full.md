# Review: Science Communication and Vividness

**Reviewer persona:** Carl Zimmer (science writer)
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**File reviewed:** `main.tex` (full paper)
**Focus:** Clarity and vividness for the non-specialist reader

---

## 1. Where the writing is vivid

The paper has genuine moments where the abstract framework becomes something you can see.

**The declining-attention trajectory (Introduction, ll. 26--44).** The opening is the strongest piece of concrete storytelling in the paper. It takes 440 years of intellectual history and compresses it into a single arc:

> "In 1586, William Bullokar wrote the earliest grammar of English. It included interjections."

That two-sentence setup works. The reader feels the weight of the category's age before learning it was later dismissed. And the kicker lands:

> "A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut."

That sentence earns its keep. It is concrete, ironic, and it does the work of three paragraphs of theoretical motivation in a single image. This is the paper at its best.

**The "damn" case study (Section 3.6, ll. 419--428).** The paper's best sustained concrete passage. The reader can hold two uses of the same word in their hands and feel the cluster breaking:

> "Damn as an interjection (Damn!) has the full cluster: prosodic isolation, syntactic non-integration, non-referentiality, emotive force. Damn as an attributive modifier (the damn car) retains emotive force but loses prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration -- it's embedded inside an NP."

This is where the abstract framework stops being abstract. The reader sees mechanisms at work because they can hear the difference between "Damn!" and "the damn car." The paper should do more of this.

**The non-referentiality ratchet (Section 4.2.2, ll. 584--591).** The image of a ratchet is exactly right. It captures a one-way mechanism in a single concrete object. "It's a ratchet rather than a cycle" -- that is a sentence a reader can hold in their mind. It does explanatory work. It makes you understand why forms don't drift back. More of the paper's mechanisms deserve images this clear.

**The "bruh" test case (Section 5.1, ll. 697--726).** Walking through five predictions for a single novel form is the right pedagogical move. The reader doesn't need to understand the whole framework to see that observing two properties and successfully predicting five others is impressive. The ranked assessment of prediction quality ("These predictions aren't equally impressive") is honest and makes the passage more credible. Science writing is always more persuasive when it concedes what's weak.

**The perturbation test, concretely (Section 4.3, ll. 614--626).** The "pop" and "God" examples make the perturbation test feel like something you could actually do rather than a philosophical construct:

> "Neutralize prosodic isolation -- integrate the form into the clause -- and it starts behaving like a different category. This is what happens with pop in pop went the cork."

That is vivid. A reader can hear "pop went the cork" and understand immediately what "neutralize prosodic isolation" means, even if they've never encountered the term.

## 2. Where the writing is abstract

**The three-part slogan (Section 2.1, ll. 108--115).** This is the most important passage in the paper, and it reads like a philosophy seminar, not like a description of something real:

> "a profile of co-occurring properties, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible relative to purposes. The first two legs matter because they make the third possible. The profile is the visible pattern. The mechanisms are the explanation. Projectibility is the payoff."

Every noun here is an abstraction. "Profile," "properties," "mechanisms," "purposes," "pattern," "explanation," "payoff." The reader is told these things matter but given nothing to see. Before unpacking the slogan, the paper needs a concrete example -- even a one-sentence preview: "A robin and a penguin are both birds, and you can predict that a newly discovered bird will have feathers, lay eggs, and build nests -- not because 'bird' has a definition that requires these things, but because the same developmental mechanisms keep producing them together. That predictive power is what HPC theory is about." Something like that. A single warm-blooded animal before the cold abstractions.

**The stabiliser definition (Section 2.1, ll. 117--122).** This is a key term and it is introduced via quotation and negation:

> "something counts as a stabiliser if removing it would change the clustering -- if the category would fragment, drift, or dissolve."

The definition-by-removal is philosophically correct but experientially empty. The reader hasn't yet seen what "change the clustering" looks like. Consider a brief image before the definition: a tent held up by poles (remove one and the shape changes), a river held in its channel by its banks (breach them and the water spreads). Something physical. The perturbation test examples in Section 4.3 eventually provide this concreteness, but the reader has to wait two full sections. Move a preview forward, or give the definition a brief concrete anchor when it first appears.

**The field-relative projectibility section (Section 2.3, ll. 170--220).** This passage introduces the subscript notation that is architecturally central to the paper. But the jade analogy (ll. 173--174) is doing too much work for too little payoff. "Jade is a natural kind in gemology but not in geology" is a sentence that requires specialized knowledge to land. Most readers will need to look up jadeite and nephrite. The noun/name example that follows (ll. 176--179) is much better -- syntacticians and semanticists tracking the same word differently is a concrete image a linguist can grasp. Lead with that. The jade can be a parenthetical.

The subscript notation itself (interjection_syn, interjection_sem, interjection_int) is introduced with bullet points full of technical terms ("use-conditional meaning cluster," "nondisplaceable," "perspective-dependent," "descriptively ineffable"). Each bullet reads like a compressed abstract. Consider a single concrete sentence for each: "The syntactician's interjection is the one you can hear standing alone at the edge of a sentence. The semanticist's interjection is the one that tells you how the speaker feels without changing what's true. The conversation analyst's interjection is the one that keeps the dialogue going." Then the technical details.

**Section 5.2 (Field-relative projectibility, ll. 729--809).** This is the longest and densest section in the paper. It reprises the subscript architecture from Section 2.3 and works through the attributive "damn" case. The problem is not that it is wrong but that it is exhausting. The reader encounters "interjection_syn," "interjection_sem," and "interjection_int" over and over in a passage that is almost entirely mechanism talk with no new concrete examples. The "damn" case carries the whole section, but it was already introduced in Section 3.6. By the time we reach "Neither CGEL's syntax nor Potts's semantics alone predicts this pattern of convergence and divergence" (ll. 806--807), the reader is so deep in subscript notation that the payoff feels like notation rather than insight.

**The "pragmatic kind" passage (Section 5.4, ll. 845--881).** This is the paper's distinctive theoretical contribution. It deserves to feel like an arrival. Instead:

> "Not all kinds project in the same domain. Nouns project morphosyntactically: they take determiners, inflect for number, fill argument slots."

This is correct but abstract. The reader needs to feel the contrast. Nouns let you predict grammar; interjections let you predict conversation. That is a striking claim. It deserves a concrete illustration, not just a list of technical properties. What does it look like, in practice, for a category to be a "pragmatic kind"? What does a field linguist do differently when they recognize a pragmatic kind versus a morphosyntactic one?

**The HPC predictions section (Section 6.1, ll. 897--963).** The four predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10) are well-structured and each has a named defeat condition, which is excellent scientific practice. But they are presented as a list of theoretical predictions with evidence marshalled for each. A reader who isn't already invested in the HPC framework will find this section abstract. Consider framing at least one prediction as a story: "If HPC theory is right, here is something surprising that should be true. And here is what we found."

## 3. The key concept test

After reading this paper, can I explain the four key concepts?

**Homeostatic property cluster:** Yes, mostly. I understand it as a bundle of properties that reliably co-occur because mechanisms keep them together, not because a definition requires them. The paper makes this clear in Section 2.1 and reinforces it throughout. What I lack is a single vivid image to anchor it. The paper gives me the slogan ("profile, stabilized, projectible") but no picture.

**Stabiliser:** Partially. I understand it as a mechanism that maintains the cluster -- remove it and the cluster changes. The non-referentiality ratchet is an excellent concrete instance. But the term is introduced abstractly (Section 2.1) and doesn't become concrete until Section 4. A reader encountering "stabiliser" for the first time in Section 2 has to hold an empty term in memory for several pages.

**Perturbation test:** Yes. This is the concept I understand most clearly, because the paper gives me "pop" and "God" immediately after defining it. The two examples are concrete and contrastive. The perturbation test is the paper's most successfully communicated concept.

**Pragmatic kind:** Only partially. I understand it as a category whose strongest predictions are about use rather than truth conditions. But the passage that introduces it (Section 5.4) is abstract, and I don't have a clear sense of what it would mean to discover another pragmatic kind, or what you would do differently if you recognized one. The concept is theoretically important but experientially thin.

## 4. The opening test

Does the Introduction hook a reader who doesn't already care about interjections?

**Almost.** The Bullokar opening is strong. The trajectory from 1586 to 2005 is well-told. The kicker about the textbook cutting the category is memorable. The problem is in the transition from historical narrative to theoretical framework (ll. 56--83). The paper moves from "interjections have been dismissed" to "classical category theory can't handle this" to "HPC theory provides the framework" in a single page. A reader who doesn't already know what HPC theory is will feel the gear-shift. The narrative momentum of the opening dissolves into framework talk.

The question "what does categorizing something as an interjection let you predict?" (ll. 64--65) is exactly the right question, and it's stated clearly. But it comes after a sentence about "fat class rather than a genuine kind" (l. 62) that most readers won't understand without having read the book. The question should come sooner and more forcefully. It is the paper's organizing question. Let it organize the reader's attention from the start, before introducing the framework.

One concrete suggestion for the Introduction: after the trajectory from Bullokar to Huddleston & Pullum, before introducing HPC, give the reader a single example of what an interjection prediction looks like. Something like: "If someone coins a new interjection tomorrow -- say, 'yikes' or 'sheesh' in a language that doesn't have them yet -- you can predict, without ever hearing it in context, that it won't inflect, won't take a determiner, and will stand at the edge of a sentence. That predictive power is what this paper is about." Then introduce HPC as the framework that explains how such predictions work.

## 5. One concrete image suggestion

The paper's central claim is that interjections are a "thin-but-genuine" natural kind, maintained by mechanisms rather than defined by essences. The paper calls this a property cluster held together by stabilisers. But the reader never gets a physical image for what that looks like.

Here is one:

**The river delta.** A river delta isn't defined by any single channel. Channels shift, appear, disappear. Individual channels are ephemeral. But the delta persists because the same forces -- the gradient of the land, the volume of the water, the resistance of the sediment -- keep producing channels in the same broad shape. You can predict where new channels will form without knowing which old ones will silt up. The delta is the cluster; the gradient, water, and sediment are the stabilisers; the ability to predict new channels is the projectibility.

Interjections are a delta. Individual forms come and go -- "nay" silts up, "yeah" carves a new channel, "bruh" appears where no channel was before. But the same forces (prosodic isolation, non-referentiality, emotive need) keep producing forms with the same profile. The delta's shape is the interjection cluster, and the fact that you can predict the shape of a new channel from the landscape is the pragmatic projectibility that makes interjections a genuine kind.

This image could appear in the Introduction, where the reader needs it most, or at the start of Section 2, before the slogan. It wouldn't replace the theoretical apparatus. It would give the reader something to see while the apparatus is being assembled.

---

## Summary of recommendations

1. **Add a concrete preview** before the HPC slogan in Section 2.1 -- a single familiar example (birds, rivers, anything) that shows what "property cluster maintained by mechanisms" looks like.
2. **Give "stabiliser" a physical image** at first introduction, not just a definition-by-removal.
3. **Lead with the organizing question** earlier in the Introduction, before the framework talk.
4. **Add a one-sentence concrete gloss** for each subscript category when introduced in Section 2.3.
5. **Thin Section 5.2** -- the field-relative projectibility section is too long and too dense; the theoretical point is clear by the second paragraph but the section keeps going.
6. **Make "pragmatic kind" feel like an arrival** -- add a concrete illustration of what it means for a kind to project pragmatically rather than morphosyntactically.
7. **Consider the river-delta image** (or something equivalently concrete) as the paper's central metaphor, introduced early and available throughout.

The paper's argument is strong. Its evidence is well-organized. Its weakest point is not logic but vividness: the reader can follow the argument but can't always see the thing the argument is about.
