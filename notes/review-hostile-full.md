# Review: "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster" -- Full Paper

**Reviewer 2** | Journal of Historical Pragmatics | Full Paper Review

---

## 0. Prior rounds: final assessment

I reviewed this paper twice in outline. My three original concerns were: (A) HPC does no work that prototype theory cannot, (B) "prediction" is post hoc accommodation, (C) projectibility is circular. In Round 2 I judged A and C addressed and B partially addressed, with a verdict of minor R&R contingent on the full paper delivering evidence on its own predictions. The question now is whether the full paper delivers.

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster sustained by diachronic recruitment pathways and synchronic stabilisers, with a distinctive contribution being field-relative projectibility and the claim that interjections are a "pragmatic kind" whose strongest inferences are about use, not denotation.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The paper is honest about what it shows and what it does not.** The criterion of success stated in Section 2 ("this paper succeeds if it shows that categorizing a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes") is clear, falsifiable, and actually revisited in the paper's own terms. The partial-observation inference argument in Section 5.1 (the *bruh* worked example) delivers on this criterion: observing prosodic isolation and stance meaning in a novel form licenses predictions about non-inflection, embedding resistance, modification resistance, interactional distribution, and etymological source. Crucially, the paper concedes which of these predictions are impressive and which are near-entailments ("predictions (a) and (c) are close to entailments"), which is a level of self-assessment I rarely see. I asked in Round 1 for non-circular projectibility; the full paper provides it.

- **The predictions are now tested, not merely stated.** This was my central demand in Round 2: "the paper must deliver evidence on at least two of its own predictions." Section 6.1 engages all four predictions against the evidence available. P1 (asymmetric fraying) is evaluated against the *damn*/*come on*/*look* gradient: tight diagnostics (subject, tense) eroded before loose ones (complement frame, prosodic isolation). P3 (phase transition) is evaluated against Gehweiler's corpus clustering of phonological modifications in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The paper concedes that both evaluations are preliminary ("suggestive," "finer-grained historical data would be needed"). P5 and P10 are presented as predictions for future work, with specific proposals for how to test them (GloWbE conditioning variables for P5; comparative perturbation for P10). Two tested, two prospective: this is the distribution I specified as "honest."

- **The subscript architecture earns its complexity -- barely.** Section 5.2 is the strongest section of the paper. The decomposition of interjection into three distinct HPCs with overlapping but non-identical extensions, maintained by different mechanisms, is not just notational. The attributive *damn* case is the proof: interjection_syn predictions fail (the form is syntactically integrated), interjection_sem predictions hold (use-conditional semantics persist). Neither CGEL's syntax alone nor Potts's semantics alone predicts this pattern of convergence and divergence. The subscript architecture does, because it tracks which mechanisms are active in each construction. This is the single best argument in the paper for HPC over prototype theory, because prototype theory has no natural way to predict that two members of "the same category" should diverge systematically when evaluated by different disciplinary criteria.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The perturbation test still does not trace a full cascade, and this remains the paper's most vulnerable point.** Section 4.3 applies the perturbation test to two cases: neutralising prosodic isolation (the *pop* case) and restoring referential content (the *God* case). The paper notes that each removal produces "distinct consequences" -- adverb-like reclassification vs. noun-like reclassification -- and calls this "the signature of genuine mechanisms rather than redescription." But this is still just showing that two features matter, not that the *co-occurrence* of features is causally sustained in a way that distinguishes HPC from weighted-feature analysis. The paper identifies three synchronic stabilisers (prosodic-syntactic coupling, the non-referentiality ratchet, social indexing) and claims they interact. But nowhere does it trace a cascade: if I remove prosodic isolation, the paper claims syntactic non-integration should erode (via prosodic-syntactic coupling), and loss of syntactic non-integration should... what? The ratchet mechanism is described as one-directional (bleaching removes reference, which removes argument structure), so it shouldn't cascade backward. Social indexing is described as preserving items rather than the cluster. The paper has three stabilisers but no account of how they interact as a system. Without this, the "homeostatic" in "homeostatic property cluster" is a metaphor, not a model. A weighted-feature prototype analysis with the same three features ranked by diagnosticity would yield the same perturbation results. The paper needs at least one explicit cascade argument: removing stabiliser A causes change X, which, via the coupling with stabiliser B, causes change Y. The *pop* and *God* examples show independent perturbations, not cascading ones.

  **Actionable fix:** Trace one full cascade path. For example: when prosodic isolation is neutralised (the *pop* case), does syntactic integration follow (via prosodic-syntactic coupling), and does syntactic integration then increase pressure toward referential content (because argument slots create referential demands)? If yes, this is a genuine cascade predicted by the mechanism model. If no, the stabilisers are independent features, and "homeostatic" is doing no work that "correlated" does not.

- **The category-vs.-function argument (Section 5.3) is strong but undercut by the paper's own logic.** The five-point comparison showing what "interjection" adds beyond "supplement" is effective: non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, and diachronic trajectory are all predictions that the function label alone cannot provide. But the paper's own subscript architecture (Section 5.2) decomposes these five predictions across three distinct HPCs. Non-referentiality and non-inflection belong to interjection_syn. Emotive force and diachronic trajectory belong to interjection_sem and interjection_int respectively. Free coinage is... where? The paper doesn't assign it. So the category-vs.-function argument works for the undifferentiated label "interjection" but becomes unclear once the subscripts are applied. Does interjection_syn, on its own, add enough beyond "supplement" to justify a distinct syntactic category? The paper says yes ("a thin syntactic HPC is still a syntactic HPC") but the evidence for interjection_syn is precisely that supplement function, non-inflection, and non-argumenthood co-occur -- which is just the distributional definition restated. The subscript architecture is the paper's best weapon against prototype theory, but it also threatens to dissolve the very category whose reality the paper is defending. The paper should address this tension explicitly.

  **Actionable fix:** Clarify whether the category-vs.-function argument applies to the undifferentiated label, to each subscripted HPC individually, or to all three jointly. If interjection_syn is too thin to add much beyond "supplement," say so and argue that the three-HPC intersection is where the inferential richness lives.

- **The diachronic evidence is good but shallow for a JHP submission.** Gehweiler (2008) provides the backbone. The COHA *nay*-to-*yeah* data and the GloWbE regional variation are described in a paragraph each. For a journal whose readers expect detailed engagement with historical corpus data, this is thin. The claim that phonological modifications "cluster in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries" (cited from Gehweiler) is doing heavy work for P3 (phase transition), but the paper doesn't present, visualize, or interrogate this data. It reports Gehweiler's conclusions rather than engaging with the evidence. The Traugott & Dasher subjectification framework is introduced in Section 4.1.3 but receives only a paragraph of application. For JHP specifically, the diachronic spine needs more corpus engagement: frequency trajectories, collocational shifts, register patterns. The theoretical apparatus is appropriate for the venue, but the empirical depth is not yet at the level JHP readers will expect.

  **Actionable fix:** Expand the corpus evidence for at least one recruitment pathway. For Path 1 (religious noun to interjection), provide frequency data from COHA or EEBO for the transition stages. For the *nay*/*yeah* shift, show the frequency crossover with decade-level granularity. This would make the diachronic claims testable and would speak directly to P3.

## 4. Key question

**"Your subscript architecture is the paper's most original contribution, and the attributive *damn* case is its strongest argument. But here is what troubles me: once you decompose 'interjection' into three distinct HPCs, each maintained by different mechanisms, what is the status of the intersection? You call it a 'pragmatic kind.' But is the intersection itself an HPC? If so, what are its mechanisms -- all nine (three per subscripted HPC), or some subset? If not, what theoretical status does it have? Because if the intersection is not itself an HPC, then 'interjection' (unsubscripted) is not a kind in Boyd's sense; it is a convenient label for the overlap region of three kinds. And if it is an HPC, you need to identify the mechanisms that maintain the intersection as such, not just the mechanisms that maintain each subscripted cluster independently."**

---

## 5. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit (minor).**

The full paper substantially delivers on what the outline promised. The projectibility argument is non-circular and well-illustrated. The predictions are tested (two of four), with honest concessions about evidential limitations. The subscript architecture is genuinely novel and does work that I cannot replicate with prototype theory alone. The writing is clear, the structure is sound, and the self-assessment is refreshingly honest.

The paper does not, however, fully answer my Round 2 concern about cascade tracing. The perturbation test shows that stabilisers matter individually, but not that they interact as a homeostatic system. This is a real gap, not a quibble, because "homeostatic" is the operative word in the framework's name, and the paper needs to show that the word earns its place. The diachronic evidence, while appropriate for the argument, is thin for JHP's expectations. And the relationship between the subscript architecture and the unsubscripted category label needs clarification.

None of these issues require architectural revision. They require the paper to do more with the evidence it already has (cascade tracing, corpus data) and to address a tension it has created (subscripts vs. the undifferentiated label). A careful revision should resolve all three.

I am no longer predisposed to think HPC is decorative vocabulary. The subscript architecture and the attributive *damn* case changed my mind on that specific point. But I remain unconvinced that the "homeostatic" part has been demonstrated, as opposed to the "property cluster" part. The paper shows there is a cluster. It shows the cluster is maintained. It does not yet show that the maintenance is homeostatic -- that the stabilisers interact to restore the cluster under perturbation, rather than each independently preserving its own feature. That is the difference between a genuine HPC and a correlated property bundle, and it is the remaining gap.

One-line justification for R&R over Accept: The paper demonstrates a property cluster with maintenance but has not yet shown the maintenance is genuinely homeostatic (interacting stabilisers, not merely co-present ones), and the diachronic evidence is thinner than JHP expects.
