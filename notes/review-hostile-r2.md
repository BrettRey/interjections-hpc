# Review: "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster" -- Round 2

**Reviewer 2** | Journal of Historical Pragmatics | Round 2

---

## 0. Round 1 concerns: status

I raised three main weaknesses with actionable fixes in Round 1. I assess each below.

### Concern A: HPC does no work that prototype theory cannot do

**Round 1 actionable fix:** "Construct an explicit comparison. Take one boundary case (say, routine formulae) and show step by step what HPC analysis yields that a prototype analysis misses. If you cannot do this, the framework is decorative."

**Status: Substantially addressed.**

The revision does three things I asked for. First, Section 2.2 now provides a genuine engagement with prototype theory, correctly identifying what it captures (graded membership, fuzzy boundaries, family resemblance) and articulating what it allegedly misses: mechanism-derived predictions. Second, and more importantly, Section 6.1 names four framework predictions (P1 asymmetric fraying, P3 phase transition, P5 conditioning recovers structure, P10 hub-node asymmetry), each with a named defeat condition. Third, the closing line of that section is the right one: "Each prediction has a named defeat condition. Prototype theory generates none of them."

I accept that these are predictions that prototype theory does not generate in its standard formulation. That said, my acceptance is provisional. The predictions are drawn from Reynolds (2026), which I have not read, and the outline does not actually *test* any of them against interjection data -- it merely states them and names their defeat conditions. At the outline stage this is acceptable; in the full paper it will not be. The paper must either report evidence bearing on at least two of these predictions or explicitly frame them as an agenda for future empirical work. If the latter, the paper's contribution shrinks to "interjections are a plausible HPC, and here is how you would test that claim" -- publishable but less impactful.

### Concern B: "Prediction" is post hoc accommodation, not genuine prediction

**Round 1 actionable fix:** "Either (a) provide an explicit mechanism-strength model (even qualitative), with evidence, and derive boundary predictions from it; or (b) downgrade 'predicts' to 'accommodates' throughout."

**Status: Partially addressed.**

The revision adds the perturbation test (Section 4.2) and applies it to two cases: removing prosodic isolation causes reclassification as adverb (the *pop* case), and restoring referential content returns the form to the noun category. This is qualitative mechanism-strength reasoning of the kind I asked for in option (a). It is not quantitative, but I acknowledge that quantitative mechanism-strength modelling for lexical categories is probably not realistic at this point.

However, the problem is not fully resolved. The perturbation test shows that individual stabilisers matter -- removing them changes something. But it does not yet show that HPC's predictions about the *pattern* of co-occurrence differ from what you would get by ranking features in a prototype model by cue validity or diagnosticity. Consider: a prototype theorist could equally say, "prosodic isolation is the most diagnostic feature; remove it and the item is no longer categorised as an interjection." This is just weighted feature analysis, not a qualitatively different framework. What HPC adds, supposedly, is the claim that the *co-occurrence* of features is causally sustained, not merely statistically observed. The perturbation test gestures at this, but the paper needs to show that the perturbation results are *asymmetric* -- that removing one stabiliser cascades differently from removing another -- in a way that weighted-feature or prototype analysis would not predict. The paper half-promises this ("each stabiliser's removal produces distinct consequences, which is the signature of a genuine mechanism") but does not deliver the asymmetry argument in enough detail to be convincing.

### Concern C: Projectibility is circular

**Round 1 actionable fix:** "Identify at least two projectible inferences that are *not* part of the defining property cluster."

**Status: Addressed.**

This was my sharpest objection, and the revision takes it seriously. Section 5.1 introduces partial-observation inference: observing *some* properties (prosodic isolation, stance meaning) in a novel form (*bruh*) licenses prediction of *other* properties (non-inflection, resistance to embedding, resistance to restrictive modification, etymology from content word). The key move is that the predicted properties are not the observed ones -- the inference is cross-property, not circular. This is Boyd's projectibility, correctly applied.

Section 5.2 adds field-relative projectibility: the same category label generates different inferences for conversation analysts (interactional distribution), historical linguists (etymological trajectory), and lexicographers (diagnostic resistance). Section 5.3 addresses the category-vs.-function question from CGEL, showing what the category label adds beyond "supplement." These are genuine inferential payoffs.

My one remaining concern: the partial-observation inference argument works only if the inter-property predictions are non-trivial. Some of them are: predicting etymological trajectory from synchronic prosodic behaviour is genuinely non-obvious. But "non-inflection predicts non-argumenthood" is close to a definitional entailment (forms that lack inflection are thereby excluded from argument positions that require agreement). The paper should be careful to distinguish mechanistically grounded inferences (impressive) from quasi-definitional ones (less impressive).

## 1. One-sentence summary

The revised outline argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster sustained by identifiable diachronic and synchronic stabilisers, and that the category's distinctive theoretical contribution is its primarily pragmatic projectibility -- licensing non-trivial cross-property inferences about prosodic, interactional, and diachronic behaviour of novel forms.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The projectibility argument is no longer circular.** The partial-observation inference framework (Section 5.1) and the *bruh* worked example are exactly what I demanded. The move from "interjections have interjection properties" to "observing two properties of a novel form licenses prediction of five others" is the right kind of inferential structure. The field-relative projectibility analysis (Section 5.2) is genuinely interesting: the claim that the *same category* projects differently for different disciplinary purposes extends HPC theory in a way I have not seen elsewhere. If this holds up in the full paper, it is a real contribution.

- **The stabiliser analysis has real mechanistic content.** The four named feedback loops in Section 4.2 are specific and, importantly, distinct in kind: a bidirectional reinforcement loop (prosodic-syntactic coupling), a unidirectional cascade (non-referentiality), a recruitment-maintenance dual-function mechanism (onomatopoeia), and a social-indexing mechanism. This is not a list of correlated features; it is a mechanistic model. The perturbation test, while still underdeveloped, shows that each stabiliser's removal produces a different outcome (reclassification as adverb vs. return to noun category), which is a meaningful asymmetry.

- **The framework predictions with defeat conditions are a genuine advance over Round 1.** Naming four predictions and their defeat conditions (Section 6.1) converts the HPC claim from post hoc accommodation into a testable programme. P3 (phase transition in genesis) is particularly well suited to JHP: Gehweiler's corpus data might actually speak to whether bleaching shows a tipping point or a smooth cline. This is the kind of empirical engagement the journal wants.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The predictions are stated but not tested, even preliminarily.** Four HPC-specific predictions are named in Section 6.1, but none is evaluated against even the evidence already available in the outline. Does Gehweiler's four-stage model show a tipping point (P3) or a smooth cline? The outline explicitly wonders ("Gehweiler's corpus data may speak to this") but does not answer. Does the *pop* case illustrate asymmetric fraying (P1) -- do tight diagnostics (non-inflection, complement resistance) erode before loose ones (prosodic isolation)? The data to at least attempt a preliminary answer are right there in the outline's own examples. The paper cannot defer all four predictions to future work and still claim that HPC "predicts" rather than "might predict." At minimum, the full paper must evaluate P3 (phase transition) using Gehweiler's data and P1 (asymmetric fraying) using the *pop*/*damn*/*come on* cases. If two of four predictions are tested, even inconclusively, the paper is honest; if zero are tested, the predictions are aspirational, and the paper is weaker than it needs to be.

- **The perturbation test, while improved, still lacks the asymmetry argument needed to distinguish HPC from weighted-feature analysis.** As noted in my assessment of Concern B above, a prototype theorist could replicate the perturbation results by ranking features by cue validity. The paper needs to show that HPC perturbation analysis yields something that weighted-feature ranking does not -- specifically, that removing one stabiliser cascades through the system (via the feedback loops named in 4.2) in a way that removing another does not, and that these cascade patterns are predicted by the mechanism model, not merely observed post hoc. The four feedback loops are well-described, but the perturbation section does not explicitly trace a cascade path. For example: removing prosodic isolation should, via loop 1, also reduce syntactic non-integration, which should, via loop 2, increase pressure toward argument structure. Does it? The *pop* example might illustrate this, but the connection is not drawn. Tracing one full cascade would distinguish HPC from weighted-feature analysis decisively.

- **"Thin-but-genuine HPC" (Section 6.2) needs sharper criteria.** The outline places interjections on a typology between "wastebasket" (no shared mechanism, no convergence) and "robust skeleton category" (nouns, verbs), comparable to adjectives. But the criteria for this placement are not explicit. What makes an HPC "thin" vs. "robust"? Mechanism density is mentioned but not defined. How many stabilisers suffice? How strong must the feedback be? Without criteria, the typology is a metaphor, not a classification. The paper should specify at least two dimensions (e.g., number of independent stabilisers, number of cross-domain predictions) on which interjections score lower than nouns but higher than wastebasket categories, and provide evidence on each. Otherwise "thin-but-genuine" is just hedging.

## 4. Key question

**"You identify four HPC-specific predictions and their defeat conditions. This is exactly what I asked for in Round 1, and I appreciate the precision. My question is about evidence. You have Gehweiler's four-stage corpus data, you have the *pop*/*damn*/*come on* boundary cases, you have GloWbE regional data. Can you, right now, tell me whether any one of those four predictions is *supported* by the evidence already in the outline, or are all four purely prospective? Because if the answer is 'all four are untested,' then what the paper actually demonstrates is that HPC generates testable predictions about interjections -- which is interesting but falls short of showing that HPC *works* for interjections."**

---

## 5. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit (minor).**

The revision addresses my Round 1 concerns with seriousness and specificity. The projectibility argument is no longer circular. The HPC-vs.-prototype distinction is no longer merely asserted; it is operationalised through named predictions with defeat conditions. The stabiliser analysis has genuine mechanistic content. These are real improvements.

What remains is execution, not conception. The predictions must be tested, not just stated. The perturbation analysis must trace at least one cascade path to distinguish HPC from weighted-feature analysis. The "thin-but-genuine" placement needs explicit criteria. These are tasks for the full paper, not for another outline revision, so I am recommending minor R&R rather than major. The architecture is sound; the load-bearing walls need to be built.

One-line justification: The theoretical framework is now well articulated and the empirical predictions are specified, but the paper must deliver evidence on at least two of its own predictions to justify the claim that HPC does more than generate a testable research programme.
