# Review: Social Positioning of the Paper

**Reviewer persona:** Howard Becker, *Writing for Social Scientists*
**Paper:** "English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster"
**Target venue:** Journal of Historical Pragmatics

---

## 1. Tribal Dynamics

The JHP tribe cares about change over time, about mechanisms of pragmatic change, about Traugott and subjectification, about corpus evidence, about the life of forms in interaction. They are comfortable with diachrony as a first-class explanatory mode. They are not, by default, readers of philosophy of science. Boyd (1999) and Slater (2015) are foreign names to them.

**Where the balance is right:**

The paper earns its JHP credentials in Sections 4.1 and 4.4. The Gehweiler (2008) material on the Jesus-to-gee trajectory is the best section of the paper from a tribal perspective. It does exactly what a JHP reader wants: it traces a documented diachronic pathway, cites a paper published *in their own journal*, and shows stages. The Traugott & Dasher (2002) subjectification framing in Section 4.1.3 is also well judged. This is speaking the tribe's language. A JHP reader will nod at "content > procedural, nonsubjective > intersubjective" (line 537-538) because that is their intellectual furniture.

The COHA and GloWbE corpus material (Section 4.5) is also tribal currency. Historical corpus data is what this audience works with.

**Where it tips:**

The paper opens with philosophy of science and stays there for all of Section 2. A JHP reader who picks this up expecting historical pragmatics will spend two full pages reading about Boyd, Slater, property clusters, perturbation tests, and subscript notation before encountering any interjections at all. The entire subsection "HPC vs. prototype theory" (2.2) is a theory-comparison exercise that belongs in a philosophy-of-science venue, not a pragmatics journal. A JHP reader doesn't lie awake wondering whether HPC beats prototype theory. They lie awake wondering how *gee* got to be an interjection and whether *bruh* is heading the same way.

The subscript notation (interjection_syn, interjection_sem, interjection_int) introduced in Section 2.3 and developed in Section 5.2 is the most foreign element. It is analytical philosophy notation dropped into a historical pragmatics paper. Whether it earns its keep analytically is a separate question; socially, it announces: "I am from a different discipline and I have brought my notation." This is risky. Some readers will find it clarifying. Others will feel they are being taught a new formalism they didn't ask for.

---

## 2. The Threat Assessment

**What will feel threatening:**

1. **The framework import.** The paper asks JHP readers to adopt apparatus from philosophy of science (Boyd, Slater, HPC, perturbation tests, stabilisers, projectibility) as a precondition for reading about interjections. That is a lot of trust to ask for. Most readers won't know whether HPC is a live framework in philosophy or a fringe position. They have no way to evaluate it and are being asked to take the author's word.

2. **The verdicts on major authorities.** Lines 33-44 deliver three rapid judgments on canonical figures: Jespersen "denied," Quirk "conceded ... marginal and anomalous," Huddleston & Pullum "dropped it" because there "really isn't anything interesting for a grammar to say." These are efficient, but they position the author as an evaluator sitting above the tradition. A JHP reader who respects these figures (and they all do) may bristle at the speed of the dismissal, even if the characterisations are accurate.

3. **The "criterion of success" declaration.** Lines 135-138: "This paper succeeds if it shows that categorizing a novel form as an interjection licenses non-trivial inferences for specific purposes. If it merely lists co-occurring properties, it has described a cluster, not demonstrated a kind." This is bold and clarifying, but it also tells the audience: "I will judge my own paper by a criterion you may not share." A JHP reader's criterion of success might be: "Did you show me something new about how interjections change over time?" The paper's criterion is epistemological, not historical.

4. **The numbered predictions (P1, P3, P5, P10).** Section 6.1 presents four numbered predictions with defeat conditions. This is hypothesis-testing rhetoric from experimental science. It feels out of register for JHP, which publishes close historical and corpus work, not prediction-and-defeat-condition frameworks.

**What will feel inviting:**

1. **The Gehweiler material.** A JHP reader will recognize the four-stage bleaching trajectory (lines 451-464) as good historical pragmatics. This is the paper's strongest tribal credential.

2. **The Ameka-Wilkins resolution.** Lines 1003-1008: "Both analysts were right about the data. The disagreement was about the framework, and HPC provides the framework that accommodates both observations." This is generous and will appeal to readers who know the debate. It positions the framework as a peacemaker, not a conqueror.

3. **The dynamic stability argument.** Section 4.5 (nay declining, yeah rising, regional forms persisting) is good historical pragmatics. The observation that the cluster persists while its membership turns over is a genuinely useful idea for historical linguists. This is showing them something they can use.

4. **The bruh/yeet/oof material.** Lines 665-670. Contemporary data that shows the framework can handle new forms. JHP readers are interested in ongoing change, not just completed change.

## 3. Attack or Invitation?

**Attacks:**

- Lines 33-34: "Jespersen (1924: 90) denied that interjections are a lexical category at all." The verb *denied* is adversarial. Jespersen had reasons. The paper doesn't give them. A reader who respects Jespersen will wonder why not.

- Lines 35-36: "Quirk et al. (1985: 67) conceded the category but called it 'marginal and anomalous.'" The verb *conceded* implies reluctance, as though Quirk et al. grudgingly admitted something obvious. They were describing what they saw, not making a political concession.

- Lines 41-44: the Huddleston & Pullum pair -- included interjections in the comprehensive grammar, dropped them from the textbook because "there really isn't anything interesting for a grammar to say." The juxtaposition is clever ("A category real enough for the definitive grammar but thin enough for the textbook to cut," line 43-44), but it is a gotcha. It makes H&P look inconsistent rather than acknowledging the reasonable textbook rationale: space constraints require cuts, and interjections may genuinely be less productive pedagogically than other categories.

- Lines 56-57: "Classical category theory can't handle this." Flat declaration. No hedging, no acknowledgement that classical category theory handles plenty of other things well.

- Line 1012: "English interjections aren't marginal or anomalous." Direct contradiction of Quirk. This is the strongest attack in the paper, positioned for maximum impact at the start of the conclusion.

**Invitations:**

- Lines 1003-1008: The Ameka-Wilkins resolution. "Both analysts were right about the data." This is Rapoport's Rules in action. It works.

- Lines 389-392: "In HPC terms, routine formulae share some cluster properties (prosodic isolation, pragmatic function) but are maintained by additional mechanisms (social convention, addressee-directedness) that interjections don't require. They sit at the boundary, as HPC predicts." The phrase "as HPC predicts" recurs throughout the paper and is its most effective rhetorical move. It turns embarrassments into confirmations. This is showing the tribe something they couldn't see before.

- Lines 792-793: "A syntactician and a conversation analyst can carve the same words differently without either being wrong." This is diplomatic and useful. It offers the framework as a way to stop arguing, not as a weapon.

- Lines 906-908: "HPC doesn't replace prototype theory's descriptive achievements. It adds something prototype theory can't provide." This is the right framing: addition, not replacement. But it comes after two pages of theory (Section 2) that felt more like replacement than addition.

## 4. The Furniture Question

**Committee-work prose** (exists because the author feels obligated):

- The entire roadmap paragraph (lines 85-94): "The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces HPC theory..." This is boilerplate. It adds nothing a reader can't get from the section headings. Every word spent on roadmaps is a word not spent earning the reader's trust.

- Section 2.2, "HPC vs. prototype theory" (lines 140-166): This subsection exists because the author anticipates a reviewer asking "how is this different from prototype theory?" It is a pre-emptive defence, not an argument. The content is fine, but the placement (before any interjection data) means the reader is asked to evaluate a theory comparison about a category they haven't yet seen described. The comparison would land better *after* the property cluster (Section 3), where the reader has the data to judge whether mechanisms add anything.

- Lines 170-220, field-relative projectibility and subscript notation: This is introduced before the reader has seen a single interjection example. The notation is motivated by examples the reader hasn't encountered yet (attributive *damn*, backchanneling *mm-hm*). It would be more effective introduced in Section 5 where those examples actually appear.

- The "Criterion of success" paragraph (lines 135-138): Important for the author's self-discipline, but performative as a paragraph in the published paper. The reader doesn't need to be told what would count as failure; they need to see the argument succeed.

**Prose that advances the argument:**

- Lines 419-428: The *damn* cluster-breaking example. This is the paper's strongest analytical passage. It shows the coupling of properties by showing what happens when the coupling breaks. The sentence "A list of co-occurring properties can't explain this pattern. A cluster maintained by mechanisms can" (lines 427-428) earns its keep because the preceding paragraph has done the work.

- Lines 449-499: The Gehweiler four-stage trajectory. This is real historical pragmatics. It shows stages, mechanisms, and predictions. It does exactly what the paper promises to do.

- Lines 612-631: The perturbation test. Concrete, testable, and short. *Pop* integrated into a clause gets reclassified; *God* with referential content gets reclassified. The evidence is compact and the logic is clear.

- Lines 795-809: The attributive *damn* analysis. This is where the subscript notation earns its keep. The convergence-divergence pattern across interjection_syn and interjection_sem is genuinely illuminating. The problem is that the reader had to wait 600 lines from when the notation was introduced to see it do real work.

---

## 5. Advice

You have a paper that does two things: it shows historical pragmaticists something genuinely new about interjections (the Gehweiler trajectories as mechanism pathways, the attractor-basin idea, the "both were right" resolution of Ameka-Wilkins), and it imports a philosophical framework that the audience hasn't asked for and can't independently evaluate. The first thing is a gift. The second is a demand. Right now the demand comes first. Sections 2 and 2.3 ask the reader to learn HPC theory, accept subscript notation, and evaluate a comparison with prototype theory before they've seen a single interjection described. Reverse the order. Start with what you know the JHP reader cares about: the Gehweiler trajectory, the corpus evidence, the boundary disputes. Let the data create the need for the framework. When the reader sees that the bleaching pathway installs cluster properties in a constrained order, *then* introduce the perturbation test. When they see that the Ameka-Wilkins dispute is about partial overlap, *then* introduce the field-relative decomposition. The philosophical apparatus should arrive as the answer to a question the reader is already asking, not as a prerequisite they must accept on faith before the argument begins. As for the attacks on Jespersen, Quirk, and H&P: soften the verbs. *Denied* and *conceded* are adversarial. Jespersen analysed, Quirk characterised, H&P prioritised other categories. You are not correcting these scholars; you are showing them something they didn't have the tools to see. That is a much stronger position than catching them in an error.
