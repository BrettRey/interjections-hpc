# CGEL Grammarian Review: Full Draft

**Paper:** English Interjections as a Homeostatic Property Cluster
**Reviewer focus:** Category-function distinction, syntactic precision, CGEL accuracy
**Date:** 2026-03-14

---

## 1. One-Sentence Summary

The paper argues that English interjections constitute a thin-but-genuine homeostatic property cluster sustained by identifiable diachronic and synchronic mechanisms, and that their projectibility is primarily pragmatic rather than truth-conditional -- a claim that is well-structured and mostly syntactically precise, though several points of CGEL analysis need tightening.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The category-vs-function distinction is the paper's best section (5.3).** The argument that "interjection" (category) adds inferential content beyond "supplement" (function) is exactly the right way to deploy the CGEL distinction. The five bullet points -- non-referentiality, emotive force, free coinage, non-inflection, diachronic trajectory -- are genuinely distinct from what supplement function alone provides. This section demonstrates that the author understands the distinction is not merely terminological but does real analytical work. It is also the section most directly relevant to a CGEL audience and should be foregrounded more in the abstract and introduction.

- **CGEL's interjection-phrase analysis is handled well.** The treatment of `damn these mosquitoes!` as IntjP with NP complement (citing H&P 2002: 1361fn) is accurate and appropriately cautious. The paper correctly notes that this is "residue of verb syntax" rather than full verbal behaviour, and uses this to set up the partial-bleaching analysis in Section 4. The distinction between `damn` as IntjP head and `damn` as attributive modifier (the `damn car` case) is correctly tracked across Sections 3.3, 3.6, 5.2, and 5.3 without conflation.

- **The supplement function is correctly characterised throughout.** The paper consistently treats supplement as a syntactic function (not a category), correctly notes that phrases of multiple categories can serve as supplements (relative clauses, NPs, AdjPs), and uses this to motivate the category-vs-function argument. The examples in (\ref{ex:supplements}) are well-chosen and accurate. The paper avoids the common error of treating "supplement" as if it were unique to interjections.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

### 3.1 The Aarts (2014) definition of supplement is imprecise and un-CGEL

Line 266 defines supplements as units that are "not integrated in clause structure" and cites Aarts, Chalker & Weiner (2014: 404). CGEL's own characterisation is more precise. In H&P (2002: 1350--1363), supplements are defined by their prosodic detachment and semantic independence from the anchor clause -- they are loosely attached, set off by comma intonation, and their content is presented as backgrounded or parenthetical. The Aarts dictionary is a useful reference work, but it is not CGEL, and the definition used here ("not integrated in clause structure") is vague enough to cover topics, dislocations, and vocatives, none of which are supplements in CGEL's technical sense. The paper should cite CGEL directly here (H&P 2002: 1350ff) and use its more precise characterisation of supplement function.

**Fix:** Replace the Aarts citation with H&P (2002: 1350) or at minimum supplement it. The definition should mention prosodic detachment and semantic independence from the anchor, not just structural non-integration.

### 3.2 "Interjection phrase" vs "interjection" as category label needs disambiguation

The paper uses "interjection" both as a lexical category label (a word class, parallel to noun/verb/adjective) and as the head of "interjection phrase" (IntjP). CGEL treats interjection as a lexical category and IntjP as the corresponding phrasal category -- but this is already unusual in CGEL's system because interjections are mostly single words (unlike nouns, which routinely project to NPs with rich internal structure). The paper should acknowledge this explicitly. When the paper refers to `oh boy` and `holy cow` (line 294-296) and says their "internal structure is less clear," this is the right instinct, but it deserves more than a parenthetical dismissal. These are among the few cases that test whether IntjP is a genuine phrasal category with internal syntax or merely a notational convenience. For a paper arguing that the category is a real kind (not a filing label), the phrasal-category question is directly relevant.

**Fix:** Add a brief discussion (2-3 sentences) of IntjP as a phrasal category. Does it have specifier, complement, and modifier positions like other phrasal categories? Or is it categorially simple (mostly single-word or frozen collocations)? The answer -- that IntjP is phrasal only in marginal cases -- actually strengthens the "thin HPC" argument: the category is real but syntactically thin.

### 3.3 The `damn` analysis conflates CGEL categories across constructions

The paper tracks `damn` across three constructions:

1. Standalone interjection: `Damn!` (IntjP, head = interjection)
2. Interjection with complement: `Damn these mosquitoes!` (IntjP, head = interjection, complement = NP)
3. Attributive modifier: `the damn car` (modifier within NP)

Construction (3) is where the analysis slips. In line 400-402, the paper says `damn` in `the damn car` "retains emotive force but loses prosodic isolation and syntactic non-integration -- it's embedded inside an NP." This is correct descriptively. But in Section 5.2 (lines 733-740), the paper says attributive `damn` is "interjection_sem but not interjection_syn." This requires a category assignment for `damn` in `the damn car`, and the paper tacitly treats it as an interjection that happens to be syntactically integrated. But CGEL would not analyse attributive `damn` as an interjection at all -- it would analyse it as an adjective (specifically, a secondary interjection being used as an adjective in attributive modifier function). The category label applies to the word as used in a particular construction, not to the lexeme across all its uses.

This matters because the subscript architecture depends on the same lexeme belonging to different HPCs. If `damn` in `the damn car` is an adjective (not an interjection) in CGEL's terms, then the interjection_sem HPC doesn't contain an interjection -- it contains an expressive, which may be an adjective, an interjection, or something else. This is fine for the paper's argument (it actually supports the field-relative decomposition), but the CGEL analysis needs to be stated correctly.

**Fix:** State explicitly that CGEL would assign `damn` in `the damn car` to the adjective category (in attributive modifier function), not to the interjection category. Then note that this is exactly what the subscript architecture predicts: interjection_sem cross-cuts CGEL's category assignments, because the semantic cluster (use-conditional meaning) is maintained by bleaching mechanisms that are indifferent to the syntactic category of the output.

## 4. Key Question

**How does the paper handle the fact that CGEL itself is ambivalent about interjection as a lexical category?**

CGEL (H&P 2002: 74) lists interjection among its lexical categories but notes that it is "a relatively minor one" and that membership is "hard to determine" for many items. The Student's Introduction (H&P 2005: 16) goes further and omits interjections from the category list entirely, saying "there really isn't anything interesting for a grammar to say about them." The paper cites this omission as part of the "declining attention" narrative (lines 36-38), but it doesn't engage with the possibility that CGEL's ambivalence is itself evidence for the thin-HPC analysis. CGEL recognizes interjection as a category in the comprehensive grammar but drops it in the pedagogical grammar -- this is exactly what you'd expect if the category is real but syntactically thin. The comprehensive grammar includes it because the distributional evidence (supplement function, non-inflection, IntjP projection) is genuine; the pedagogical grammar drops it because the inferential payoff is low for students learning clause structure.

This is an opportunity, not a problem. The paper should use CGEL's own ambivalence as further evidence for the thin-HPC characterization.

## 5. Additional Notes on CGEL Accuracy

### Page/section references

- **Line 32:** `\textcite[90]{jespersen1924}` -- Jespersen (1924: 90) does discuss interjections, but the claim that he "denied that interjections are a lexical category at all" requires verification. Jespersen's actual position is more nuanced: he argues that interjections are "not a part of speech in the ordinary sense" (p. 90) but doesn't deny they exist as a class. The paper should be precise about what Jespersen denied.

- **Line 34-35:** `\textcite[67]{quirk1985}` -- the "marginal and anomalous" quote. I cannot verify the exact page from training data, but the phrase is characteristically Quirk et al. and appears in the discussion of word classes. This should be source-verified.

- **Line 273:** `\citep[1356]{HuddlestonPullum2002}` for supplements including relative clauses, NPs, AdjPs. H&P's supplement discussion begins at Chapter 15, Section 3 (pp. 1350ff). The page number 1356 is plausible for the examples given, but should be verified against the physical text.

- **Line 287-289:** `\textcite[1361fn]{HuddlestonPullum2002}` for the IntjP analysis of `damn these mosquitoes!`. The page is plausible (the interjection discussion is brief and near the end of Chapter 18). This needs source verification.

### Terminology consistency

The paper is mostly consistent, but there is one slip. Line 272 refers to "adjective phrases" using the abbreviated form "AdjPs." CGEL's notation is AdjP, which the paper uses. However, the paper also uses "NPs" (line 272) and "IntjP" (line 289). The notation should be consistent: either use the pluralised abbreviation throughout (NPs, AdjPs, IntjPs) or use the full forms throughout.

### Category vs. function in the property survey

The paper generally maintains the distinction well. One potential slip: line 260 says "adverbs also don't inflect" -- this is correct as a statement about the adverb category (adverbs as a word class don't inflect in English), but it would be more precise to note that this is a property of the category (adverb), not of the function (adjunct/modifier). Since the paper is comparing categories (interjection vs. adverb), the statement is fine, but it could be made clearer by saying "words of the adverb category also don't inflect."

---

## 6. Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit.**

The paper is structurally sound and makes a genuine contribution. The category-vs-function argument (Section 5.3) is the strongest part and works well in context. The weaknesses are all fixable: cite CGEL directly for supplements, disambiguate the phrasal-category question, and correct the analysis of attributive `damn`. The Jespersen characterization needs source verification. None of these issues require restructuring; they require precision.

The paper would also benefit from using CGEL's own ambivalence about interjection status as evidence for the thin-HPC analysis (see Key Question above) -- this is a missed opportunity that would strengthen both the empirical argument and the engagement with the CGEL tradition.

Overall, the syntactic analysis is careful and the category-function distinction is maintained throughout with only the attributive-`damn` slip noted above. For a paper arguing that interjections are a genuine (if thin) category, the CGEL apparatus is deployed appropriately and effectively.
